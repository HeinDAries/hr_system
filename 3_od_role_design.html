OD Tool 3 / 9 — Role Design & Job Architecture

Role Design
& Architecture

A well-designed role is the atom of a well-designed organisation. Get the role right and the structure follows. Get it wrong and no amount of structural redesign will fix the dysfunction that results.

"Most organisational problems that look like people problems are actually role problems. The wrong work is assigned to the wrong grade — and everyone wonders why performance is inconsistent."

Elliott Jaques — Requisite Organisation
What Role Design Actually Is
Role design is the deliberate process of defining what a role exists to do, what authority it carries, what outputs it is accountable for, and what capability it requires. It is upstream of job descriptions, grade assignments, and performance frameworks — all of which should flow from a well-designed role, not precede it.
Purpose. Why does this role exist? What would not happen — or would happen badly — if this role were vacant? If the answer is unclear, the role has not been designed; it has been inherited.
Accountability. What specific outputs is the role holder responsible for delivering? Accountability is not a list of tasks — it is a defined set of outcomes the role holder owns, regardless of how they achieve them.
Authority. What decisions can the role holder make without escalation? What requires approval? Where the authority boundary is unclear, decisions either stall or get made by the wrong person.
Capability. What knowledge, skill, and judgement does the role require to deliver its accountabilities? This determines both how the role is graded and how people are selected for it.
Role Design vs Job Description
These are not the same thing. Most organisations produce job descriptions without ever doing role design. The difference matters.
Job Description
— A list of tasks and responsibilities
— Describes what the person does day-to-day
— Often written to justify existing pay
— Quickly becomes outdated
— Silent on authority and decision rights
— HR administrative document
Role Profile (Well-Designed)
— Defines purpose, accountability, authority
— Describes what the role exists to deliver
— Anchored to organisational need
— Stable through strategy cycles
— Explicit on decision rights
— Strategic people management tool
At BaKuu Holding: Most current role documentation is task-list job descriptions. The OD agenda requires converting the G4+ critical roles to proper role profiles — starting with Site Manager and Group Ops Lead.

Section 02 — Job Architecture Principles

Job Architecture
Principles

Job architecture is the framework that organises all roles in the organisation into a coherent, comparable structure — so that grading, pay, career paths, and succession planning all operate from the same foundation.

Why Job Architecture Matters at BaKuu HoldingBaKuu Holding operates across four countries with different labour markets, cultural norms, and regulatory contexts. Without a common job architecture, the G3 driller in Laos and the G3 driller in Indonesia are notionally the same grade but may have vastly different accountabilities, pay, and career expectations. A shared architecture creates the common language that enables cross-country equity, mobility, and succession planning.
The Four Pillars of Job Architecture
1. Grade Structure
A defined set of levels (grades) that reflect increasing complexity, scope, and accountability. Each grade has a clear definition of the work complexity it represents — not just a pay band. At BaKuu Holding: G1–G8.
2. Job Families
Groups of roles that perform related work and follow similar career progressions. At BaKuu Holding: Operations/Drilling, Site Leadership, Group Functions (HR, Finance, HSE), Corporate Leadership. Each family has its own progression pathway within the grade structure.
3. Career Pathways
The defined progression routes through the grade structure — vertical (promotion) and lateral (role rotation). A well-designed architecture makes progression legible to every employee. At BaKuu Holding, the G1→G4 driller pathway is the most critical and currently the least well-defined.
4. Evaluation Criteria
The factors used to assign roles to grades — consistently across job families and countries. At BaKuu Holding, the C&B Job Evaluation tool (C&B Tool 2) uses: Knowledge & Skill, Problem Solving, Accountability, and Working Conditions. These four factors anchor the architecture.
The BaKuu Holding Job Architecture — Design Principles
PrincipleWhat it means in practiceThe BaKuu Holding application
Grade = complexity, not tenureA role is graded by the complexity of work it requires, not by how long someone has been doing it. Tenure rewards belong in the pay range, not in the grade.A G3 driller who has been at G3 for 8 years is not automatically G4. Section 04 — Anatomy of a Role Profile

A role profile is the definitive reference document for a role. It defines what the role exists to do, what it is accountable for, what authority it carries, and what capability it requires. Every element serves a specific purpose — and leaving any out creates a gap that performance management, recruitment, and pay equity will have to navigate around.

BaKuu Holding Group — Role Profile
Senior Driller
G4 · Operations & Drilling Family A · All Countries
Role Purpose
The Senior Driller delivers the highest level of technical drilling performance on assigned programme sections while simultaneously developing the next generation of drillers. This role is the fulcrum of the BaKuu Holding pipeline — it is where technical excellence and people development meet in a single accountability.
Key Accountabilities
Programme section delivery: Accountable for technical quality, daily metreage targets, and safety compliance on assigned drilling sections. Reports progress to Site Manager daily.
Trainee development: Formally accountable for the development outcomes of assigned G1–G2 trainees. Conducts structured development conversations monthly and provides milestone assessments. Target: trainee-to-G2 conversion rate ≥70%.
Technical authority: First point of escalation for technical decisions on site. Makes independent judgement calls on drilling method within programme parameters.
Safety leadership: Models safety behaviour and holds all G1–G3 colleagues accountable for safety standards on section. Authority to stop work when safety is at risk.
Critical Note — Role Architecture
The trainee development accountability is not optional — it is a core accountability of this grade. A G4 driller who delivers excellent technical results but does not develop their trainees is not performing their role. Performance management at G4 must assess both dimensions equally. This is the design correction that connects the reward framework (C&B) to the pipeline strategy (TA).
BaKuu Holding Group — New Role Profile · DRAFT
Group Operations Lead
G6 · Group Functions NEW ROLE · 2026
Role Purpose
The Group Operations Lead ensures that BaKuu Holding's drilling programme standards, operational practices, and technical methodology are consistent, improving, and shared across all country sites — enabling the COO to hold a manageable span of control as the group expands to five countries.
Key Accountabilities
Group operational standards: Owns and maintains the group operational standards framework (drilling methodology, safety protocols, programme reporting format). Accountable for all sites operating to standard — not for how sites achieve their programme delivery.
Best practice transfer: Identifies high-performing practices at individual sites and facilitates their adoption across the group. Chairs the quarterly SM peer forum. Accountable for measurable improvement in the lagging sites' performance.
COO advisory: Provides the COO with a consolidated operational picture across all countries — one source of truth for programme metrics, risk flags, and operational improvement initiatives.
Country 5 operational integration: Leads the operational onboarding of Country 5 — standards adoption, SM orientation, and programme reporting integration.
Authority Boundary — Critical Design Decision
Group Ops Lead HAS authority to: Set and enforce group standards. Require reporting in the group format. Escalate standards non-compliance to COO. Chair the SM forum and set its agenda.
Group Ops Lead does NOT have authority to: Direct SMs on how to deliver their programme. Override SM operational decisions. Manage SM performance. Hire or fire country staff.
Design rationale: This boundary is the difference between a coordination role and a management layer. If the authority boundary is not explicit, the Indonesia SM will perceive the Group Ops Lead as a threat to his autonomy — and resistance will follow. The boundary must be defined in the role profile, communicated directly to all SMs by the COO, and respected consistently.
Section 05 — Common Role Design Failures

Common
Failures

Most role design problems are not random — they follow recognisable patterns. Knowing these patterns is the HRBP's early warning system. If you can name the failure mode, you can prescribe the fix before it becomes a performance problem, a grievance, or a resignation.

Role Inflation
What it looks like
Responsibilities accumulate in a role over time without a corresponding grade review. The role holder ends up doing G5 work at a G4 grade and pay.
Consequences
The role holder feels undervalued and becomes a flight risk. If they leave, the replacement is hired at the higher grade — revealing the inflation that was always there. If they stay, compression builds as others are hired above or beside them at higher grades for the same complexity of work.
BaKuu Holding signal
G3 drillers informally performing supervisory work without G4 authority or pay. The informal supervision expectation has grown beyond what the G3 role was designed to carry.
The Fix
Annual role profile review as part of the performance cycle. Any role that has gained new significant accountabilities in the previous year triggers an evaluation review — not an automatic grade change, but a deliberate assessment.
The Ghost Accountability
What it looks like
An important outcome exists — everyone knows it needs to happen — but it is not formally assigned to any role. It lives in the gap between roles.
Consequences
The work either does not happen, happens inconsistently, or happens well below the quality it would receive if someone owned it formally. When it goes wrong, blame diffuses across several roles — no one is accountable, so no one improves.
BaKuu Holding signal
Trainee development at month 3 — no one formally owns the attrition problem. The G4 driller has an informal mentoring role but no formal accountability for trainee outcomes. The SM sees it as a G4 responsibility. The G4 sees it as an informal add-on. The trainee has no structured support.
The Fix
Assign the accountability explicitly to a role — in the role profile, not in a side conversation. At BaKuu Holding: formally add trainee development accountability to the G4 role profile, with a measurable outcome (conversion rate ≥70%) and include it in performance review.
Dual Accountability
What it looks like
Two roles share accountability for the same outcome — often without realising it. Both partially own it, so neither fully owns it.
Consequences
The work happens twice (waste) or not at all (gap) depending on which role holder steps back first. Under stress, both roles assume the other is covering the critical task. This is the structural root cause of many "communication failures" that are actually accountability design failures.
BaKuu Holding signal
Group HR and Site Manager both claim accountability for country HR compliance. The SM says "HR should handle that." HR says "the SM is the country lead." In the gap, a compliance obligation is missed.
The Fix
The RACI model (covered in Tool 9 — Decision Rights & Governance). For each significant accountability, one role is Responsible (does the work) and one is Accountable (owns the outcome). These can be the same role — but only one role is Accountable.
The Vanity Grade
What it looks like
A role is over-graded relative to its actual complexity — usually to retain someone, reward loyalty, or make a title sound more impressive in a recruitment process.
Consequences
Pay compression for roles at the correct grade. The architecture loses its integrity — grades no longer mean what they say. Peers in comparable roles feel devalued. Future grading decisions become political rather than analytical.
Prevention
Use the job evaluation methodology (C&B Tool 2) consistently. When retention requires paying above band, use off-scale pay with a plan to address — not a grade change. Grade integrity is more valuable long-term than the short-term retention solution.
The Fix
Re-evaluate the role against the grade criteria without reference to the current role holder. If the grade should change, it changes — and pay is adjusted accordingly. The conversation with the role holder is a leadership challenge, not an architecture one.

Section 08 — Keeping Roles Current

Keeping
Roles Current

A role profile written once and never reviewed is worse than no profile — it creates false certainty about a role that has since changed. Role profiles decay. The HRBP's discipline is building the review cadence that keeps them current.

"The moment you finish designing a role, it starts to drift. Strategy changes. Work accumulates. People improvise. The discipline of role maintenance is not about documentation — it is about keeping accountability where it belongs."

OD Practice — Role Maintenance Principle
When Roles Must Be Reviewed
Triggered review — Strategy change. When BaKuu Holding enters Country 5, the Site Manager profile must be reviewed — the role in a new country may have different client relationship or regulatory requirements. Review within 30 days of any structural change.
Triggered review — Structural change. When the Group Ops Lead role is created, the Site Manager role must also be reviewed — because some former SM accountability may now be shared with or transferred to the new role. Never create a new role without reviewing adjacent roles.
Triggered review — Performance gap. When a role consistently underperforms, check the role profile before assuming a capability problem. If the role has expanded beyond its defined scope, the gap may be a design problem — not a people problem.
Annual review — All G4+ roles. As part of the performance cycle (Q4 each year), all G4 and above roles are reviewed against their current profile. The line manager confirms the profile is still accurate — or flags for HRBP review. This takes 15 minutes per role and prevents silent drift.
The HRBP's Role Maintenance Checklist
Complete G4 Senior Driller profile with trainee development accountability — Q2 2026
Complete G5 Site Manager profile with authority definition and succession specification — Q2 2026
Draft G6 Group Ops Lead profile ahead of recruitment — Q3 2026
Establish annual review cadence for all G4+ roles linked to Q4 performance cycle — Q4 2026
Review all Site Manager profiles when Group Ops Lead role is activated — confirm SM authority boundaries still accurate — Q4 2026
Update G3 Driller profile with explicit G3→G4 development criteria so Laos succession plans have a clear target — Q3 2026
Role Design as a Leadership Conversation
Role profiles are not HR documents — they are the foundation of the HRBP's most important leadership conversations. Each of the following conversations at BaKuu Holding in 2026 depends on having a current, well-designed role profile to anchor it.
ConversationWhy the role profile is essentialRisk without it
Myo H. retention conversation (deadline Sep 2026)The Site Manager profile defines what Myo H. is being retained to do — and what the COO expects of the role. Without it, the retention conversation is about salary only, not about role clarity, career development, or succession.Retention achieved at a cost but without addressing the underlying performance or succession question. Myo H. stays but the succession gap remains.
Indonesia SM authority disputeThe Site Manager and Group Ops Lead profiles, with explicit authority boundaries, give the HRBP a factual reference for the COO conversation about what the Indonesia SM is and is not entitled to decide independently.The authority dispute continues to be managed politically rather than architecturally — each incident is a fresh negotiation.
Laos G3→G4 development plans (activate Jun 2026)The Senior Driller profile defines exactly what the G3 candidates need to demonstrate to be assessed as G4-ready. Development plans can be specific, measurable, and consistent across candidates.Development plans are vague ("show more leadership") and subjective. Progression decisions are made on gut feel and country-specific norms rather than a common standard.
Group Ops Lead recruitment (Q3 2026)The role profile is the recruitment brief — it defines the capability and experience the right candidate must have. Without it, recruitment starts from "who could do this?" rather than "what does this role need?"Hire is made based on availability and personal chemistry rather than role requirements. The first person to define the role in practice is the hire — not the HRBP or the COO.
