HRBP Tool 9 — Strategic Talent Strategy Builder

Talent
Strategy

A talent strategy is the organisation's deliberate plan for ensuring it always has the people it needs to execute its business strategy — now and in the future. It is the document that earns the HR function a seat in the boardroom.

"Strategy without people is a plan. People without strategy is activity. A talent strategy is the bridge that turns both into results."

HRBP Principle — Valentis Group
What a Talent Strategy Is — and Is Not
Most HR departments have activity plans. Very few have talent strategies. The difference is not sophistication — it is the starting point.
Activity Plan
— Starts with HR activities
— Driven by HR calendar
— Answers "what will HR do?"
— Measured by completion
— Presented to HR team
— Updated annually or never
Talent Strategy
— Starts with business goals
— Driven by business priorities
— Answers "what does the business need from its people?"
— Measured by business outcomes
— Presented to CEO and COO
— Updated with the business plan
Why Valentis Needs One Now
Valentis is at an inflection point. The business is growing — from 4 countries to potentially 5, from 42 to 65+ FTE — in a constrained talent market for a specialist role that takes 18–24 months to develop. This is not a situation you can manage reactively.

A documented talent strategy does three things for Valentis that nothing else can:
Business credibility: The CEO and COO see that HR understands the business well enough to connect people decisions to financial outcomes.
Investment justification: Every HR investment — development programmes, retention bonuses, compensation reviews — can be traced to a strategic outcome, not just a HR activity.
HRBP positioning: The HRBP who presents a talent strategy is no longer a service provider. They are a strategic advisor. This is the document that accelerates the path from Group HR Manager to CHRO.

Section 02 — Business–Talent Linkage

Business–Talent
Linkage

The most important section of any talent strategy. Every talent priority must trace directly to a business priority. If the linkage cannot be stated clearly, the priority should not be in the strategy.

The Linkage TestFor every talent priority, complete this sentence: "We must do [talent action] because the business needs [specific outcome] — and without this, [specific consequence] will happen." If you cannot complete it with specificity, the priority is an HR assumption, not a business requirement.

Section 03 — The Five Talent Pillars

The Five
Talent Pillars

Valentis's talent strategy is organised around five pillars — each addressing a critical dimension of the talent challenge. Together, they form a complete and integrated talent system.

01
Build the Pipeline — Driller Supply Chain
Talent Acquisition + L&D
+
The Problem
Valentis cannot hire G4 drillers off the shelf in Southeast Asia. The only reliable path to G4 supply is a functional trainee pipeline. The current pipeline is producing at 58% conversion — below the 70% required to sustain growth. At current attrition, the G4 supply gap reaches −5 FTE by end of 2026.
Strategic Response
Expand intake to 8 trainees per cohort. Fix the Myanmar supervisor communication gap to recover conversion to ≥70%. Activate formal G3→G4 development plans for 4 identified candidates. Run contract G4 bridge hires (×2, 12 months) while the internal pipeline catches up.
Success Measures
Trainee conversion ≥70% by Q3 2026. Four G3→G4 candidates on structured development plans by June 2026. G4 supply gap ≤ −2 FTE by December 2026. Pipeline producing one G4-ready candidate per quarter by 2028.
02
Retain Critical Talent — The Irreplaceables
C&B + Engagement
+
The Problem
Valentis has a small number of individuals whose departure would cause immediate operational disruption: Myo H. (Myanmar SM, retention bonus expiring Apr 2027), Wirawan S. (Indonesia, retention bonus expiring Q1 2027), and the Laos senior driller cohort (5 G4, average age 51, 3 at retirement risk within 3 years). All three risk events are approaching simultaneously.
Strategic Response
Initiate Myo H. retention conversation Q3 2026 — nine months before expiry. Review Wirawan S. package by October 2026. For the Laos G4 cohort: structured knowledge transfer and mentoring of G3 successors, combined with a recognition and flexible arrangement to extend working tenure where appropriate.
Success Measures
Myo H. retention secured (or succession plan activated) by Q4 2026. Wirawan S. retention decision confirmed by Q3 2026. Laos G4 cohort: at least 2 of 3 at-risk drillers extend tenure by 12+ months through flexible arrangements. Zero unplanned critical talent exits in 2026.
03
Develop Leaders — The Next Generation
L&D + Succession
+
The Problem
Valentis's leadership bench is thin. All four country site managers are the only person who can do their job — there is no ready successor for any of them. As the business grows to 5+ countries, this becomes an acute risk. The business cannot expand faster than its leadership pipeline can grow.
Strategic Response
Identify one high-potential G4 leader at each site. Build individual development plans targeting Site Manager capability within 24 months. Create structured cross-country exposure — the future SM of Country 5 needs to see how all four existing countries operate. Identify the Country 5 SM candidate by Q3 2026.
Success Measures
One identified successor for each SM role by Q4 2026. Country 5 SM candidate on development plan by Q3 2026. Leadership readiness score: ≥1 ready candidate per critical role by end of 2027. Internal fill rate for SM roles ≥60% by 2028.
04
Reward Competitively — Total Rewards Positioning
C&B + Benefits
+
The Problem
Valentis must compete for rare G4 drillers against better-known operators with deeper pockets. Pay competitiveness must be verified before every new hire and every retention risk — not after the first offer decline. One known risk: Zaw M. (Myanmar, G3) is below minimum compa-ratio — a retention vulnerability.
Strategic Response
Annual market salary benchmarking for G3 and G4 roles across all four countries. Remediate Zaw M. below-minimum position by April 2026. Country 5 market pricing before first hire. Formalise total rewards proposition — not just salary but site allowances, development, and career path — as a retention and attraction tool.
Success Measures
All G3/G4 roles at 90–110% compa-ratio by mid-2026. Zero below-minimum positions by Q2 2026. Offer acceptance rate ≥80% across all new hires. People cost maintained at 40–45% of revenue through efficient compensation design.
05
Build the HR Function — From Service to Strategy
HRBP Capability + Systems
+
The Problem
Valentis's HR function is currently reactive — filling vacancies, processing payroll, managing compliance. As the business grows to 65+ FTE across 5 countries, the current HR model will not scale. The function must evolve from transactional to strategic while continuing to deliver the basics reliably.
Strategic Response
Establish the monthly HR scorecard and quarterly business review as permanent fixtures by Q2 2026. Complete HRBP certification (AIHR) by year-end. Build the workforce planning model so that the business plan and the people plan are produced simultaneously, not sequentially. Prepare the case for a second HR hire as headcount crosses 50 FTE.
Success Measures
HRBP included in business planning cycle by Q4 2026. Monthly scorecard delivered without fail for 6 consecutive months. CEO references people data in board reporting. HRBP AIHR certification completed. Business case for HR team expansion presented to COO by Q3 2026.
Section 04 — Talent SWOT

Talent SWOT
Valentis

The talent SWOT is the evidence base for the strategy. It defines where Valentis stands in the talent market — honestly — and where the greatest opportunity lies. Strategy without honest self-assessment is wishful thinking.

Strengths — What We Have
Established driller development programme. A functional trainee-to-G4 pipeline exists — one of very few in the region. At 70% conversion it is a competitive moat.
Multi-country operational experience. Staff with cross-country exposure are rare and highly sought after. Valentis creates this experience by default.
Strong site culture in Thailand. Thailand site demonstrates what Valentis can be at its best — high retention, high conversion, high satisfaction. A replicable model.
Relatively low people cost. At 43% of revenue, there is room within the 45% ceiling to invest in retention and development before financial pressure mounts.
Weaknesses — What We Lack
No succession cover for any SM role. A single leadership departure in any country creates an immediate programme risk with no internal bridge.
Myanmar supervisor capability gap. Trainee attrition is directly linked to supervisor communication — a capability gap that has been allowed to persist without diagnosis or intervention.
HR function is reactive. No workforce planning, no regular business review, no structured talent pipeline management. The function is event-driven, not strategy-driven.
Ageing G4 cohort in Laos. Three of five senior drillers approaching retirement. Replacement pipeline is net negative if no action taken.
Opportunities — What We Could Capture
Country 5 expansion. A new country entry creates the opportunity to build the HR model correctly from day one — structured onboarding, competitive rewards benchmarked before the first hire, clear career path.
Thailand as a talent development hub. The highest-performing site can become the reference model and the cross-site rotation destination for high-potential talent from other countries.
Formalising the EVP. Valentis has an informal employer value proposition — interesting work, international exposure, development programme — that has never been formalised or used in attraction. Doing so would improve offer acceptance rate and reduce sourcing cost.
HRBP strategic positioning. As the first person to build a structured talent strategy for Valentis, the Group HR Manager has an open field to define what strategic HR looks like in this organisation.
Threats — What Could Undermine Us
Constrained driller supply market. Experienced G4 drillers in Southeast Asia are rare and increasingly competed for. Any major operator entering the region will compress the external hiring market.
Political and regulatory risk in operating countries. Sudden changes to work permit regimes (Myanmar, Indonesia) can strand workforce plans that depend on cross-border mobility.
Simultaneous retention risk events. Myo H., Wirawan S., and the Laos G4 cohort all create risk within the same 18-month window — a simultaneous cluster that would overwhelm a reactive HR function.
Contract loss or revenue contraction. A lost contract forces rapid workforce adjustment — and organisations with no talent strategy lose the wrong people when they restructure.

Section 08 — The CEO Pitch

The CEO
Pitch

The talent strategy is only as powerful as the conversation it creates. This section prepares the HRBP for the most important pitch of the year — the annual people strategy presentation to the CEO and COO.

This Is the CHRO ConversationThe HRBP who presents a talent strategy to the CEO is no longer a HR manager. They are a business strategist who specialises in people. This distinction is felt in the room before the first slide is shown — in how you open, how you speak, and what you are willing to commit to.
The Opening — Five Sentences
The first five sentences of the CEO pitch determine whether the next 30 minutes are a strategic conversation or a presentation the CEO politely endures. Practice these until they are effortless.
Sentence 1 — The Business Frame
"Valentis is growing faster than its talent pipeline — and by the end of this year, that gap will start to constrain what the business can deliver."
Sentence 2 — The Evidence
"Without action in the next 90 days, we project a 34% headcount shortfall against programme requirement by December — a position we cannot recover from in the hiring market."
Sentence 3 — The Strategy
"The strategy I am presenting today addresses this in five integrated moves — building the pipeline, retaining the irreplaceables, developing future leaders, fixing our rewards competitiveness, and upgrading the HR function itself."
Sentence 4 — The Investment
"The total investment is approximately $130k across six initiatives — against a cost-of-inaction estimate that runs significantly higher in lost programme revenue and replacement costs."
Sentence 5 — The Ask
"I need two decisions from this conversation — and I will take ownership of the rest."
Questions the CEO Will Ask
"How does this compare to what other operators are doing?"
Response: "Our trainee programme is a competitive differentiator — most regional operators recruit externally and pay the premium. At 70% conversion, our internal cost-per-G4 is approximately 40% below external hire cost. The strategy invests in making that advantage more reliable."
"What happens if we do nothing for another 6 months?"
Response: "In 6 months, the Myo H. retention window narrows to 3 months — too short to negotiate effectively. The Laos G4 succession gap becomes acute. And the G3→G4 development candidates we could have started in June will be 6 months behind schedule — pushing their G4 readiness from Q4 2027 to Q2 2028. The cost of delay compounds."
"What is the single most important thing you need from me?"
Response: "To be included in the 2027 business planning process before the plan is set — not after. Every people constraint I am managing today is the consequence of a business decision made without HR input. With a seat at that table, I can prevent the next generation of these problems rather than solve them in retrospect."
Closing the Pitch — The Commitment Statement
Every talent strategy pitch must close with a personal commitment. Not "HR will deliver the following initiatives." But "I will personally ensure the following outcomes — and you will see them in the monthly scorecard."
Sample Closing Statement
"I am committing to three outcomes by the end of 2026: trainee conversion back above 70%, a named successor for every Site Manager role, and Valentis included in the business planning cycle for 2027. You will see progress on all three in every monthly scorecard. If any of them slip, I will tell you why in the review — and what changes. That is the kind of HR function I am building here."
— Group HR Manager, Valentis Group
