C&B Tool 4 — Merit and Increment Matrix

Merit &
Increment Matrix

The decision engine that translates PM ratings and compa-ratio positions into specific salary increase recommendations. Connects the performance review cycle (PM Tool 2) to the compensation structure (C&B Tool 3).

How the Matrix Works
Two inputs. One output.

Input 1 — Performance Rating (1–5)
From the PM system. Ratings drive the base percentage range. A Rating 5 employee receives a larger increase than a Rating 3 employee — even if both are at the same point in the band.

Input 2 — Compa-Ratio Zone
From Tool 3. An employee at the low end of their band receives a higher increase than an employee at the top — even at the same rating. This is the self-correcting mechanism that moves employees toward market over time.

Together they produce a recommended increase percentage. Managers cannot override this without a written exception approved by HR and COO.
Why This Approach
Removes Bias
Without a matrix, salary increases are set by manager confidence and negotiation. Vocal employees get more. Quiet high performers get less. The matrix makes the same factors apply to everyone.
Controls Budget
A matrix makes the total merit cost predictable before the cycle starts. HR can model the budget at any distribution assumption and present the cost to leadership for approval.
Separates Rating from Pay
Rating conversations stay clean. Managers discuss performance — not money. Salary discussions happen separately, anchored to the matrix. This is the key principle from the Total Rewards Framework.
The Valentis Merit Budget — 2026
The total merit pool is set at the start of the cycle by leadership. It constrains the total cost of all increases. The matrix distributes it based on performance and position.

How to Read the Matrix
Find the employee's performance rating in the left column. Find their compa-ratio zone across the top. The cell shows the recommended increase range as a percentage of current base salary.

The range (e.g. 6–8%) gives the manager discretion within the cell. Use the lower end for employees who are solid performers at that rating. Use the upper end for those at the top of the rating band who are close to a promotion or have taken on scope beyond their grade.
Matrix Design Principles
Employees below market (low compa) always receive higher increases than employees above market — regardless of rating
Rating 1 and 2 employees receive 0% regardless of band position — performance must be addressed, not compensated
Red-circle employees (above max) receive 0% base increase regardless of rating — performance is recognised via bonus only
Increases above the matrix ceiling require written justification and COO approval

Section 05 — Review Cycle Guide

Review Cycle
Guide

The end-to-end process for the annual merit cycle — from the performance calibration session through to salary letters being signed and filed.

2026 Merit Cycle Timeline
Nov Wk 1–2
Self-Assessments and Manager Reviews Completed
All employees submit self-assessments using PM Tool 2. Managers complete their assessments. Deadline is firm — no extensions without HR approval.
Nov Wk 3
Calibration Session — COO and HR
HR presents draft rating distribution to COO. Outliers are discussed — any Rating 5 must be justified with specific examples. Any Rating 1 or 2 must have a documented performance history. Ratings are finalised.
Nov Wk 4
Matrix Applied — Merit Increases Calculated
HR uses the matrix to calculate recommended increases for all eligible employees. Total cost is checked against the 4.5% pool. Any exceptions are flagged and written justifications are prepared.
Dec Wk 1
CEO Approval — Total Merit Cost
HR presents the full merit cost to CEO for final approval. All above-matrix exceptions require individual CEO approval at this stage. Budget must not be exceeded without explicit CEO sign-off.
Dec Wk 2
Merit Conversations — Managers Deliver
Managers hold merit conversations with each employee. They communicate the rating and the increase. They do not share what others received. Scripts are in Section 07. HR coaches managers before this week.
Dec Wk 3
Salary Letters Signed and Filed
Merit letters (Section 08) signed by employee and HR. New salary effective 1 January. Letters filed in HR system. Payroll updated by HR — not by managers.
Section 06 — Exceptions and Off-Matrix

Exceptions
and Off-Matrix

Situations where the standard matrix does not apply, or where a deviation is being requested. Every exception requires written documentation and the appropriate level of approval.

Valid Exception Grounds
Market Adjustment
External market has moved significantly for a specific role (typically 10%+ gap to P50). Requires market data evidence. Approved by COO.
Retention Risk
Employee has a credible competing offer or is demonstrably being targeted. Requires documentary evidence. CEO approval required for Retention Bonus designation.
Equity Correction
Internal equity analysis (Tool 3) has identified an unjustifiable pay gap for a long-tenured, high-performing employee. COO approval required.
Promotion Adjustment
Employee is simultaneously being promoted to a higher grade. The merit increase and promotion increase are calculated separately and documented as distinct actions.
Not Valid Exception Grounds
Manager Relationship
A manager wants to reward an employee they value personally. The matrix applies equally to all. Favouritism via exception is the primary risk a merit matrix is designed to eliminate.
Verbal Commitment
A manager promised a salary increase verbally. This is a management error and is handled separately — the exception is not approved simply because a promise was made. The manager is coached.
Perceived Unfairness
An employee believes their increase is too low. Unless they have evidence of a factual error in their rating or compa-ratio calculation, this is not an exception grounds. The matrix is the answer.
Budget Pressure
Requesting a below-matrix increase to save budget. Employees must receive at least the minimum matrix recommendation for their rating. Below-matrix is only permitted for Rating 1 and 2 (zero increase).

Section 07 — Communication Scripts

Communication
Scripts

Ready-to-use scripts for managers delivering merit outcomes. Different scenarios require different approaches. The goal is always: employee feels heard, decision is clear, and the relationship is preserved.

Script 1 — Strong Performer, Good Increase (Rating 4–5)
"I want to talk with you about your performance this year and what that means for your salary. Your rating this year is a [4/5] — [specific achievement example]. That puts you in the Strong/Exceptional category, and I want that to be recognised in how we compensate you.

Based on your rating and where your salary sits in the band, the company is giving you a [X]% increase, effective 1 January. Your new salary will be [amount]. You will receive a letter confirming this for your records.

I want to be clear that this reflects genuine recognition of your contribution. Is there anything you want to ask me about this?"
Note: Manager should know the exact percentage and new salary before the meeting. Do not say you will check and come back.
Script 2 — Meets Expectations (Rating 3)
"I want to go through your performance review outcome and your salary for next year. Your rating this year is a 3 — Meets Expectations. I want to be direct with you about what that means: a 3 is a good rating. It means you are doing your job well, you are reliable, and the team depends on you. It is not a criticism.

Your salary increase for this year is [X]%. That is the market-aligned increase for your rating and where you sit in the salary band. Your new salary from January will be [amount].

If you want to talk about what a path to a higher rating looks like next year, I am very happy to have that conversation now or separately."
Note: Managers often under-deliver Rating 3 news because they feel awkward. The script positions 3 as a good outcome — which it is. Coach managers on this framing in advance.
Script 3 — Low Performer (Rating 1–2), Zero Increase
"I need to be honest with you about your performance review outcome, because it directly affects your salary. Your rating this year is a [1/2]. That means there are areas where your performance has not met the expectations of the role, and we have discussed [specific issues] during the year.

As a result, there is no salary increase this year. I know that is difficult to hear, and I want to be clear: this is not permanent. If your performance improves significantly in the next review period, the conversation will be very different.

I want to talk about exactly what needs to change, and how I can support you in getting there. Can we do that now?"
Note: Do not soften to the point of confusion. The employee should leave knowing: their rating, that there is no increase, and what needs to change. If there is a PIP in place, reference it.
Script 4 — Employee Pushes Back on the Increase
"I understand this feels lower than you expected, and I want to explain how we got here. Valentis uses a structured salary review process where increases are set based on your performance rating and where your salary sits within the market band for your role. This is applied consistently to everyone in the company — I do not have discretion to change it unilaterally.

Your increase of [X]% is the outcome of that process for your rating and band position. If you believe there has been an error in your performance rating, that is the right thing to raise — and we can look at the assessment together.

What I cannot do is change the outcome because you feel it should be different. What I can do is make sure you understand the process fully and that your concerns are heard."
Note: Never apologise for the matrix. Never make comparative statements about other employees. If the employee escalates to HR, that is a legitimate step — let them know they can do that.
