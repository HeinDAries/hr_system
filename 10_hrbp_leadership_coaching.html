HRBP Tool 10 / 10 — Leadership Capability & Coaching Framework

Leadership
Coaching

Leadership capability is the multiplier on everything else in the talent strategy. A high-capability leader retains their team, develops successors, converts trainees, and delivers results. A low-capability leader does the opposite — at scale. The HRBP builds, assesses, and coaches leadership capability across BaKuu Holding.

"The single biggest decision you make in your job — bigger than all the rest — is who you name manager. Because it will determine the engagement of five times as many employees."

Jim Harter — Gallup
Why Leadership Capability is the HRBP's Job
The HRBP does not manage people directly — but every people outcome in the organisation is downstream of leadership quality. Attrition, engagement, productivity, safety, trainee conversion, talent development — all of them are leadership outcomes first and HR outcomes second.

The HRBP who improves the leadership capability of five Site Managers improves the people experience of every person in the organisation. This is the highest-leverage intervention available to the HR function — and it is entirely within the HRBP's gift to make happen.
At BaKuu Holding: Three of the five trainee exits in Myanmar trace to supervisor communication quality. The root cause of the BaKuu Holding attrition problem is a leadership capability gap — not a rewards gap, not a market gap.
The HRBP's role: Diagnose the capability gap, design the development intervention, coach the leader, and embed the capability into the way BaKuu Holding selects and promotes its people.
The BaKuu Holding Leadership Standard
BaKuu Holding needs a specific kind of leader — not a generic high-performer, but someone who can lead a small, remote, multi-cultural team in a high-risk technical environment. The leadership standard is defined by what the business requires, not by what looks impressive on paper.
Technical credibility
Field staff in oil and gas follow leaders who have done the work. A site manager who cannot speak to technical challenges with authority will lose the respect of experienced G4 drillers within weeks.
Cross-cultural communication
A site manager in Myanmar is leading a culturally distinct team in a foreign language context. Communication that works in Bangkok does not work in Mandalay. This capability is rarely developed through training alone — it requires lived experience and intentional reflection.
People development instinct
The BaKuu Holding growth plan depends entirely on leaders who develop the people below them — not leaders who hoard talent or ignore it. This is the competency the Myanmar supervisors are missing. It is also the competency that distinguishes a G4 driller from a G4 who could become a Site Manager.

Section 04 — GROW Coaching Model

GROW
Coaching Model

The GROW model is the HRBP's primary coaching framework. It structures a coaching conversation in four stages — Goal, Reality, Options, Way Forward — moving the leader from where they are to where they need to be through questions rather than answers.

The Coaching PrincipleA coaching conversation is not advice-giving dressed up with questions. It is a disciplined process of helping someone think more clearly about a situation they are already inside. The HRBP who coaches well asks questions the leader has not asked themselves — and waits for the answer.
G
Goal — What are we working toward?
Establish a clear, specific, motivating outcome for this conversation and for the broader development area
Coaching questions — Goal
"What would you like to walk away from this conversation having achieved?"
"In 6 months, when this is working well — what does that look like specifically?"
"How will you know when you've achieved this? What will you be seeing, hearing, or feeling?"
"Is that goal entirely within your control, or does it depend on others?"
Purpose: clarity and ownership. The leader must own the goal — not inherit one from the HRBP.
R
Reality — Where are we now?
Build an honest picture of the current situation — without judgement, without rushing to solutions
Coaching questions — Reality
"What is happening right now? Tell me what you are actually experiencing — not what you think should be happening."
"What have you already tried? What happened?"
"What is your role in the current situation? What have you contributed to it — positively or negatively?"
"On a scale of 1–10, how serious is this? What makes it a [their number] and not a 1?"
"What do you know for certain, and what are you assuming?"
BaKuu Holding application: "Tell me what happens when a trainee arrives on site for the first time. Walk me through what you actually do and say."
O
Options — What could we do?
Generate the full range of possible actions — before committing to any of them
Coaching questions — Options
"If you could do anything — without worrying about constraints — what options would you have?"
"What would you do if you had more time / budget / authority?"
"What would someone you really respect do in this situation?"
"You have said what you can't do. What can you do — even a smaller version of it?"
"Of everything we have explored, what feels most energising to you?"
Important: resist giving options before they have exhausted their own. The leader's own ideas have ten times the commitment of the HRBP's suggestions.
W
Way Forward — What will we do?
Convert insight into committed action — with specific steps, dates, and accountability
Coaching questions — Way Forward
"Of the options you explored, which one will you commit to? Not which one seems best in theory — which one will you actually do?"
"What is the first concrete action you will take, and when exactly will you do it?"
"What could stop you? How will you handle it if it does?"
"Who else needs to know about this? Who do you need support from?"
"When will we review progress? What will you report back to me?"
Close every coaching conversation with a specific commitment: action, timeline, and review date. Write it down in the room.
Section 05 — Difficult Conversations

Difficult
Conversations

The conversations most leaders avoid are usually the ones that matter most. The HRBP coaches leaders to have these conversations — and when necessary, has them directly. This section covers the most common difficult conversations at BaKuu Holding and how to navigate them.

Addressing Underperformance
The most avoided conversation in leadership. Leaders delay, hint, manage around, or complain to HR instead of speaking directly. The cost of delay compounds: the underperformer remains in place, the team loses respect for the leader, and the eventual conversation is harder than it would have been six months earlier.
Opening — Specific and Direct
"I want to talk about your performance on [specific metric/behaviour]. In the last [time period], [specific observable example]. This is not meeting the standard we need for this role."
Then — Create Understanding
"What is your perspective on why this is happening? I want to understand what is getting in the way before we talk about what changes."
Close — Agreement and Timeline
"What I need to see by [date] is [specific measurable improvement]. I will support you to get there. But I need to be clear: if we are not seeing this change by [date], we will need to have a different conversation."
HRBP note: Document the conversation immediately. Date, attendees, what was said, what was agreed. If this escalates to formal process, the documentation is the evidence.
The Retention Conversation
The retention conversation done well is a development conversation, not a negotiation. The mistake leaders make is waiting until the person has one foot out the door — by then, the conversation is reactive and desperate. Proactive retention conversations happen 9–12 months before the risk event.
Opening — Curiosity not Panic
"I want to have a genuine conversation about your future here. Not about the contract — about what you want, what would make this the right place for you for the next few years."
Explore — What Actually Matters
"What is working well for you right now? What would make this role even better? Is there anything about the future that concerns you or makes you uncertain?"
BaKuu Holding — Myo H. Application
"Myo, your contract renewal is coming up next year and I want to have this conversation well before then. You have built something exceptional in Myanmar. I want to understand what the next 3 years look like for you — what would make you want to see them through here."
HRBP note: The retention conversation must be authorised by the COO before it starts. Do not begin a negotiation without knowing the parameters — or you will make commitments you cannot keep.
Coaching a Resistant Leader — The Indonesia Scenario
The Indonesia Site Manager is the most challenging stakeholder in the BaKuu Holding leadership picture. High power, low trust in HR, consistent resistance to group processes. The HRBP cannot win this through authority — only through demonstrated value and patient relationship-building.
What Not to Do
Mandate HR process compliance — triggers more resistance
Position as "HR needs this from you" — immediately defensive
Escalate to COO before exhausting direct approaches
The Approach
Start with his operational problem — the 3 G3 vacancies — not the HR process
Solve it faster and better than he expected. Then introduce the next tool.
Use the Thailand SM as a peer voice — "here's what worked for him"
The Goal
Convert from High Power Sceptic to Neutral within 6 months. Convert to Champion within 12 months by accumulating demonstrated value. The Indonesia SM championing HR processes is worth more than any mandate from the COO — because the other site managers will follow his lead.

Section 07 — HRBP as Coach

HRBP
as Coach

The HRBP is not just a coach of others — they are in a continuous development journey themselves. This section is the HRBP's self-assessment and personal development framework for the path from Group HR Manager to CHRO.

HRBP Capability Self-Assessment — Ba, 2026
An honest assessment of current capability across the HRBP competency dimensions — and where to focus development energy for the CHRO transition.
The CHRO Gap — What to Close in 2026
The strongest foundation is the payroll and operational HR depth — nine years of multi-country experience that most CHRO candidates do not have. The gap to close is the strategic layer: being seen to think in business outcomes first, being included in business planning, and demonstrating that people decisions translate to financial results. The AIHR certifications, the toolkit, and the quarterly business review are all steps in exactly the right direction.
The CHRO Development Path — 2026–2027
Complete AIHR dual certification
Q4 2026
C&B + Strategic Talent Acquisition certifications. The credentials frame the strategic repositioning from payroll-lead to C&B / HRBP specialist.
Present talent strategy to CEO
Q2 2026
The single highest-leverage positioning move available. The HRBP who presents a talent strategy to the CEO has changed how the CEO sees the HR function.
Earn inclusion in 2027 business planning
Q4 2026
Not a request — something earned by demonstrating that HR input improves the quality of business decisions. Six months of scorecard, two successful business reviews, and a talent strategy on the table earns this.
Build the HR team — make the case for hire
Q3 2026
A CHRO leads an HR function — not a solo HR manager. The business case for a second HR hire signals readiness to lead a team and to think about building organisational capability, not just personal capability.
Coaching Yourself — Questions for the Journey
"What decision did the business make this quarter that was worse because HR wasn't in the room — and how do I use that as evidence that I should be?"
"What did I do this month that I could only have done because of my payroll background — and how do I make that visible to leadership?"
"If I were coaching someone else on the same transition I am making — what would I tell them to stop doing, start doing, and keep doing?"
"In 3 years, when I look back at 2026, what decision will I be most grateful I made early?"
"Am I building a reputation or a résumé? They are different things — and only one of them gets you the CHRO conversation."

Every tool was built on the same principle: that HR earns its authority by understanding the business better than anyone expects it to, and by connecting every people decision to a business outcome that the CEO and COO care about.

The HRBP who uses this toolkit consistently — the scorecard monthly, the business review quarterly, the workforce plan annually, the talent strategy as the anchor document — will have, within 12 months, transformed how the BaKuu Holding leadership team thinks about HR.
