HRBP Tool 3 — Stakeholder Mapping & Advisory Planner

Stakeholder
Mapping

An HRBP has no line authority over the leaders they advise. Every recommendation succeeds or fails based entirely on the quality of the relationships behind it. This toolkit makes stakeholder influence systematic, not accidental.

The HRBP Influence ProblemUnlike a COO who can direct teams, or a Finance Director who controls budgets, the HRBP achieves outcomes entirely through influence. That means the quality of your stakeholder relationships is not just important — it is your primary operating asset. This toolkit helps you manage that asset deliberately.
What Stakeholder Mapping Achieves
Most HR professionals manage stakeholder relationships reactively — responding to who approaches them, being helpful when asked, and hoping credibility accumulates over time. Stakeholder mapping replaces hope with strategy.
ClarityYou know exactly who you need to influence, at what level, and why — before you need their support.
PrioritisationYou allocate relationship-building time to the stakeholders who matter most, not just the loudest or most visible ones.
StrategyYou approach each stakeholder with a tailored engagement plan — their priorities, their communication style, their concerns about HR.
Early warningYou detect shifting attitudes before they become blockers — and act to maintain alignment before relationships erode.
The Three Stakeholder Questions
Before mapping any stakeholder, answer these three questions. They determine your entire engagement approach.
Power
How much formal authority does this person have over decisions that affect HR outcomes? Can they approve, block, or accelerate what you need?
Interest
How engaged is this person in people and HR matters? Do they actively seek HR input, or do they see HR as an administrative function to be managed?
Stance
Is this person an active supporter of strategic HR, neutral, sceptical, or resistant? Their current stance determines how you approach them — not how you wish they would respond.
Section 02 — The Influence Model

The Influence
Model

The Power-Interest Matrix is the foundational stakeholder tool. It places every stakeholder into one of four quadrants based on how much power they hold and how interested they are in HR matters — and prescribes a specific engagement strategy for each.

↑ HIGH POWER LOW POWER ↓
KEEP SATISFIED High Power / Low Interest
These stakeholders can block or approve major HR initiatives but are not deeply engaged in the day-to-day. Do not overload them — but do not let them be surprised. Keep them informed of major moves before they happen.
CEO (when disengaged) Board members External investors
MANAGE CLOSELY High Power / High Interest
Your most critical stakeholders. They have the authority to make decisions and the interest to be actively involved in them. Invest the most time here. Build deep, trust-based relationships. Meet regularly. Know their priorities.
COO CEO (when engaged) Country Managers
MONITOR Low Power / Low Interest
Minimum viable engagement. Keep informed through general communications. Do not invest significant personal relationship-building time here — but do not ignore them. Attitudes can shift, and today's monitor can become tomorrow's resistant stakeholder.
Junior office staff External vendors
KEEP INFORMED Low Power / High Interest
These people care deeply about people matters but do not hold formal decision authority. They are often informal influencers — respected voices whose opinion shapes others. Keep them well-informed and engaged; they can become powerful advocates or vocal critics.
Team leads Senior drillers Employee advocates
← LOW INTEREST Interest in HR / People Matters HIGH INTEREST →
Beyond the Matrix — The Third Dimension
Power and interest tell you where to focus. But the most important variable is stance — how each stakeholder currently views HR's role. This determines how you approach the relationship, not just how often.
Champion
Actively advocates for HR. Believes people strategy drives business outcomes. Pulls HR into decisions.
Approach: leverage and maintain
Neutral
Neither blocks nor enables. Follows the path of least resistance. Waits to see if HR is useful before committing.
Approach: prove value early
Sceptic
Doubts HR's strategic value. May have had a bad experience with HR. Needs to see evidence before engaging.
Approach: data and small wins
Resistant
Actively works against HR involvement. Sees people decisions as exclusively managerial territory. Requires careful navigation.
Approach: understand root cause

Section 05 — Engagement Strategies

Engagement
Strategies

A tailored engagement strategy for each quadrant of the Power-Interest Matrix. The right approach depends entirely on where the stakeholder sits — and using the wrong strategy is worse than using none.

Quadrant 1 — High Power, High Interest
Manage Closely
COO · Laos · Thailand
These stakeholders require your most intensive and personalised engagement. The relationship must be genuinely two-way — you learn from them as much as you advise them.
→Monthly one-to-one advisory meetings with a prepared agenda
→Proactive sharing of people intelligence before they ask for it
→Pre-briefing on any significant HR decision before it is announced
→Soliciting their input on HR priorities — and visibly acting on it
→Attendance in their operational review meetings, not just HR meetings
Common Mistakes to Avoid
✗Only engaging when you have a problem or need an approval
✗Sending lengthy reports when a concise verbal update would suffice
✗Using HR language when they think in operational outcomes
✗Agreeing with everything they say to preserve the relationship
Quadrant 2 — High Power, Low Interest
Keep Satisfied
CEO · Myo H. · Finance · Indonesia
These stakeholders will not block you if you keep them informed and avoid surprises. The risk is in assuming silence means support — it does not. Silence means they have not had a reason to object yet.
→Quarterly briefings — concise, outcome-focused, no HR jargon
→Pre-notification before any decision that affects their area
→Framing HR in terms of risk mitigation — they respond to risk language
→Never surprise them with bad news — always pre-brief sensitive matters
The Danger Zone
The most common HRBP failure with Keep Satisfied stakeholders is under-engaging until a crisis hits, then over-engaging at the worst possible moment. The absence of visible interest does not mean absence of opinion — it means they have not been given a reason to form one yet.
Quadrant 3 — Low Power, High Interest
Keep Informed
Site Supervisors · Senior Drillers
These are your informal intelligence network. They are close to the ground, and they know things leadership does not. They cannot approve HR decisions but they can influence sentiment. Invest in genuine listening relationships here — not policy communications. A supervisor who trusts the HRBP will flag attrition risk before the person resigns. That early warning is worth more than any retention programme implemented after the fact.
Quadrant 4 — Low Power, Low Interest
Monitor
Junior Staff · Vendors
Minimum viable attention. Include in general HR communications. Note any shifts in engagement or sentiment. The primary risk here is not current friction — it is missing the moment when someone from this quadrant moves into a higher-influence position. Positions shift as BaKuu Holding grows. Today's junior driller is tomorrow's team lead.
Section 06 — Trust-Building Framework

Trust-Building
Framework

Trust is the HRBP's operating currency. Without it, the best recommendation lands as an opinion. With it, the same recommendation becomes a decision. Trust is built systematically — through consistent behaviours over time, not through single impressive moments.

The Maister Trust EquationDavid Maister's Trust Equation (from The Trusted Advisor) defines trust as: Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) ÷ Self-Orientation. The denominator — self-orientation — is the single biggest driver of low trust. An HRBP who appears to be advancing their own agenda rather than the business's agenda destroys trust faster than any competence gap.
The Four Components of HRBP Trust
Credibility
Know your stuff
You are believed because you demonstrate deep knowledge — of HR, of the business, of the data. At BaKuu Holding, your nine years of multi-country payroll depth is the foundation. Layer business acumen (Tool 2) on top to become credible in both HR and business domains.
Reliability
Do what you say
You are trusted because you follow through — every time, on time. A COO who relies on HR data needs to know the data will be there, accurate and on schedule. Every missed commitment erodes reliability. Every delivery on a promise builds it.
Intimacy
Create safety
People share difficult information with you because they trust you to handle it with discretion and good judgement. Intimacy is built through confidential conversations handled well, through remembering what matters to people, through genuine curiosity about their challenges — not just their role.
Low Self-Orientation
The trust killer
If leaders believe you are advancing HR's interests over the business's interests, trust collapses. An HRBP who defends every HR policy regardless of context, or who prioritises process compliance over business outcomes, signals high self-orientation. The antidote: always lead with what is best for the business, and let HR process serve that goal — not the other way around.

Section 07 — Difficult Stakeholders

Difficult
Stakeholders

Every HRBP encounters stakeholders who resist, ignore, or actively undermine HR's role. This section provides a diagnostic and response framework for the four most common difficult stakeholder types — all of which exist at BaKuu Holding.

The Core PrincipleA difficult stakeholder is not a problem to be managed around. They are a signal. Their resistance carries information about a past experience, a current fear, or a genuine gap between HR's value and what HR has actually delivered. Diagnose before you respond.
S
The Sceptic
Indonesia Site Manager archetype — doubts HR value, waits for proof
Root cause: past disappointment
Diagnosis
Ask yourself: what has HR done to this person in the past? Was there a policy applied without context? A promise not kept? An HR process that cost them time with no visible benefit? The scepticism almost always has a specific origin.
Response
Do not defend HR or explain what HR is supposed to do. Ask what their biggest people problem is — then solve it. A single tangible win changes the conversation from "what has HR done for me?" to "what else can HR do?"
What to avoid
Do not introduce new HR requirements to a sceptic. Do not try to convince them HR is valuable through argument. Do not schedule relationship-building meetings before you have earned the right to their time.
R
The Resistant
Actively undermines HR involvement — makes unilateral people decisions
Root cause: territory or fear
Diagnosis
Resistance usually reflects either territorial protection (they see people decisions as their exclusive domain) or fear (HR involvement means scrutiny they would rather avoid). Which is it? The response differs significantly.
Response
For territorial resistance: frame HR involvement as making them more effective, not limiting their authority. For fear-based resistance: create safety — no HR conversation should feel like an inspection. Position yourself as an advisor, not an auditor.
Escalation
If resistance persists and creates genuine business risk (unilateral hiring outside grade, discriminatory decisions), escalate to COO with specific examples and business risk framing — not a complaint about non-cooperation.
B
The Bypasser
Makes people decisions without consulting HR — acts first, informs later
Root cause: habit or urgency
Diagnosis and Response
Bypassers are often not hostile to HR — they are just fast-moving and have learned that involving HR slows things down. The solution is to make HR involvement faster and more valuable than bypassing it. If it takes HR three days to respond to a hiring query and the manager can decide in an afternoon, the behaviour is rational. Make HR the faster, smarter option.
Practical Actions
Set a 24-hour response commitment for urgent people queries. Create a one-page decision guide for the most common people decisions. After a bypass occurs, debrief — not to discipline, but to show what could have been done better with HR involved. One good debrief often prevents ten future bypasses.
