HRBP Tool 5 — HR Scorecard & People Metrics Dashboard

HR Scorecard
& Metrics

The monthly HR Scorecard is the single most credibility-building artefact an HRBP can produce. It turns people data into a business conversation — and earns the HRBP a permanent seat in the operational review room.

The Strategic PurposeA People Scorecard is not a reporting exercise. It is the mechanism by which the HRBP demonstrates that people decisions have measurable business consequences. Every number on the scorecard should connect to a business outcome. If it does not, it should not be on the scorecard.
What a Scorecard Changes
Without a scorecard, HR operates on opinion. With a scorecard, HR operates on evidence. The difference is not just credibility — it is the nature of the conversations you are invited into.

A COO who receives a monthly people scorecard begins to internalise HR as a strategic function. They start asking HR questions before making decisions — because they have learned that HR has data, not just opinions. That shift is the foundation of the HRBP mandate.
Before scorecard: "HR says attrition is high" — easy to dismiss as perception
After scorecard: "Myanmar attrition is 24% annualised vs 12% target — $87k replacement cost exposure this quarter" — impossible to dismiss
The Four Scorecard Principles
01 — Business First
Every metric must connect to a business outcome. Headcount for its own sake is not a scorecard metric. Headcount vs programme requirement — is.
02 — Targets Always
A number without a target is just data. A number vs a target is intelligence. Every metric on the scorecard must have an agreed target or threshold — agreed with the COO, not set unilaterally by HR.
03 — Trend Over Snapshot
A single data point tells you where you are. A trend tells you where you are going. Always show three to six periods of history alongside the current reading.
04 — Narrative Required
Data without interpretation is not analysis. Every scorecard must include a two-to-three sentence narrative explaining what the data means and what the HRBP recommends as a result.
Section 02 — Metric Selection Guide

Metric
Selection

Not all HR metrics deserve scorecard space. The right metrics are the ones that connect directly to your organisation's operational value drivers. This guide explains how to select, weight, and present them.

The Metric Selection Test
Before adding any metric to the scorecard, it must pass three tests. If it fails any one of them, it should not be on the scorecard — it belongs in an internal HR report instead.
01
Business Relevance Test
Can you complete this sentence with a specific operational consequence? "If this metric deteriorates, [specific business outcome] is at risk." If you cannot complete it convincingly, the metric is not scorecard-ready.
02
Actionability Test
If this metric moves in the wrong direction, can you name a specific action the HRBP or business would take in response? If the answer is "raise awareness" or "monitor more closely," the metric is not driving decisions — it is just tracking them.
03
Data Quality Test
Can you produce this metric accurately, consistently, and on time every reporting cycle? A metric you can only calculate approximately, or that changes definition month to month, will undermine rather than build your credibility.

The Vanity Metric Trap
These metrics feel important but rarely drive decisions. Understand why they appear on HR dashboards — and why they should not dominate your COO scorecard.
Training hours per employee Vanity
Measures input, not outcome. Replace with: training completion rate for critical skills, or capability uplift score post-training.
Total headcount Vanity
Tells you how many people you have, not whether you have the right ones. Replace with: headcount vs programme requirement by site.
HR policy compliance % Low Value
Process measure. Relevant internally; not a business leadership metric. Remove from COO scorecard.
Number of HR initiatives completed Low Value
Activity measure. A COO does not care how many HR projects ran — only what changed as a result.

Section 06 — Narrative & Commentary

Narrative
& Commentary

The numbers are only half the scorecard. The HRBP's narrative — what the data means, why it moved, and what comes next — is what transforms a report into a strategic conversation.

Why Narrative MattersA COO who sees attrition at 24% will immediately want to know: why is it 24%? What is being done? When will it improve? If your scorecard does not answer those questions, the COO will form their own answers — and those answers may not include HR.
The HRBP Commentary Structure
Every scorecard metric with an amber or red status requires a structured commentary. Use this three-part structure every time.
01
What: State the fact and its direction
Lead with the specific number and whether it is moving in the right or wrong direction. Do not bury the lead. "Myanmar attrition is 24% annualised, up from 18% last quarter and above the 12% target."
02
Why: State the root cause (not the symptom)
Explain the driver you have identified through your diagnostic or intelligence. Be specific — avoid vague language like "market conditions" or "general disengagement." "Exit data from three Q1 leavers consistently cites supervisor communication and lack of development feedback as primary reasons."
03
Next: State the action and the expected outcome
Name what you are doing and when results should be visible. Give the COO a specific date to check back on. "Supervisor communication coaching commencing March 18. 30/60/90-day trainee check-in programme activating with next cohort. Expect stabilisation by Q3 with data confirmation in the July scorecard."
Language to Use
✓ "This creates a direct risk to Q3 programme delivery in Indonesia — specifically rig utilisation is projected to fall to 78% without the three open G3 roles filled."
✓ "If the trend continues at the current rate, we will breach the 18% attrition threshold by June — triggering an estimated $174k in replacement costs."
✓ "We are recommending Option A (targeted retention bonus for G4 drillers in Laos) over Option B (market hiring) — 60-day timeline vs 120-day timeline, at comparable cost."
Language to Avoid
✗ "We are working on improving our engagement levels across the organisation." — vague, no action, no timeline, no owner
✗ "Attrition remains a challenge in the current market environment." — excuses instead of analysis, no accountability
✗ "HR has been very busy this quarter implementing multiple initiatives." — activity reporting, not outcome reporting

Section 08 — Reporting Cadence

Reporting
Cadence

A great scorecard delivered inconsistently is not a scorecard — it is a one-off report. Cadence turns data into a rhythm that stakeholders come to expect, rely on, and plan around. This is what embeds the HRBP function into the operating heartbeat of the business.

The Cadence PrincipleThe COO's confidence in your scorecard is built over six months, not six minutes. Consistent delivery on a predictable schedule — same format, same timing, always with a recommendation — is what converts a data-sharing exercise into a strategic rhythm.

Monthly Scorecard — Prep Timeline
Day -7
Request data extracts from payroll, ATS, and HSE records for the previous month
Day -5
Calculate all Tier 1 KPIs and populate the live scorecard. Flag any amber or red readings
Day -3
Draft narrative commentary for all off-track metrics. Identify the one COO decision required this period
Day -1
Build the COO one-pager using the Report Builder (Section 07). Review language — no HR jargon
Day 0
Send scorecard to COO by 8am. Schedule 15-minute verbal walkthrough within 48 hours
Day +2
Verbal walkthrough with COO. Capture any decisions, challenges to your analysis, or additional data requests
Day +5
Log agreed actions in HRBP action tracker. Update next month's scorecard template with any new metric requests
What Good Looks Like — 12 Months In
These are the observable signs that your scorecard cadence has successfully embedded HR as a strategic function at BaKuu Holding.
✓ The COO asks "what does the scorecard say?" before making a significant people decision
✓ Site managers proactively flag people issues to HRBP before they appear in the data
✓ The CEO references people metrics in board discussions without being prompted by HR
✓ HR is invited into the annual business planning cycle before it begins — not after the plan is set
✓ The COO challenges a scorecard reading because they have become familiar enough with the data to notice anomalies
✓ Finance requests HR people cost data to complete their own reporting — HR becomes a data source, not just a consumer
