OD Tool 9 / 9 — Decision Rights & Governance Map

What Are
Decision Rights?

Decision rights define who has the authority to make which decisions — and who must be consulted, informed, or involved. When decision rights are unclear, the organisation defaults to either over-escalation (every decision goes to the COO) or under-accountability (no one owns the outcome). Both are active problems at BaKuu Holding.

"Most coordination failures at BaKuu Holding are not caused by bad intentions or weak people — they are caused by the absence of a shared understanding of who has the authority to decide what. Decision rights make the implicit explicit."

OD Governance Principle
Decision Rights vs Accountability
These two concepts are related but distinct. Confusing them is a common governance design error.
Decision Rights
The authority to make a decision — before the outcome is known. Includes the right to gather input, weigh options, and commit to a course of action. The decision-maker bears the process risk.
Accountability
The obligation to answer for the outcome — after the decision is made. Accountability can be assigned to someone different from the decision-maker. The accountable party bears the outcome risk.
BaKuu Holding example: The G4 Senior Driller has the accountability for trainee conversion rate — but currently has no formal decision rights over trainee programme delivery, schedule, or development interventions. This mismatch produces ghost accountability: the outcome is expected but the authority to influence it is absent.
Why Decision Rights Break Down
Never defined in the first placeThe organisation grew from a small team where everyone knew who decided what — informally. As it scaled, the informal norms were never formalised. Each person assumes someone else has the authority. At BaKuu Holding: who approves an emergency G3 hire? Who decides programme delivery schedule?
Defined but not communicatedRole profiles exist but do not specify authority limits. The G4 knows they are responsible for trainees — but not whether they can unilaterally adjust the training schedule or must consult the SM first.
Structure changed but rights were not updatedWhen the Ops Lead is created, the COO's decision rights over operational matters must shift to the Ops Lead — or the role has no real authority and the COO remains the de facto decision-maker for everything the SMs escalate.
Consistency risk across countriesIn a four-country operation, the same decision (e.g. local hire at G2) may be made by the SM in one country and escalated to the COO in another — because rights were communicated inconsistently at founding. Result: SMs with different effective authority levels, and a COO who receives inconsistent escalation volumes.

Section 02 — The RACI Framework

The RACI
Framework

RACI is the standard decision rights mapping tool. Used correctly, it makes authority, accountability, input, and information flow explicit for every decision that matters. Used incorrectly, it becomes a bureaucratic exercise that produces a spreadsheet nobody reads.

RACI — The Four Roles
R
Responsible
The person who does the work — executes the decision, completes the task, or produces the output. There can be more than one R, but multiple Rs without clear coordination often produce the same confusion as no R.
BaKuu Holding: G4 driller who conducts the month-3 check-in conversation
A
Accountable
The person who owns the outcome — the single named individual who answers for whether the task was done correctly and on time. There must be exactly one A per decision. If there are two, there is effectively none.
BaKuu Holding: Site Manager — accountable for trainee conversion rate in their country
C
Consulted
People whose input is sought before the decision is made. Two-way communication. The decision-maker is not obligated to follow their input — but is obligated to seek it. Too many Cs slow decisions.
BaKuu Holding: Group HR consulted on any G1–G4 termination before SM decides
I
Informed
People who receive information after the decision is made. One-way communication. No input required. If someone who is I frequently provides unsolicited input, either they should be C — or they have a boundary management issue.
BaKuu Holding: COO informed of all G1–G3 hires made by SM within budget authority
RACI Design Rules
Rule 1 — Exactly one A per decision. If two people share accountability, neither is accountable. If the RACI shows two As, resolve it before publishing.
Rule 2 — Minimise Cs. More than two Cs on a single decision usually indicates scope creep in the consultation. Each C adds cycle time. Ask: "do we need their input, or just their buy-in?"
Rule 3 — A and R can be the same person. For operational decisions, the person doing the work is often also the accountable party. This is appropriate at SM level for within-country operational decisions.
Rule 4 — Every cell must be intentional. A blank cell is a governance gap. An "I" that is really a "C" in practice is a governance ambiguity. Review each cell and ask: "is this what we actually want?"
Rule 5 — RACI requires communication, not just documentation. A RACI that is designed but not communicated to all role holders produces no behaviour change. Each A must know they are the A. Each R must know what R means in practice.
RACI Design Anti-Patterns
The Rubber-Stamp A: The COO is listed as A for every decision because "ultimately everything reports up to them." Produces over-escalation. The A should be the lowest level at which the decision can be made effectively.
The Courtesy C: Stakeholders who are "consulted" out of political courtesy rather than genuine need for input. Creates decision delay without adding decision quality. At BaKuu Holding: Indonesia SM does not need to be C on Thailand programme scheduling.
The Vanity I: Role holders who are listed as I because they "want to be kept in the loop" — but who then use the information to provide unsolicited input and effectively behave as C. Either designate them C or remove the I and manage expectations.
The Orphan Decision: A decision that has an R (someone doing it) but no A (no one accountable for the outcome). Common in cross-functional decisions. At BaKuu Holding: monthly HR data submission — who is accountable if a country does not submit?

Section 04 — HR Governance Model

HR Governance
Model

The HR governance model defines where Group HR has mandatory authority, where it has advisory authority, and where Site Managers have full autonomy. Without this distinction, SMs will treat all HR guidance as optional — because no one has explicitly told them otherwise.

The Core Distinction in HR Governance
Mandatory vs Advisory vs SM Autonomy
Group HR does not have — and should not have — authority over everything HR-related. The governance model is designed to define the three zones: where compliance is non-negotiable (Group HR is mandatory), where Group HR's role is to add value through expertise (advisory), and where the SM is best placed to decide without Group HR involvement (full autonomy). Getting this wrong in either direction creates problems: too much mandatory authority creates bureaucracy and resistance; too little creates inconsistency and compliance risk.
Mandatory
Group HR has binding authority — SM compliance is not optional
These are the domains where inconsistent application creates legal risk, ethical risk, or structural integrity risk. Group HR must be involved before action is taken — not informed after. The COO explicitly endorses this authority in the SM onboarding and the annual performance review.
All terminationsGroup HR must review and confirm legal process compliance before any termination communication. No exceptions. This applies in all four (and future five) countries regardless of local HR capacity.
Salary band placement and merit increasesAll G1–G4 offers and merit adjustments must be within approved salary bands. Group HR approves exceptions. No SM can make a salary commitment outside the band without Group HR sign-off.
Grade assignmentsAll roles must be graded against the G1–G8 framework. SMs cannot unilaterally assign a grade to a new or changed role. Group HR grades all roles — including those the SM initiates.
HR policy complianceAll group HR policies (harassment, conduct, data privacy, working hours, HSE where HR-related) are mandatory across all countries. SMs apply policies — they do not adapt or override them.
Monthly HR data submissionAll SMs submit the standard monthly HR data template by the 5th of each month. Non-submission is escalated to COO within 3 working days. Group HR consolidates for group talent review.
Trainee programme frameworkThe G1→G4 development framework, milestones, and check-in process are defined by Group HR and are mandatory across all countries. Country-level adaptation is within the framework, not outside it.
Advisory
Group HR provides expertise — SM makes the final decision
These are the domains where Group HR's value is in bringing expertise, frameworks, and cross-country perspective — not in controlling outcomes. The SM makes the final decision and is accountable for it. Group HR's job is to make that decision better, not to own it.
G1–G2 candidate selectionGroup HR provides job design support and recruitment framework. SM selects from candidates who meet the minimum criteria.
Performance management (individual)Group HR provides the framework and quality-checks the process. SM owns the conversation and the outcome rating.
Development conversations (G1–G3)Group HR trains G4 supervisors and provides the check-in framework. G4 and SM own the conversations.
Team dynamics and cultureGroup HR advises on cultural interventions. SM decides whether and how to act. Group HR does not impose culture programmes without SM partnership.
Country-level workforce planningGroup HR provides the workforce planning model (OD Tool 6). SM inputs country-level data and reviews projections. Final plan approved by COO.
Succession candidate identificationGroup HR facilitates the process and provides the assessment framework. SM recommends candidates. COO approves. Group HR does not have veto over SM recommendations.
SM Autonomy
SM decides — Group HR is not involved unless requested
These are the domains where the SM has full authority and Group HR's involvement would add no value — and would signal a lack of trust in SM judgment. The SM does not need Group HR's permission or input. Group HR's role is to ensure SMs know what falls in this category — so they do not over-escalate or unnecessarily involve Group HR.

Section 06 — Governance Forums & Cadence

Governance Forums
& Cadence

Governance is not only about who decides — it is also about when decisions are made, reviewed, and escalated. The governance calendar ensures that the right conversations happen at the right frequency, with the right attendees and the right outputs.

BaKuu Holding Governance Architecture — 2026 onwards
Five forums. Four cadences. One integrated governance rhythm.
BaKuu Holding currently has no formal governance calendar. Decisions are made ad hoc, reviews happen when the COO initiates them, and Group HR has no standing agenda. The governance architecture below is not bureaucracy — it is the minimum structure needed to manage a 42-person, 4-country organisation that is growing to 65 FTE across 5 countries. Without it, the COO remains the sole integrating mechanism, which is the structural problem the Ops Lead is designed to solve.
Forum 1
Monthly HR Data Review
Cadence: Monthly
Owner: Group HR (Ba)
Attendees: Ba alone (reporting to COO)
Duration: 1–2 hours (analysis)
Group HR consolidates the monthly HR data submission from all four (then five) countries. Produces a one-page group talent summary for the COO: headcount vs plan, attrition, pipeline status, vacancies, compliance flags. Flags any issues requiring COO decision within the current month. Inputs into the quarterly talent review.
Required output: Group HR monthly summary Deadline: 10th of each month
Forum 2
COO–HRBP Monthly 1:1
Cadence: Monthly
Owner: Ba (HRBP)
Attendees: COO + Ba
Duration: 45–60 minutes
The primary channel for HR-to-business intelligence. Ba presents the monthly HR summary, flags decisions required from the COO, updates on active interventions, and raises any governance issues that require COO direction. This is how Ba earns access to business planning — not by being invited to business meetings, but by demonstrating that every monthly conversation has business-level intelligence and a clear ask. Agenda: data review (15 min), active decisions (15 min), HR workplan update (10 min), COO intel (10 min).

The output is the CEO-approved HR plan that gives Ba authority to act on structural interventions without individual approvals throughout the year.
Required output: Approved HR plan, headcount budget, scenario confirmation

Section 08 — Embedding & Maintaining

Embedding
& Maintaining

A governance map that is designed but not embedded is an academic exercise. Decision rights must be communicated, tested, reinforced, and updated as the organisation changes. This section is the implementation guide for making BaKuu Holding's governance model real rather than theoretical.

"The RACI is not a document. It is a set of conversations that need to happen. The document is the output. The conversations are the intervention."

OD Governance Principle
The Four-Step Embedding Sequence
1
COO endorsement — not just Group HR publication
The governance map must be communicated by the COO to all SMs — not distributed by Group HR alone. If SMs receive the RACI from Group HR, they may treat it as an HR initiative. If they receive it from the COO, they treat it as an operational requirement. The COO endorsement conversation should be: "I am formalising who decides what across the group. This is the framework I expect everyone to operate within. Group HR has designed it — I have approved it — and I will hold you to it." The SM peer forum inaugural session is the right venue for this communication.
2
Role profile integration
Decision rights must appear in role profiles — not only in a separate governance document. When a G4 driller's role profile includes "decision authority: adjust trainee programme schedule within SM-approved parameters; escalate to SM if trainee is at risk of programme failure," it becomes part of the role holder's lived understanding of what their job is. When it only appears in a governance map, it stays theoretical. Integrate the most relevant RACI entries into each role profile as part of the OD Tool 3 update cycle (G4 by Q2 2026; Ops Lead by Q3 2026).
3
Test through use — track escalation patterns
The most reliable test of whether the governance model is working is to track what the COO is actually being asked to decide. If operational decisions are still being escalated to COO level 90 days after the RACI is communicated, either the RACI was not communicated effectively, or there is a behaviour pattern (Indonesia SM's escalation habit) that requires a separate intervention. Ba tracks the types of COO escalations monthly and reviews whether the pattern is consistent with the RACI. Anomalies are raised in the monthly 1:1.

Update the RACI when the structure changes
The governance map becomes outdated the moment a structural change occurs. Three structural changes are planned for 2026–2027 that each require a RACI update: (1) Group Ops Lead appointment — Q3 2026, (2) HR Coordinator appointment — Q4 2026, (3) Country 5 SM appointment — Q2 2027. Each of these changes shifts A/R assignments in multiple decision categories. Ba schedules a RACI review within 30 days of each structural change. Updated RACI is communicated by COO (or Ops Lead where relevant) at the next governance forum.
Signs the Governance Model is Working
COO escalation volume decreasing — SMs making operational decisions without COO involvement
Group HR mandatory guidance is being followed consistently across all four countries — no policy divergence
G4 drillers taking ownership of trainee development decisions within their defined authority — not escalating to SM for routine programme questions
Monthly HR data submitted by all countries by the 5th — no Group HR chasing required
No termination or G4+ salary commitment made without Group HR involvement — even in Indonesia
Signs the Governance Model is Breaking Down
COO is still making operational decisions that the RACI assigns to SM or Ops Lead — either the COO has not delegated, or SMs are bypassing the Ops Lead
SMs in different countries describe different understandings of what they can decide autonomously — governance was communicated inconsistently
Group HR is being informed of decisions after they are made in domains where Group HR should be consulted before — particularly terminations and salary commitments
Structural changes (Ops Lead hire, Country 5 SM) have happened but the RACI has not been updated — new role holders are operating without a defined decision rights framework
The governance forums (peer forum, talent review) have lapsed — decisions are being made informally again and the monthly data flow has broken down
