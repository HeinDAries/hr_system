HRBP Tool 6 — Workforce Planning & Scenario Modeller

Workforce
Planning

Workforce planning is the process of ensuring the organisation has the right people, with the right skills, in the right roles, at the right time — at a cost the business can sustain. It is the most strategically important thing an HRBP does.

The Core PrincipleMost organisations hire reactively — filling vacancies as they appear. Workforce planning replaces that reactive cycle with a forward-looking model: understanding what the business will need 12 to 24 months ahead, then working backwards to ensure supply is ready before demand arrives. The HRBP who masters this becomes indispensable in business planning.
Why Workforce Planning Matters at BaKuu Holding
BaKuu Holding operates in a constrained talent market for a highly specialised role — the experienced driller. Unlike most professional roles, drillers cannot be hired off the shelf. They take 18–24 months to develop from G3 to G4. External hiring is slow, expensive, and unreliable in Southeast Asia.

This means the gap between "we need more G4 drillers" and "we have more G4 drillers" is nearly two years. An HRBP who does not plan 24 months ahead on the driller pipeline is already behind the business need by the time the problem becomes visible.
Reactive model: G4 driller leaves → COO flags programme risk → HRBP begins sourcing → 90 days to hire → programme slips
Planned model: HRBP flags G4 retirement risk 18 months out → G3 development activated → successor ready before departure → zero programme impact

Section 04 — Demand Forecasting

Demand
Forecasting

Demand forecasting translates the business plan into headcount requirements. At BaKuu Holding, the primary driver of workforce demand is the drilling programme schedule — metres committed, rigs planned, and new country operations.

Demand Drivers — BaKuu Holding
Primary Driver
Programme Schedule
Number of active drilling programmes per country directly determines G3/G4 headcount requirement. Each active rig requires one G4 and 1.5 G3 FTE on average.
Growth Driver
Country Expansion
Any new country entry adds a baseline requirement: 1 Site Manager, 2 G4, 3 G3, plus support. Lead time for country-entry staffing is typically 6–9 months.
Pipeline Driver
Trainee Programme
Each cohort requires 6 trainees per intake to produce 4 qualified G2 completions (at 70% conversion target). Intake timing must align with 18-month development cycle.
Constraint Driver
Succession Events
Planned retirements, retention expiries, and known departure risks create demand spikes that must be pre-planned 12–18 months ahead to avoid operational disruption.

Section 06 — Scenario Planning

Scenario
Planning

Scenario planning stress-tests the workforce plan against different business futures. At BaKuu Holding, the three scenarios that matter most are base-case programme delivery, accelerated growth, and a contract-loss contraction.

Why Three ScenariosA workforce plan built around a single future is fragile. If the business grows faster than planned, you are understaffed. If it contracts, you are overstaffed and people costs rise as a percentage of revenue. Modelling three scenarios allows the COO to understand the range of outcomes and make informed decisions under uncertainty.

Additional Actions — High Growth Scenario
In addition to all base-case actions, the following are required if the growth scenario materialises:
Succession acceleration: Identify and fast-track one internal candidate to Site Manager level for 5th country — begin now, 15-month development window.
External G4 pipeline: Build a pre-qualified external candidate pool of 6–8 experienced drillers in the target region — engage before the contracts are signed, not after.
Trainee intake expansion: Add one additional cohort in Q1 2026 (targeting 12 trainees total) to ensure G2 supply is available for the growth wave in 2027.
Rewards review: Benchmark G4 and G3 compensation in the new country — ensure BaKuu Holding is competitive before starting hiring rather than after the first offer declines.

HRBP Response — Contraction Scenario
The HRBP's role in a contraction is to protect business-critical talent while managing people cost responsibly — in that order.
Step 1 — Redeploy: Identify cross-country or cross-programme deployment options for surplus G3/G4 before considering any exits. One country's surplus is often another's shortage.
Step 2 — Pause hiring: Immediately suspend all external hiring in the affected country. Defer the next trainee cohort intake by one cycle.
Step 3 — Protect critical talent: Identify the 5–8 individuals who are absolutely irreplaceable in the 24-month recovery window. Ringfence them with retention mechanisms before the market learns about the contraction.
Step 4 — If restructure required: Present COO with a talent-first restructure plan: who stays, who goes, why — based on capability and future-state requirements, not seniority or cost alone.

The Build–Buy–Borrow–Bind Framework
Build
Develop internally. Use when: the role requires BaKuu Holding-specific knowledge, the development timeline is feasible, and the candidate is identified.
Best for
G3→G4 pipeline, Site Manager succession, trainee programme
Buy
Hire externally. Use when: the gap is immediate, internal pipeline is insufficient, and the external market has supply.
Best for
Immediate G3 gap (Indonesia), bridge G4 supply, 5th country Site Manager
Borrow
Redeploy internally or use contractors. Use when: the gap is temporary, cross-country deployment is viable, or a contract hire bridges a known window.
Best for
Cross-country deployment, contract G4 bridge (12 months), programme surge cover
Bind
Retain critical talent. Use when: the person is irreplaceable in the planning window and departure would be catastrophic.
Best for
Myo H. retention bonus renewal, Laos G4 senior drillers, Wirawan S.
