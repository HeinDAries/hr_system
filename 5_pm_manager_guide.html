Chapter 01 — Foundation

Why Performance
Management Matters

Most managers in operations have never had formal training in performance conversations. This guide exists because the PM system only works if the people running it understand why it matters and how to do it well.

The Real Cost of Getting This Wrong In mineral exploration, losing one experienced driller costs an estimated 3–6 months and significant recruitment fees. Losing a Site Manager can delay an entire drilling program. Most of those departures begin with a feeling — not a better salary offer — that their work was not seen, valued, or rewarded fairly. Performance management, done well, is retention strategy.
The Manager's Job in the PM System
You are not the judge. You are the coach. Your job is to be clear about what good looks like, honest about where someone stands, and genuinely invested in helping them get better.
🎯
Set Goals Together
Goals agreed with the employee — not handed down. Clear, measurable, connected to what the company needs.
💬
Give Honest Feedback
Regularly, not just at review time. Praise what works. Address what does not. Never save everything for December.
📋
Document What Happens
If it was not written down, it did not happen. Brief notes after important conversations protect everyone.
The Number One Mistake Managers Make
Saving all feedback for the annual review. If your crew member finds out in December that you have been unhappy with their safety reporting since March, two things happen: they feel ambushed, and they do not trust the process. Neither outcome helps anyone.
What good looks like
  • Quarterly informal check-ins — 20 minutes, honest conversation
  • Immediate feedback when something goes wrong — same week, not same year
  • Genuine acknowledgement when something goes right
  • Mid-year review used to surface any concerns formally
  • Annual review has zero surprises for the employee
What to avoid
  • Silence for 11 months, then a Rating 2 in December
  • Rating everyone 3 to avoid difficult conversations
  • Vague goals that cannot be measured at review time
  • Basing ratings on the last 2 months (recency bias)
  • Letting one great thing inflate all ratings (halo effect)
What the Rating Scale Actually Means
The most important thing to understand about the BaKuu Holding rating scale: a Rating 3 is a good outcome. It means the employee did what was expected of them, fully and competently. Most of your team should be Rating 3. That is what a healthy, functional team looks like.
5
Exceptional
Significantly exceeded all goals. Rare — should be 1–3 people across the whole company. If you are rating more than that, your goals were probably too easy.
4
Strong
Exceeded most goals. Consistently high quality. This should be 5–7 people company-wide. Not half your team.
3
Meets Expectations — THIS IS GOOD
Did what was agreed, fully and competently. This is the most common rating. It is a positive outcome. Communicate it as such.
2
Needs Improvement
Key deliverables missed. Employee must know about this concern before the annual review — not at it.
1
Unsatisfactory
Significantly below standard. If this is the first time they are hearing about it, the process has already failed. Prior documentation is mandatory.
Chapter 02 — January Every Year

Setting Goals
with Your Team

Goals set the foundation for everything that follows. A poor goal makes a fair review impossible. A good goal makes the whole year clearer — for you and for your employee.

The SMART Test — Does Your Goal Pass?
Before you lock any goal, run it through this test. If it fails any of the five checks, rewrite it before signing.
S
Specific — Could two people read this goal and agree on what it means?
Bad: "Improve safety performance." Good: "Zero LTIs in 2026. Log all near-misses within 24 hours using the site reporting system."
M
Measurable — Will you know at review time whether this was achieved?
Bad: "Good crew management." Good: "Zero crew departures that were within my control. Resolve crew conflicts within 48 hours."
A
Achievable — Is this stretching but realistic given the resources available?
If you give someone a goal that requires equipment that does not work, or targets that are impossible without additional headcount, you have set them up to fail — not succeed.
R
Relevant — Does this connect to what the team and company need?
Individual goals should roll up to department goals, which roll up to company goals. If you cannot explain why a goal matters to the business, it probably should not be a goal.
T
Time-Bound — Is there a clear deadline or review point?
Annual goals are reviewed in December. Milestones within the year (e.g. Q2 targets) help you track progress at mid-year check-in.
Goal Templates by Role — Starting Points
Use these as a starting point, then adapt with your employee based on their specific situation and the company's priorities for the year. The weights must add to 100.
Site Manager / Drilling Supervisor
25%Safety: Zero LTIs for full year. All near-misses reported within 24 hours. HSE training 100% complete for crew.
20%Drilling Output: Achieve minimum 95% of agreed monthly meterage target over the year.
15%Rig Uptime: Maintain rig operational time above 85%. Unplanned downtime less than 5% of working days.
15%Crew Retention: Zero voluntary departures that were within manager's control. All crew performance conversations completed.
25%Development: Complete one agreed training or certification by end of year. Mentor one junior team member.
Senior Driller / Driller
30%Safety: Zero LTIs. Active participation in toolbox talks. Near-miss reporting maintained.
25%Drilling Performance: Achieve personal meterage targets set quarterly. Core recovery rate above agreed standard.
15%Equipment Care: Daily pre-use checks completed. Zero incidents attributable to equipment neglect.
15%Team Contribution: Support crew integration. Share knowledge with junior drillers.
15%Development: Complete one agreed skills or certification goal by year-end.
HSE Officer
30%Zero Incidents: Zero LTIs across assigned site. All incidents investigated within 5 working days.
25%Compliance: 100% HSE training completion for site. All permits and certifications current with zero lapses.
20%Near-Miss Culture: Increase near-miss reporting by minimum 20% vs prior year. All near-misses actioned within 48 hours.
10%Audit Readiness: Site passes all internal and external HSE audits without major findings.
15%Development: Complete agreed certification or regulatory update training by year-end.
Rules for Goal-Setting Conversations 3–5 goals maximum. Weights must add to 100%. At least one development goal every year. Safety must be weighted at least 25% for all operational roles. Goals are agreed together — not presented to be signed. If the employee disagrees, discuss it. Document the conversation.
Chapter 03 — Year-Round Practice

Giving Feedback
Throughout the Year

The annual review is a summary of a year of conversations — not a replacement for them. If you are only talking about performance once a year, you are already behind.

The Feedback Calendar — Minimum Standard
These are the minimum touchpoints BaKuu Holding expects from every manager. More is better. Less means the review system will not work.
Jan
Goal-Setting Meeting — 45–60 minutes
Sit down with each direct report. Set 3–5 goals together. Agree weights. Sign the goal sheet. Send to HR by 31 January. Do not do this over the phone or in a toolbox talk — it needs dedicated time.
Mar
Q1 Informal Check-In — 20 minutes
Ask: "How is 2026 going so far? Are your goals still realistic? Any blockers?" No forms, no ratings. Just a genuine conversation. Follow up with a brief email summary.
Jul
Mid-Year Review — 30–45 minutes
This is a formal meeting. Go through each goal — on track or behind? If behind, why and what changes? If there is a performance concern, raise it here in writing. A concern not raised at mid-year cannot appear as a low rating in December.
Oct
Q3 Check-In — 20 minutes
How is H2 tracking? Start preparing for annual review. Are goals still the same? What evidence do you have for each rating? Begin making notes now — do not try to remember everything in November.
Dec
Annual Review — 60–75 minutes
The formal end-of-year review. Rating confirmed by HR before this meeting. No surprises. See Chapter 04 for the full conversation guide.
The SBI Model — How to Give Feedback That Lands
SBI stands for Situation, Behaviour, Impact. It is the most reliable structure for giving feedback that is specific, fair, and actionable — whether positive or developmental.
S
Situation — When and where did this happen?
Be specific. Not "last month" but "on Tuesday 14 March, at the morning briefing." The situation grounds the conversation in fact, not impression.
B
Behaviour — What did the person do (or not do)?
Describe the observable behaviour — not the intention, character, or attitude. Not "you were reckless" but "you did not complete the pre-use check before starting the rig."
I
Impact — What was the effect?
Explain why it matters. On safety, on the team, on the project, on you. Not "it was bad" but "it meant the crew operated on unverified equipment for two hours — that is a safety exposure I cannot accept."
Example — Developmental Feedback Using SBI
Manager: "Somchai, I want to talk about what happened on Wednesday at the pre-shift briefing. [S — Situation] You started the brief five minutes late and three crew members had already gone to the rig. [B — Behaviour] What that meant was that the safety briefing was not heard by everyone, which is a compliance issue and a risk to the crew. [I — Impact] I need us to get that right every day — what happened, and what is going to change?"
Example — Positive Feedback Using SBI
Manager: "I want to acknowledge something you did this week. On Thursday morning, you spotted the hydraulic line wear before the shift started. [S] You stopped work, reported it immediately, and waited for the replacement before the crew started drilling. [B] That decision probably prevented a serious incident. That is exactly the safety culture we are building here — and I want you to know it was noticed. [I]"
Common Feedback Mistakes to Avoid
✗
The Feedback Sandwich
"You are doing great — but your output is too low — but you have a great attitude." The developmental feedback is buried and forgotten. Give it directly, not wrapped in praise.
✗
Personality Feedback
"You have a bad attitude." People cannot change their attitude on request — but they can change specific behaviours. Describe the behaviour you want to change, not the person.
✗
Vague Feedback
"You need to do better." Better at what? By how much? By when? Feedback that is too vague cannot be acted on. It just creates anxiety.
✗
Only Giving Feedback in Problems
If your crew only hears from you when something goes wrong, they will dread your voice. Acknowledge good work regularly. It costs nothing and changes everything.
Chapter 04 — The Most Important Meeting of the Year

The Review
Conversation

Book 60–75 minutes. Private room. No phone. The annual review conversation is where the whole year is summarised, the rating is confirmed, and the path forward is agreed. Here is how to run it well.

Before the Meeting — Three Things You Must Have Ready (1) The employee's self-assessment — read it before you arrive, not during. (2) Your completed manager assessment — ratings decided, evidence noted for each goal. (3) HR confirmation of the final calibrated rating — never walk into an annual review not knowing the final number.
The Six-Phase Meeting Structure
Follow this structure for every annual review. It takes 60–75 minutes. Do not skip phases to save time — each one serves a purpose.
1
Open — 5 minutes
Purpose: Set tone. Make it a conversation, not an interrogation.
Thank them for their self-assessment. Explain the purpose: "Today we are looking back at 2026 together, agreeing on the rating, and thinking about 2027." Start with something genuine and positive — one thing you appreciated about their year. One specific thing, not a generic compliment.
2
Employee Speaks First — 15 minutes
Purpose: Hear their view before you share yours. This changes the tone of the whole conversation.
"Walk me through your year — what are you most proud of?" Listen fully. Do not correct mid-sentence. Do not start defending your assessment. Just listen. Then: "What felt difficult? What would you do differently?" The answers tell you a lot about their self-awareness and maturity.
3
Goal Review — 20 minutes
Purpose: Go through each goal with evidence. Agree or discuss disagreements.
Take each goal in turn. State the agreed target, then the actual result. Assign the rating. For goals where you agree with their self-assessment, confirm and move on. Where you disagree: "My view on this goal is a 3, not a 4. Here is why — [specific evidence]. What is your response?" They have a right to respond. Listen. Note their response. If your evidence is solid, the rating stands — even if they disagree.
4
The Final Rating — 5 minutes
Purpose: State it clearly. Explain how it was calculated. Own it.
State the rating directly: "Your final rating for 2026 is [X] — [label]." Explain the weighted score: "Based on your goal scores and weights, your final score is [X.XX]." For Rating 3: reinforce that it is a good outcome — say it explicitly. "This means you delivered everything we agreed. That is solid performance." Do not be vague. Do not apologise for the rating. Own it.
5
Strengths, Development, Forward — 15 minutes
Purpose: Make the conversation useful, not just administrative.
Name 2–3 specific strengths — not generic ("good worker") but specific ("your near-miss reporting improved substantially — you logged 6 in H2 and the crew followed your lead"). Name 1–2 development areas — framed as growth, not criticism. Ask: "What do you need from me to get there?" Discuss their career goals: "Where do you want to be in 2–3 years?"
6
Close and Sign — 10 minutes
Purpose: Confirm next steps. Get the signature. End positively.
Outline goal-setting for 2027: "We will set goals together by end of January." Confirm when salary decisions will be communicated. Ask: "How are you feeling about the conversation? Anything to add?" Hand them the review document to sign. Explain: "Your signature confirms we had this conversation — not that you agree with every rating." If they refuse to sign, see Chapter 05.
Chapter 05 — The Conversations Nobody Wants to Have

Difficult
Conversations

Most performance systems fail because managers avoid difficult conversations. This chapter gives you practical scripts and frameworks for the situations that feel hardest.

01
Employee rated themselves a 4. You are giving them a 2.
This is the biggest gap possible. The employee believes they had a strong year. You have evidence that they did not. The gap is not a miscommunication — it is a fundamental difference in perception. Handle it directly.
Do
  • Acknowledge what went well — genuinely
  • Present your evidence goal by goal, factually
  • Give them space to respond to each goal
  • Acknowledge what was outside their control
  • Keep the rating if your evidence is solid
  • End with: "I want to help you succeed in 2027"
Do not
  • Change the rating under social pressure alone
  • Apologise for having a different view
  • Rush through to avoid the discomfort
  • Make it personal or attack their character
  • Use vague language — "your performance was not quite there"
Say this
Opening:"I can see from your self-assessment that you felt this was a strong year, and I want to acknowledge [specific genuine positive]. On the goal assessment, my view is different, and I want to walk you through my reasoning."
On each gap:"For goal 2 — drilling output — the target was 95% of monthly meterage. Your actual average was 71% over the year. I know some of this was affected by the equipment issue in Q2, and I have reflected that. The portion that was within your control still fell significantly short. My rating on this goal is a 2."
Closing:"I know this is not what you were expecting. I am telling you this directly because I want you to succeed in 2027, and I am committed to supporting that. Let us talk about what needs to change."
02
Employee becomes upset or emotional during the review.
Performance conversations carry emotional weight — especially for employees who genuinely care about their work and feel their rating does not reflect their effort. This is a normal human response. Your job is to stay calm and give the emotion space.
Do
  • Pause. Say nothing for a moment. Let it settle.
  • "Take a moment. There is no rush."
  • Offer a short break if needed
  • If very distressed: offer to continue the next day
  • Document that the conversation occurred and how it ended
Do not
  • Change the rating to manage the emotion
  • Become defensive or raise your voice
  • Keep talking while they are clearly not listening
  • Tell them they are "overreacting"
  • Promise things you cannot deliver to calm them down
03
Employee refuses to sign the review document.
This happens more often than managers expect, and it is almost always a misunderstanding of what the signature means. Clarify before escalating.
Do
  • Clarify first: "Your signature confirms the conversation happened — not that you agree with the rating"
  • If still refusing: note on the document with two witnesses present
  • Note: "Employee declined to sign — meeting held [date], witnessed by [name]"
  • Escalate to HR immediately
  • Never delay the process waiting for a signature
Do not
  • Argue or force the signature
  • Change the rating to get the signature
  • Leave the situation unresolved without HR
  • Treat refusal to sign as the end of the process
04
You need to raise a performance concern at mid-year.
This is the conversation most managers avoid and then regret in December when the concern has not improved. Raising it now protects both you and the employee.
How to open this conversation
Manager:"I want to use this mid-year check-in to raise something directly with you. I have noticed over H1 that [specific behaviour or result with evidence]. This concerns me because [impact]. I am not giving you a low rating today — this is a mid-year review, not a final assessment. But I want to be honest with you now, because if this does not improve in H2, it will affect your annual rating in December. I would rather give you the chance to turn this around than surprise you in December. What is your view on what I have described?"
After this conversation — send a follow-up email Within 24 hours, send a brief email: "Following our mid-year check-in today, I wanted to summarise the concern I raised: [description]. We agreed on the following next steps: [steps]. I am available to discuss further." Copy HR. This creates the documentation trail.
05
Rating 5 employee wants a promotion, not just a good review.
Your best people are the ones competitors most want to hire. If you cannot offer a promotion, you need to be honest about it — and offer something real in its place. Vague promises are worse than honest limitations.
How to handle this
Manager:"You had an exceptional year and I want you to hear that clearly. I also want to be honest with you: there is no immediate promotion available. What I can offer is [specific: merit increase %, expanded role scope, timeline for next role review, fast-track development plan]. I do not want to make promises I cannot keep — but I do want you to know that your contribution is valued and that we are serious about keeping you here."
Chapter 06 — The Paper Trail That Protects Everyone

What to Document
and When

Documentation is not bureaucracy. In a multi-country operation, a well-documented conversation protects you, protects the company, and protects the employee. Here is what to write down and when.

The Core Rule If it was not written down, it did not happen. This applies to every significant performance conversation — especially informal ones. A brief follow-up email takes 5 minutes and is worth its weight in legal protection.
Documentation Requirements by Event Type
Event What to Document How File Where Timing
Goal-Setting Meeting Agreed goals, weights, signatures BaKuu Holding Goal Sheet (Tool 1) HR file + employee copy Before 31 January
Informal Coaching / Feedback What was discussed, what was agreed, any actions Brief follow-up email to employee, CC HR Email trail — do not delete Within 24 hours
Mid-Year Review Progress on each goal, any concerns raised, H2 focus Mid-Year Review form (Tool 2) + summary email HR file + employee copy Within 5 working days
Performance Concern Raised Specific concern, evidence, what was said, employee response Email to employee summarising the conversation. CC HR. HR performance file (separate from general) Within 24 hours of the conversation
Annual Review Meeting Full review, final rating, development plan, signatures Annual Review form (Tool 2) — signed by all parties HR file + performance file + employee copy Within 5 working days
Refusal to Sign Date, attendees, that employee declined, manager statement Note on review form. Two witnesses sign. HR file immediately Same day
PIP Initiated Full PIP document — requirements, support, consequences PIP Tracker (Tool 3) Separate performance file Signed at PIP meeting
What "Brief Follow-Up Email" Actually Looks Like
You do not need a template — you need a habit. After any significant performance conversation, send something like this before the end of the day.
Example — post coaching conversation
To: [Employee name] CC: HR
Subject: Follow-up — our conversation today

Hi [Name],

Following our conversation this afternoon, I wanted to summarise what we discussed so we are both clear.

I raised a concern about [specific issue — e.g. pre-shift check completion rates in March]. We discussed that [summary of context and your view]. You responded that [their response — record it accurately].

We agreed that you will [specific action] by [date]. I will check in with you on [date].

Please let me know if I have missed anything or if you would like to discuss further.

[Your name]
Why CC HR on performance conversations? It creates a timestamped record outside your own email. If there is ever a dispute, HR has an independent copy. This is not about distrust — it is about protection for both parties.
