What is the PM framework · how it works at BaKuu Holding
Design principle: BaKuu Holding runs multi-country, mixed operations and office workforce. One rigid PM system fails both. This framework uses a common structure — goal-setting, mid-year review, annual review — but adapts the KPIs, language, and depth per role category. Operations managers use different metrics than HR or Finance staff. The process is the same. The content is not.

◈
The 5-Point Rating Scale
Used at annual review only · consistent definitions across all roles
5
Exceptional
Significantly exceeded all goals. Demonstrated outstanding impact beyond the scope of the role. Top 5–10% of performers.
4
Strong
Exceeded most goals. Delivered more than expected in key areas. Recognised by peers and stakeholders as high performer.
3
Meets Expectations
Met most goals. Performed reliably in all core areas. This is a good rating — it means the employee is doing their job well.
2
Needs Improvement
Partially met goals. Clear gaps in one or more areas. Development plan required. PIP may be initiated if pattern continues.
1
Unsatisfactory
Did not meet goals. Significant gaps across multiple areas. PIP initiated. Employment at risk if no sustained improvement.

BaKuu Holding PM Principles
Design decisions and why
No forced distribution
We do not mandate that X% of people receive a 3 and Y% receive a 5. If the team performs well, ratings reflect that. Forced distribution destroys trust and encourages gaming.
No surprise at annual review
Performance issues raised at annual review that were not raised during the year are HR failures, not employee failures. Feedback is continuous, not stored for December.
3 is explicitly a good rating
In many companies, employees fear a 3. We name it clearly: Meets Expectations means doing the job well. It is not a warning. This language must be consistent across all managers.
Goals agreed, not handed down
Manager proposes, employee discusses and agrees. A goal handed without conversation is a target, not a commitment. Ownership requires participation in setting.

SMART goal builder · generate properly structured goals
SMART Goal Framework
Every goal at BaKuu Holding must pass the SMART test before being agreed and logged
S
Specific
Clear, unambiguous description of what will be achieved and how it will be done.
Bad: "Improve safety." Good: "Reduce near-miss incidents by 20% through weekly toolbox talks."
M
Measurable
Quantifiable outcome — a number, percentage, or binary yes/no that cannot be disputed.
Bad: "Process payroll well." Good: "Achieve 99% payroll accuracy across all 4 countries."
A
Achievable
Stretching but realistic given the employee's role, resources, and context. Not a guaranteed pass.
Bad: "Fill all 13 vacancies in Q1." Good: "Fill 4 critical roles by end of Q2 2026."
R
Relevant
Connected to business objectives and the employee's actual scope of influence.
Bad: "Learn a new software." Good: "Implement PM system that covers all 28 BaKuu Holding employees."
T
Time-bound
Specific deadline or milestones. Not "this year" — a concrete date or review point.
Bad: "Complete HR audit eventually." Good: "Complete HR compliance audit by 31 March 2026."

Goal-Setting Rules at BaKuu Holding
Non-negotiable standards for all managers
3–5 goals maximum. More than 5 goals means no priorities. Fewer than 3 means insufficient accountability. The sweet spot is 4.
Weights must add to 100%. If a manager sets 4 goals and weights are 25/25/25/25 — fine. If weights add to 90% — incomplete. HR reviews all goal sheets before cycle is locked.
At least one development goal. Every employee's goal set must include at least one goal related to their own development — not just deliverables.
Safety always weighted ≥20% for ops roles. No site or drilling role may have a goal set where HSE/safety accounts for less than 20% of the total review score.
Goals signed by both parties. A goal is not a goal until the employee has signed the Goal Agreement Form. Manager cannot score against unsigned goals.
Goals may be updated mid-cycle. If business context changes significantly (new project, role change, site disruption), goals may be amended with HR approval. Both parties re-sign.

Goal weighting framework · how to allocate weight by role
%
Weighting Principles
How to assign weight — the reasoning behind the numbers
Weight = Importance to the business, not difficulty.A goal weighted at 25% should be the most critical outcome for that role this year — not necessarily the hardest to achieve.
No single goal above 30%.If one goal carries more than 30% weight, it dominates the review. An employee who achieves everything else but misses one major goal can fail the review unfairly.
Development goals should carry 15–25% weight.If development goals are weighted at 5%, the message is that development does not matter. Weight communicates priority.
Weights must be agreed at the start, not assigned at review.Assigning weights after the fact is unfair. The employee must know what counts most before the year begins.
◈
Scoring Calculation
How the final review score is calculated
FORMULA
Final Score = Σ (Goal Rating × Goal Weight)
Sum of each goal's score (1–5) multiplied by its weight
Example — Site Manager
Safety — Zero LTI 25% Rating 5 = 1.25
Drilling Output 20% Rating 4 = 0.80
Crew Retention 15% Rating 3 = 0.45
Permit Compliance 15% Rating 5 = 0.75
Development 25% Rating 4 = 1.00
Final Score 100% 4.25
Score 4.25 = Strong rating. Salary review: eligible for merit increase.

Manager Responsibilities
What every people manager must do in the PM cycle
✓ Set goals with each direct report by 31 Jan — not for them, with them
✓ Conduct quarterly informal check-ins — brief, documented in a line or two
✓ Raise performance concerns in writing before mid-year — not for the first time at annual
✓ Complete mid-year and annual reviews on time — late submissions are escalated to COO
✓ Calibrate ratings with HR before finalising — no rating is issued without HR review
⚑
Common PM Failures to Avoid
Saving all feedback for the annual review — then surprising the employee with a 2 rating
Rating everyone a 3 to avoid difficult conversations — "grade inflation" destroys system credibility
Setting goals so vague they cannot be measured — "improve team communication"
Giving a rating without evidence — manager must bring documented examples to calibration
