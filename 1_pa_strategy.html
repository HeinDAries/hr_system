People Analytics · Tool 1 / 9 — Analytics Strategy & Framework

Why Analytics
at BaKuu Holding

People analytics at BaKuu Holding is not about building a data science function. It is about answering the questions the COO cannot currently answer — and answering them before the consequence arrives. At 42 people today and a growth path to 1,000, the analytics infrastructure built now determines whether HR leads decisions or reacts to them.

The Analytics Imperative
People cost is 43% of revenue. The COO is making $3.7M+ people decisions without a data system.
Every retention decision, every promotion, every sourcing call at BaKuu Holding is a financial decision. When Myo H.'s retention bonus expires, the COO needs to know what it costs if he leaves — not guess. When Indonesia's programme delivery drops from 93% to 84%, the COO needs to trace that to a G3 headcount gap within days, not quarters. When BaKuu Holding plans Country 5, the COO needs a workforce model that shows the net supply deficit against the growth plan. People analytics is the infrastructure that makes those answers available when the decisions are being made — not after.
Three Analytics Purposes at BaKuu Holding
PURPOSE 1 — DESCRIBE
What is the state of the BaKuu Holding workforce right now?
Headcount by grade and country. Attrition rates. Pipeline conversion. Programme delivery. Grade band adherence. The descriptive layer answers the COO's "where are we?" question — and it is the foundation every other analytics layer depends on. At BaKuu Holding today, this layer is mostly absent. Building it is the first priority.
PURPOSE 2 — DIAGNOSE
Why is this happening — and what is driving it?
Diagnostic analytics connects symptoms to causes. Indonesia delivery dropped — diagnostic analytics traces it to a G3 vacancy, which traces to a G2 promotion gap, which traces to a G4 coaching quality issue. Without this layer, HR fixes symptoms. With it, HR fixes root causes. At BaKuu Holding's scale, the diagnostic layer is achievable with good data collection — no advanced tools required.
PURPOSE 3 — PREDICT
What will happen if we do — or do not — act?
Predictive analytics at BaKuu Holding does not require machine learning. It requires trend extrapolation: if the current G1 conversion rate holds, how many G4s will BaKuu Holding have in 24 months? If the Laos G4 retirement cliff hits without successors, what is the delivery impact? Scenario modelling is BaKuu Holding's version of predictive analytics — and it is the layer that earns Group HR a seat at business planning.
What BaKuu Holding Analytics Is Not
Clarity on scope prevents the analytics function from being either over-engineered or dismissed as irrelevant to a small company.

The BaKuu Holding Analytics Journey — Now to 1,000 Staff
The analytics infrastructure built for 42 people must be designed to scale. Every data definition, every metric, every reporting structure should be built as if BaKuu Holding will have 1,000 staff — because the cost of rebuilding a broken data architecture at scale is far higher than building it right at small scale.

Section 02 — The Priority Questions

The Priority
Questions

Analytics starts with questions, not data. This section defines the twelve priority questions BaKuu Holding needs people analytics to answer — ranked by business urgency and data availability. Every analytics investment is justified by its connection to one of these questions.

"If you cannot name the decision the data will inform, you do not have an analytics use case. You have a data collection project."

BaKuu Holding People Analytics Principle · 2026
Tier 1 — Answer Now
Tier 2 — Build Capability
Tier 3 — Scale Ready
Tier 1 questions — answerable with data that exists or can be collected within 90 days.These are the questions the COO is implicitly asking every quarter. Group HR's credibility depends on being able to answer them with data rather than estimates.
1
PIPELINE HEALTH · TIER 1
What is the current G1→G4 conversion rate by country, and is it improving or deteriorating?
The single most important people metric at BaKuu Holding. Drives operational capacity, revenue delivery, and every workforce planning decision. Currently known informally — needs to be tracked formally with a quarterly trend line. Data needed: cohort start dates, G2/G3/G4 completion dates, exits by month.
2
ATTRITION RISK · TIER 1
Which G1 trainees are most likely to exit before G2 — and can Group HR identify them before month 3?
Myanmar's 58% conversion rate means approximately 3–4 trainees per cohort are exiting before G2. The month-3 check-in is the intervention window — but only if at-risk trainees are identified in advance. Data needed: check-in records, milestone completion velocity, G4 debrief frequency as leading indicator.
3
COST OF ATTRITION · TIER 1
What does it cost BaKuu Holding each time a trained driller exits — and what is the total annual attrition cost?
Replacement cost is estimated at $1,000–$2,000 per driller. Production downtime cost is unknown. The full cost — recruitment, onboarding, lost productivity, downtime — is the number that makes the COO take retention seriously. Data needed: recruitment cost per hire from finance, time-to-fill by grade, production value per driller per week.
4
HEADCOUNT SUPPLY/DEMAND · TIER 1
Is BaKuu Holding on track to meet its 65 FTE target by Q2 2027 — and where is the net supply deficit?
Current headcount is approximately 42 active. Growth to 65 FTE by Q2 2027 requires a net addition of 23 FTE over 6 quarters — approximately 4 FTE per quarter. Against current attrition and conversion rates, is the pipeline producing enough? Data needed: pipeline log, current attrition rate, planned cohort intake schedule.
5
PEOPLE COST · TIER 1
Is people cost as a percentage of revenue stable, increasing, or decreasing — and what is driving the movement?
People cost runs at approximately 43% of revenue. The COO needs to know whether this ratio is structurally stable or drifting. The driver decomposition — grade mix, country mix, overtime, external hire cost — tells Group HR where to focus cost management attention. Data needed: payroll by grade/country, total revenue (from finance), headcount timeline.
Tier 2 questions — answerable within 6–12 months as the data infrastructure is built.These questions require data that does not yet exist in a usable form. Building the answer to these questions is the 2026–2027 analytics build priority.
6
COMPENSATION EQUITY · TIER 2
Are there employees paid below the grade minimum — and is pay distribution within bands equitable across countries?
Zaw M. (Myanmar G3) is flagged below minimum — action required April 2026. This is a symptom of a broader question: are there other below-minimum cases? Is the country factor adjustment producing equitable outcomes? Data needed: full salary data by grade/country, grade band minimum/maximum, country factors applied.
7
G4 COACHING IMPACT · TIER 2
Is the G4 coaching programme producing measurable improvement in trainee conversion rates — and which coaching behaviours matter most?
The coaching programme's ROI depends on being able to show that conversion rates improved in the 6–12 months after G4 certification. The granular question — which coaching behaviour (debrief frequency, check-in quality, milestone rigour) correlates most strongly with conversion — requires 12 months of post-programme data. Data needed: pre/post rubric scores, debrief frequency by G4, conversion rates by G4's cohort.
8
SM RETENTION RISK · TIER 2
What is the probability of Myo H. and Wirawan S. exiting in 2027 — and what is the financial impact if they do?
Both retention bonuses expire in 2027. The exit risk is not theoretical — it is a named, dated event. Quantifying the financial impact (recruitment cost, onboarding, programme delivery disruption, G4 pipeline impact in their country) makes the retention investment case concrete. Data needed: replacement cost model for SM grade, programme delivery impact per SM vacancy month, G4 pipeline dependency on each SM.
9
GRADE PROGRESSION VELOCITY · TIER 2
How long does it actually take to progress from G1 to G4 at BaKuu Holding — and where are the bottlenecks?
The designed pathway is 18–24 months G1→G2, 12–18 months G2→G3, 18–24 months G3→G4. Is this what is actually happening? If actual time-in-grade is longer, the pipeline is slower than planned and the 65 FTE target is at risk. If faster, there may be milestone quality concerns. Data needed: grade entry dates for all current and historical employees, milestone completion dates.
Tier 3 questions — relevant as BaKuu Holding scales toward 150–1,000 staff.These questions are not answerable today — the data does not exist and the decision complexity is not yet present. But the data architecture built now should be designed to eventually answer them.
10
PREDICTIVE ATTRITION · TIER 3
Which employees are at highest flight risk in the next 6 months — before they signal intent to leave?
Predictive attrition modelling requires 2–3 years of historical attrition data with behavioural and operational variables attached. At current scale this is not achievable — but the data fields needed (grade, time-in-grade, check-in risk flag, manager quality score, salary position in band) should be built into the data infrastructure now so the model can be trained when the data volume exists.
11
WORKFORCE COMPOSITION OPTIMISATION · TIER 3
What is the optimal grade mix and country mix for BaKuu Holding at 500 staff — and what does the transition path look like?
At scale, the ratio of G1–G2 trainees to G3–G4 senior drillers, and the distribution across countries, becomes a strategic optimisation question. Too heavy at junior grades means high training cost and delivery risk. Too heavy at senior grades means high salary cost and succession fragility. The workforce planning model (Tool 6) builds this capability.
12
EXTERNAL LABOUR MARKET INTELLIGENCE · TIER 3
How does BaKuu Holding's total compensation compare to the market in each country — and are there emerging labour market risks?
At current scale, external benchmarking is done manually and infrequently. At 500+ staff across potentially 6–8 countries, systematic external market intelligence becomes essential for compensation strategy and talent sourcing. The data infrastructure and benchmarking methodology should be designed for this scale — even if it is not yet being used.
Section 03 — Data Maturity Model

Data Maturity
Model

The five-level data maturity model defines where BaKuu Holding is today, where it needs to be at each growth stage, and what it takes to move between levels. Maturity is not about technology — it is about discipline, consistency, and the quality of decisions the data enables.

BaKuu Holding today: Level 1 transitioning to Level 2.Most HR data exists in informal or fragmented forms — payroll has headcount, operations has delivery data, site sign-off cards have milestone records. The data exists. It is not integrated, not consistently defined, and not used for decisions. Moving to Level 2 is the 2026 priority — not building technology, but building discipline.
1
Reactive
Informal / fragmented
What it looks like
HR data exists in multiple disconnected places — payroll, site records, email, memory. No consistent definitions. Data extracted manually when needed. Decisions made on intuition and experience rather than data.
BaKuu Holding today
Pipeline data: informal site records. Attrition: known but not tracked formally. Compensation: payroll system exists but not analysed against bands. Delivery data: operations report — not connected to HR data.
2
Descriptive
Target: 2026
What it looks like
Key HR metrics are tracked consistently with agreed definitions. Data is collected regularly, consolidated by Group HR, and reported to the COO on a quarterly basis. Decisions are informed by data — though the data is backward-looking.
What BaKuu Holding needs to build
Pipeline log (in progress). Check-in tracker. Conversion rates by country (Q1 baseline). Attrition rate from payroll. People cost % from finance. Grade band adherence from salary data. Quarterly COO report integrating all sources.
3
Diagnostic
Target: 65–150 staff
What it looks like
HR can answer "why" questions, not just "what" questions. Root cause analysis of attrition, delivery shortfalls, and pipeline stalls. Data is integrated across HR and operational systems. An HRIS is in place. Analytics takes 2–4 hours per week of Group HR time.
Requirements
HRIS selected and implemented. Attrition root cause model. G4 coaching impact correlation analysis. Workforce planning model with scenario capability. Compensation equity analysis by grade and country. At least one analytics dashboard used weekly by Group HR.
4
Predictive
Target: 150–500 staff
What it looks like
HR uses historical patterns to forecast future workforce states. Attrition risk flags appear before employees signal intent to leave. Workforce demand forecasts are generated from operational plans. People analytics has a dedicated function — at minimum a part-time analyst supporting Group HR.
Requirements
3+ years of historical data. Attrition prediction model (logistic regression or equivalent). Pipeline supply forecast model. Talent demand forecast integrated with business planning. Group HR presents predictive insights at business planning meetings — not just historical reports.
5
Prescriptive
Target: 500–1,000 staff
What it looks like
HR analytics recommends specific actions — not just flags risks. The system suggests which candidates to promote, which compensation adjustments to make, which markets to source from. People analytics team of 2–4 professionals. CHRO presents analytics to the board. External benchmarking integrated.
Requirements
Advanced analytics platform (Workday, SAP, or equivalent). Dedicated analytics team. Machine learning models trained on 5+ years of BaKuu Holding data. Integration with external labour market data. Real-time dashboards for CHRO and COO. Board-level people analytics reporting.

Section 05 — Analytics Principles

Analytics
Principles

Eight principles that govern how people analytics is built and used at BaKuu Holding. These are not aspirational values — they are operating constraints that prevent the most common analytics failures at small-company scale.

1
Question first. Data second. Visualisation last.
Every analytics project starts with a decision that needs to be made. If Group HR cannot name the decision, the analytics project does not start. Data collection follows the question — not vice versa. Visualisation is chosen to communicate the answer clearly — not to demonstrate analytics sophistication.
2
One definition, everywhere.
Every metric has exactly one agreed definition — in the HR data dictionary (Tool 2). "Attrition" means the same thing in Myanmar and Indonesia. "Headcount" is counted on the same date each month. "Active employee" has a consistent definition across all four countries. Inconsistent definitions produce inconsistent data — and inconsistent data destroys COO trust in HR analytics faster than no data at all.
3
Acknowledge uncertainty. Never fabricate precision.
When data is incomplete, say so — and provide a range or estimate with the confidence level stated. "The attrition rate is approximately 22% based on 8 months of data — full-year figure will be available in Q4" is more credible than a false-precise figure. The COO's trust in analytics is built on honesty about data limitations, not on hiding them.
4
Analytics is a service to decision-makers — not a demonstration of HR sophistication.
The COO does not care how the analysis was conducted. The COO cares what it means and what to do. Every analytics output ends with: "What this means for the business: [one sentence]. Recommended action: [one sentence]." If Group HR cannot write those two sentences, the analysis is not finished.
5
Collect the minimum viable data — then add only what a decision requires.
Data collection has a cost — Group HR time, SM time, and G4 compliance. Every data point collected must earn its place by answering a Tier 1 or Tier 2 question. Do not collect data because it might be useful someday. Collect data because a specific question requires it. The data dictionary is the control mechanism — no new field without a named use case.
6
Build for scale from day one.
Every data definition, every report structure, every metric is designed as if BaKuu Holding has 1,000 staff. The pipeline log structure that works for 42 staff should work for 420. The grade band adherence analysis for 4 countries should work for 10. Building for scale means choosing data architectures and definitions that do not need to be rebuilt — even though the immediate cost is slightly higher.
7
Individual data is used for development, never for surveillance.
Operational analytics uses aggregate, grade-level, and cohort-level data. Individual-level data — coaching rubric scores, development plan progress, salary position — is used only in development conversations and compensation reviews, with the individual's awareness. Group HR does not produce rankings of employees by performance data without a development conversation context. Trust is the data collection infrastructure at 42 people.
8
Revenue language is the translation layer.
Every HR analytics finding that goes to the COO is expressed in revenue or cost terms — not just HR metric terms. "Conversion rate is 58%" is an HR metric. "At 58% conversion, BaKuu Holding is producing 4–5 G2 drillers per cohort instead of the 5–6 at target, at an additional replacement cost of $1,500 per lost trainee — approximately $4,500–$6,000 per cohort" is a business insight. The translation layer is always applied before the COO sees the analysis.

Section 07 — Scaling to 1,000 Staff

Scaling to
1,000 Staff

The analytics infrastructure being built for 42 staff must be designed to scale to 1,000. This section defines what changes at each growth stage — the data architecture decisions, the technology choices, the team structure, and the analytics capability that the CHRO will need to operate at scale.

Scale Decisions — What to Build Now vs Later
Some decisions are cheap to change at 42 staff and expensive at 500. These are the decisions to get right now.
DecisionGet right now (42 staff)Why it matters at scale
Grade structure naming conventionG1–G8 with consistent naming across all countries. No country-specific grade names that diverge from the group standard.At 1,000 staff across 10 countries, inconsistent grade naming makes cross-country analytics impossible. Retroactively standardising grade data at scale is a major project.

Assigned at hire, never reused.At scale, name-based employee identification creates duplicate and merge errors. Documented in the HR data dictionary.At scale, different teams calculating attrition differently produces contradictory figures that undermine analytics credibility. HRIS Selection — When and What
BaKuu Holding does not currently have an HRIS. The right time to select one is when the data architecture (Tool 2) is complete and the analytics requirements are clear — not before. Selecting an HRIS without a defined data architecture means the vendor's data model drives BaKuu Holding's HR decisions, rather than BaKuu Holding's decisions driving the HRIS configuration.
When to select: When BaKuu Holding reaches 65–80 staff and the data architecture (Tool 2) is complete. The HRIS should be selected against the data dictionary — not before it exists.
What to require at 65–150 staff: Multi-country support (4 countries minimum). Grade structure configuration. Basic reporting. Payroll integration. Mobile access for site SMs. Cloud-hosted. Cost: $10–$30 per employee per month at this scale.
What to require at 150–500 staff: Analytics module. Workforce planning capability. Performance management integration. Learning management. Succession planning. Cost: $30–$60 per employee per month.
What to require at 500–1,000 staff: Advanced analytics. Predictive attrition. Compensation benchmarking integration. Board reporting capability. API integration with payroll, finance, and operational systems. Cost: Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, or Oracle HCM tier.
