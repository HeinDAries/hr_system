L&D Tool 2 / 7 — Training Needs Analysis

What is a
TNA?

A Training Needs Analysis is the structured process of identifying the gap between the capability an organisation needs and the capability it currently has — then translating that gap into a prioritised, costed set of learning interventions. At BaKuu Holding, the TNA is the mechanism that stops L&D being driven by whoever asked last.

"The TNA is not a survey asking people what training they want. It is a diagnostic asking what capability the business needs — and identifying the gap between that requirement and what currently exists."

BaKuu Holding L&D Framework · 2026
TNA vs Training Request
Training Request
— Initiated by the employee or SM
— Based on perceived need or interest
— Reactive to a specific event or complaint
— No link to business strategy required
— Approved or denied based on budget availability
— No outcome measurement expected
TNA-Driven L&D
+ Initiated by Group HR from business data
+ Based on measured capability gap
+ Proactive — before the gap causes harm
+ Directly linked to pipeline or strategy
+ Investment sized against business impact
+ Outcome measured against defined success criteria
BaKuu Holding today: Currently operating on training requests. The Myanmar coaching programme was identified reactively — after conversion rate data revealed the gap. The TNA process formalises this so the gap is identified before the damage accumulates, not after.
The Three Levels of TNA
Level 1 — Organisational TNA
What capability does BaKuu Holding as a whole need to execute its strategy? Driven by the business plan — 65 FTE by Q2 2027, Country 5 entry, 93%+ programme delivery across all countries. This level identifies the aggregate capability gap and drives the L&D investment priorities.
Level 2 — Functional / Country TNA
What capability does each country need that it currently lacks? Myanmar G4 coaching gap. Indonesia G3 technical gap from vacancy. Laos G3 development gap ahead of retirement cohort. Thailand practice documentation gap. Each country has a different profile.
Level 3 — Individual TNA
What specific development does this individual need to reach the next milestone? Activated at the G3→G4 transition (the hardest and most consequential) and for G5 succession candidates. The G4 performance review and the quarterly talent review are the primary inputs to this level.

Section 02 — The TNA Process

The TNA
Process

The TNA process at BaKuu Holding follows a five-step sequence — from defining the business requirement through to communicating the output. Each step has a defined owner, a method, and a deliverable. The process runs annually as part of the HR planning cycle, with a mid-year update triggered by any significant pipeline event.

1
Define
Define the Business Requirement
Start with what the business needs to achieve — not with what training is available. At BaKuu Holding: 65 FTE by Q2 2027, 93%+ programme delivery across all countries, Country 5 entry capability, G4 pipeline sufficient to cover 3 Laos retirements. This is the business requirement that the TNA tests capability against.
Owner: Group HR (Ba) Input: business plan, workforce plan (Tool 6), scenario model (OD Tool 8) Output: capability requirement statement by grade and country
2
Assess
Assess Current Capability
Map the current capability state at each grade and country against the requirement. This is not a self-assessment survey — it draws on pipeline data (conversion rates, milestone completions, attrition patterns), performance data (G4 review outcomes, SM observations), and operational data (programme delivery %, vacancy duration). The SM is a key data source, not the decision-maker on what the gaps are.
Owner: Group HR Input: monthly HR data, performance reviews, pipeline metrics, SM observations Output: current capability map by grade and country
3
Gap
Identify and Size the Gaps
The gap is the difference between Step 1 (what the business needs) and Step 2 (what currently exists). Size each gap in two dimensions: severity (how large is the shortfall?) and urgency (how soon does the gap cause business harm?). This is the core analytical output of the TNA. A gap that is severe but not urgent requires a different intervention than a gap that is moderate but immediate.
Owner: Group HR Output: gap register with severity and urgency ratings — the BaKuu Holding gap matrix (Section 04)
4
Design
Design the Intervention
For each prioritised gap, select and design the most appropriate intervention — applying the 70/20/10 model and the build/buy/borrow decision framework from Tool 1. Not every gap requires a training programme. Some require a process change (month-3 check-in formalisation), a role design change (G4 decision rights), or a structural intervention (cross-country exposure for Laos G3 candidates). Training is one option, not the default.
Owner: Group HR Input: gap register, 70/20/10 model, build/buy matrix Output: intervention plan with method, timeline, cost, and success measure
5
Communicate
Communicate the TNA Output
The TNA output must be communicated in two directions: upward to the COO (business case for L&D investment — what are the gaps, what will it cost to close them, what is the cost of not closing them) and laterally to SMs (what is expected of them in delivering the 70% and 20% components, and what Group HR will deliver in the 10% component). The COO presentation uses the gap-to-risk framing: here is what we will lose if we do not act.
Owner: Group HR Upward: COO business case with gap-to-risk quantification Lateral: SM briefing on their role in the intervention plan

Section 03 — Data Sources & Methods

Data Sources
& Methods

The quality of a TNA is only as good as the data it draws on. At BaKuu Holding, the primary data sources are operational — pipeline metrics, performance reviews, attrition patterns. The TNA does not rely on self-reported training needs, which are systematically biased toward comfortable requests rather than actual capability gaps.

BaKuu Holding data advantageBaKuu Holding is small enough that Group HR can speak directly with every SM and review every G4's performance data. This is a genuine advantage — large organisations run TNAs on surveys because they cannot access people directly. At 42 active employees across 4 countries, Ba can conduct the TNA through structured conversations and data review, not a tick-box questionnaire.
Primary Data Sources
Pipeline metrics
Highest reliability
Conversion rate by country and cohort. Time-to-milestone at each grade transition. Month-3 attrition rate. G3 vacancy duration. Programme delivery % vs target. These are the most objective indicators of capability gaps — they show the output of the training system, regardless of what inputs were delivered. Myanmar's 58% conversion rate is the most important single data point in the current TNA.
Performance review data
High reliability
G4 performance ratings — particularly the coaching and development quality dimensions. SM assessments of their G3 candidates' readiness. Development conversation quality scores. These reveal where individual capability gaps exist that aggregate pipeline data might obscure — a country with good conversion rates might still have one G4 who is a weak coach, creating a fragile pipeline dependent on only the strong G4s.
SM structured interview
Medium-high reliability
Annual structured interview with each SM using a consistent question set. Focuses on: what capability is your team missing that is affecting delivery? What do your G4s struggle with most? What is preventing your G3s from progressing? The interview supplements pipeline data with SM-level intelligence — but is weighted lower than outcome data because SMs often identify symptoms rather than root causes. Indonesia SM may cite "G3 availability" when the root cause is lack of an accelerated development pipeline for G2 candidates.
Trainee exit data
Medium reliability
Exit interview data from departing trainees and G1–G3 employees. Particularly valuable for understanding the month-3 attrition pattern in Myanmar. Data is self-reported and subject to social desirability bias — trainees often cite "better opportunity" when the actual reason is lack of investment in their development. Cross-reference with timing (month of departure) and G4 assignment to identify coaching quality as a driver.
Operational data
Contextual
Programme delivery %, HSE incident rates, client feedback, equipment utilisation. Not direct capability measures but useful for contextualising capability gaps. A country with declining programme delivery and increasing HSE incidents may have a capability gap in site management, not just technical drilling — pointing to an SM-level development need rather than a G3 technical need.

Data Quality Rules
Rule 1 — Outcome data beats perception data. If pipeline metrics say conversion rate is 58%, and the Myanmar SM says "our G4s are good coaches," the pipeline data wins. Look for what is causing the discrepancy in the SM's perception.
Rule 2 — Triangulate before concluding. No single data source is definitive. A spike in Myanmar attrition could be a coaching gap, a compensation gap, a working conditions issue, or a local labour market shift. Cross-reference before designing an L&D intervention for what might be a C&B problem.
Rule 3 — Disaggregate by country and G4. A group-level conversion rate masks country variation. A country-level conversion rate masks G4-level variation. Get to the lowest level of disaggregation the data supports — the gap is almost always more specific than it first appears.
Rule 4 — Training is the last hypothesis, not the first. Before concluding that a capability gap requires a training intervention, ask: is this a knowledge gap (training helps), a motivation gap (rewards/recognition), a process gap (system or tools), or a structural gap (wrong role design)? Each requires a different response.

Gap Type Analysis — Root Cause Before Intervention
Before designing any intervention, the root cause type determines the response. Training is appropriate for knowledge and ability gaps. Process, motivation, and structural gaps require different solutions — and applying training to them wastes budget and time.
Knowledge + Ability gap → Training appropriate
The person does not know how to do the thing, or cannot yet execute it to the required standard. Training, coaching, and practice are the right responses. G4 coaching skills, G3 technical drilling capability, SM financial literacy.
Process gap → System or tool design, not training
The person knows what to do but the system does not support them doing it. Month-3 check-in is informal not because G4s lack knowledge — but because no documented process exists. Fix the process, not the person. Month-3 check-in, G3→G4 development pathway, practice transfer mechanism.
Motivation gap → Rewards or recognition design
The person knows how and the system supports them — but they are not motivated to do it. If G4 coaching quality is low because G4s are not rewarded for trainee development outcomes, training them in coaching skills will not solve it. Fix the incentive. Weak total rewards was identified as a root cause of trainee attrition — C&B department toolkit addresses this separately.
Structural gap → Role or org design change
The capability gap exists because the role is not designed correctly — decision rights are absent, reporting lines create confusion, or the role combines incompatible responsibilities. G4 ghost accountability on trainee conversion is a structural gap, not a training gap. Fix the role design (OD Tool 3). G4 decision rights, SM authority boundaries.
