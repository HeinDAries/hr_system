STAR Method — Required for All Answers
Every competency question must be answered using STAR format
S
Situation
What was the context or challenge? When, where, what role?
T
Task
What was their specific responsibility? What were they accountable for?
A
Action
What did THEY specifically do? Not the team — the individual.
R
Result
What was the measurable outcome? Numbers, timeframes, impact.
⚑
Interviewer Ground Rules
Non-negotiable standards for every interview
✓ Ask the same core questions to every candidate for the same role — no improvising
✓ Score DURING the interview — do not rely on memory after
✓ Never share your score or opinion with other interviewers before independent scoring
✓ Use probe questions if an answer is vague — push for specifics
✗ Do not ask about age, marital status, religion, or family plans — illegal in most jurisdictions
✗ Do not make hiring decisions based on "gut feel" alone — score must support decision
✗ Do not discuss salary in round 1 — this is HR's role and comes only after shortlisting
▸
Universal Opening & Closing Questions
Use for every role — adapt wording to context
Opening (after candidate overview)
"What specifically attracted you to this role and to BaKuu Holding — not a generic answer, what about this opportunity specifically?"
"Walk me through a career decision you made that didn't work out as planned and what you learned from it."
"What does working in a remote SEA site mean to you practically — what have you done to prepare for or adapt to that environment?"
Closing (before candidate Q&A)
"Is there anything about your experience or background that you haven't had the chance to share today that you think is relevant?"
"What is your notice period and are there any constraints on your availability to start?"
"On a scale of 1–10, how interested are you in this role right now — and what would make it a 10?"
The interview flow is a minimum standard — not a ceiling. For senior roles (Site Manager, Drilling Supervisor), add a second round technical assessment or case study. For all roles, a reference check is mandatory before offer — see reference check framework (pending build).
When to use panel vs solo interview · and how to run each
Solo Interview
1 interviewer · HR or Hiring Manager
Best for:
Initial screening. Support / admin roles. Lower-risk hires. Exploratory pre-screen calls.
Risks:
Single perspective. Higher bias risk. Not defensible for senior hires.
Use for Round 1 screening only
2-Person Panel
HR + Hiring Manager
Best for:
Most standard hires. Operational roles (Driller, Supervisor). Balances HR and technical perspective. Manageable coordination.
Risks:
Interviewer groupthink if both are aligned before interview. Need clear role split.
Recommended for most roles
3-Person Panel
HR + Hiring Mgr + COO/CEO
Best for:
Senior hires only (Site Manager, Country HR Manager). Roles reporting directly to C-suite. When culture fit is a critical factor.
Risks:
Intimidating for candidate. Scheduling complexity. CEO/COO influence can dominate scoring.
Senior roles only · Round 2
◉
Role Split in a 2-Person Panel
Clear ownership prevents duplication and gaps
HR Interviewer Owns:
• Opening, structure, and closing
• Behavioral / competency questions
• Culture and values fit assessment
• Red flag monitoring (personal conduct, stability)
• Logistics: availability, notice period, location
• Communication and documentation of next steps
Hiring Manager Owns:
• Technical competency questions
• Role-specific scenario / problem solving
• Equipment or process knowledge assessment
• Team dynamics and site environment fit
• Operational expectations and deliverables
• Technical red flags (credibility of experience claims)
Never brief the Hiring Manager on your own scoring or impressions before they have independently scored. Discuss and compare AFTER both parties have completed their individual scorecards. This is the most important anti-bias practice in structured interviewing.
Site Manager · competency-based question bank
Site Operations Leadership
STAR
Tell me about the largest workforce you have personally managed on a single site. What was your structure, what were the biggest people challenges, and how did you address them?
Look for: Specific headcount numbers (we need 300+). Clear org structure described. Concrete people challenges with actions taken. Evidence of proactive management, not just reactive.
PROBE
You mentioned [X headcount] — who were your direct reports and how did you cascade accountability below you?
Look for: Clear delegation structure. Understands difference between managing and micromanaging. Can describe how they held people accountable without being on top of everything.
HSE Leadership
STAR
Describe a serious safety incident or near-miss that occurred on your site. Walk me through your immediate response, the investigation process, and what systemic changes you made afterward.
Look for: Calm, methodical response. Uses structured investigation (5-Why, root cause). Takes accountability — doesn't blame subordinates. Demonstrates systemic fix, not just individual reprimand. Most importantly: what changed after?
HYPO
You arrive on site and observe a driller operating without required PPE. This is not the first time. The driller is your most experienced and productive operator. What do you do?
Look for: Zero tolerance framing but pragmatic approach. Addresses it immediately, not later. Follows process. Does NOT make an exception for productivity. Understands this is a culture signal to the whole site.
Decision Making Under Pressure
STAR
Tell me about a time operations on your site were at serious risk — equipment failure, supply crisis, or personnel emergency. What was the situation and how did you manage it?
Look for: Clear-headed under pressure. Prioritizes correctly (people safety first, then ops continuity). Can make decisions without full information. Communicates up the chain while managing the situation. Quantifies the impact and outcome.
Stakeholder & Government Relations
STAR
Describe your experience managing relationships with government authorities or regulatory bodies at a site level. What was the most challenging interaction and how did you handle it?
Look for: Has direct experience — not "my team handled it." Understands local government relationship dynamics in SEA or similar environment. Diplomatic but firm. Does not describe avoiding or ignoring regulatory contact.
PROBE
What do you know about operating in Laos specifically — the regulatory environment, local labor practices, and cultural norms?
Look for: Genuine research or experience. If no Laos experience, do they acknowledge the gap and show how they would close it? Red flag: "It's similar to everywhere else I've worked."
Budget & Cost Control
STAR
Tell me about the largest operational budget you have personally managed. How did you track spend, identify variances, and report to leadership?
Look for: Owns budget accountability — not just "I had visibility." Specific dollar amounts. Can describe variance analysis process. Understands cost drivers in drilling operations. Has escalated budget issues proactively.
Site Manager is a Tier 1 critical role — use all 8 questions across two rounds. Round 1 (HR): competency and culture questions. Round 2 (HR + COO): technical depth, budget, and government relations. Reference checks mandatory for this role before any offer.
Drilling Supervisor · competency-based question bank
Drilling Operations Expertise
STAR
Walk me through the most technically complex drilling program you have supervised. What were the geological or operational challenges and how did you manage them?
Look for: Specific technical knowledge — drill parameters, formation challenges, deviation control. Understands the program vs execution gap. Can articulate what made it complex and what they changed.
HYPO
You're 200 metres into a hole and you suddenly lose circulation — mud is not returning. You cannot reach the Geologist. What do you do in the next 30 minutes?
Look for: Immediate steps: stop drilling, assess, document depth and parameters. Does not just keep going. Follows emergency protocol. Knows when to make the call to pull out vs attempt remediation. Communicates up immediately.
HSE on the Rig Floor
STAR
Tell me about a time a crew member refused to follow a safety instruction. What was the situation and how did you handle it?
Look for: Does not ignore it or handle it "later." Acts immediately and consistently. Understands the cultural dynamics of crew authority. Does not use physical intimidation (red flag). Documents the incident properly.
Crew Leadership
STAR
Describe a time you had a serious performance problem with a crew member on a remote site. What steps did you take?
Look for: Follows process — verbal warning, documentation, escalation path. Does not handle it through intimidation. Understands the isolation challenge of remote site discipline. Involves HR/Site Manager at right point.
PROBE
How do you manage a mixed crew of local nationals and expats where language and cultural differences create friction?
Look for: Specific strategies — clear visual SOPs, buddy systems, daily briefings, translators. Has actually done this. Does not dismiss it as "not a real issue." Understanding of cultural hierarchy on drilling sites in SEA.
Equipment & Maintenance
STAR
Tell me about a major equipment breakdown you experienced during an active drilling program. How did you minimize downtime and what was the business impact?
Look for: Quantifies downtime and recovery. Has clear escalation path. Understands the cost of unplanned downtime. Proactively reviews maintenance after the event. Strong candidates will mention what they did differently going forward.
Drilling Supervisor is a CRITICAL OVERDUE role for Myanmar. Compress to a single combined round (HR + Ops Manager) to save time. Technical depth questions are more important than behavioral for this role. Verify certifications before interview.
Senior Driller · competency-based question bank
Rig Operation
STAR
Which rig types have you operated and what are the key operational differences between them? Which do you prefer and why?
Look for: Specific rig models and manufacturers. Can articulate technical differences. Preference should be backed by operational rationale, not just familiarity.
HYPO
You're on shift and you notice the drill string is vibrating abnormally and penetration rate has dropped significantly. Your Supervisor is not on site. What do you do?
Look for: Stops drilling. Checks parameters. Diagnoses possible cause (bit wear, formation change, balling). Does not just push through. Contacts Supervisor. Documents findings. Strong candidates will troubleshoot methodically before escalating.
Crew & Mentoring
STAR
Describe how you have developed a junior driller or helper into a competent operator. What was your approach?
Look for: Genuine investment in others' development. Uses show-don't-tell method. Patient but holds standards. This is a differentiator between a Senior Driller and someone who just does their own job.
Site Safety Ownership
STAR
Tell me about a time you identified a safety hazard on the rig before an incident occurred. What did you do?
Look for: Proactively identifies — not just responds to incidents. Reports it formally. Does not wait for Supervisor to notice. Follows up to ensure it was fixed. Best candidates will describe a hazard that was non-obvious.
Senior Driller can be assessed in a single 45-minute round with HR + Drilling Supervisor or Ops Manager. Focus 60% of time on technical competency. Verify certifications and check reference from most recent Drilling Supervisor before offer.
HSE Officer · competency-based question bank
HSE Management System
STAR
Tell me about a site where you built or significantly improved the HSE management system from scratch. What was the baseline, what did you do, and what changed?
Look for: Systematic approach — risk register, permit to work, inspection regime, training. Quantifies improvement (incident rate, near-miss reporting frequency). Understands the difference between a paper system and a live one.
PROBE
How would you approach HSE compliance in a country where the local regulatory standard is below your own company standard? Which standard do you apply?
Look for: Always applies the higher standard. Can articulate why (moral, legal liability, company reputation). Does not take the easy path. Understands how to educate and enforce simultaneously.
Incident Investigation
STAR
Walk me through an incident investigation you personally led. What methodology did you use and what was the most important finding?
Look for: Named methodology (5-Why, ICAM, Bowtie). Systematic process. Understands that root cause is almost never "operator error." Findings led to systemic change. Can distinguish between immediate cause and contributing factors.
Safety Culture Influence
HYPO
You arrive at a new site and discover that near-miss reporting is almost zero — not because nothing is happening, but because workers fear punishment for reporting. What is your first 30 days approach?
Look for: Does not mandate reporting from day one. Builds trust first. Separates reporting from blame. Uses positive reinforcement for reporters. Works with Site Manager to create visible leadership commitment. Understands this is cultural, not procedural.
STAR
Describe a time you had to challenge a senior manager or Site Manager on a safety decision. How did you handle it and what was the outcome?
Look for: Does it — does not back down when safety is at stake. Uses data and standards, not emotion. Documents the challenge formally. Escalates if necessary. Red flag: never has had to do this or avoids conflict at all costs.
HSE Officer must have NEBOSH or equivalent verified before interview. Ask to see certificates. This role has functional authority over everyone on site on safety matters — assess their confidence and assertiveness in behavioral questions, not just knowledge.
HRBP / HR Manager · competency-based question bank
HR Generalist Depth
STAR
Tell me about a time you had to manage a complex employee relations case — disciplinary, grievance, or termination — from beginning to resolution. Walk me through your process.
Look for: Follows process rigorously. Documents every step. Consults labor law. Manages the business stakeholder alongside the employee. Reaches a defensible conclusion. Does not cut corners to resolve quickly.
PROBE
In that case — what was the most difficult part of the process and where did you feel most uncertain?
Look for: Self-awareness. Knows their limits. Sought advice when uncertain. Red flag: "I knew exactly what to do throughout" — HR cases are almost never that clean.
Thai Labor Law
HYPO
A line manager wants to terminate an employee who has been with the company for 3 years for "poor performance" — but there are no documented performance reviews. What do you advise?
Look for: Flags the legal risk immediately (Thailand LPA severance exposure). Does not just comply with the manager. Advises on a proper PIP and documentation path going forward. Understands the company's liability. Strong candidates will cite specific LPA provisions.
Working in a Lean HR Function
STAR
Describe a time you were the only HR person for a large or complex operation. What did you prioritize, what did you let go, and how did you manage your own time?
Look for: Can triage effectively. Knows what has legal or operational urgency vs what is nice-to-have. Does not try to do everything perfectly. Communicates workload limitations upward. This is critical for BaKuu Holding context — solo HR in a multi-country operation.
HYPO
In your first 90 days here, you identify that payroll compliance in two countries has serious gaps. You also have three urgent hires to fill. You cannot do both simultaneously. How do you decide what to do first?
Look for: Compliance first — legal exposure outweighs recruitment delay. Communicates the triage to leadership clearly. Seeks to get temporary support for the deprioritized workstream. Does not just silently do one and ignore the other.
Stakeholder Management
STAR
Tell me about a time a senior manager or business leader pushed back hard on your HR recommendation. How did you handle it?
Look for: Holds their position when backed by policy/law. Listens and adjusts when the manager has a valid point. Does not simply capitulate under pressure. Documents disagreements. Red flag: "I always deferred to the business" — that is compliance, not partnership.
HRBP is a medium-priority role but critical for reducing Group HR Manager workload. Assess self-direction heavily — this person needs to operate with minimal supervision in Bangkok while you are managing SEA operations. Solo HR experience is non-negotiable.
Support & Admin roles · universal question bank
Organisation & Reliability
STAR
Tell me about a time you were managing multiple urgent tasks simultaneously and something fell through the cracks. What happened and what did you do?
Look for: Owns the mistake. Does not blame workload volume. Describes a specific recovery action. Most importantly — describes what system or habit they changed afterward.
Remote / Site Environment Fit
HYPO
This role is based on a remote drilling site — limited internet, no city amenities, mostly male workforce. What's your honest reaction to that and what would be your biggest personal adjustment?
Look for: Has thought about it. Is honest about the adjustment. Red flag: "That's no problem for me" with zero reflection — they haven't actually thought about it. Green flag: specific coping mechanisms, prior remote experience, practical questions about facilities.
Communication & Discretion
STAR
Tell me about a time you handled sensitive or confidential information. How did you manage it and was there ever a moment where you were tempted or pressured to share it?
Look for: Takes confidentiality seriously. Has a clear principle. Was not swayed by social pressure to share. For EA/Office Manager roles especially — discretion failure in this role directly affects C-suite operations.
STAR
Describe the most complex logistics or coordination challenge you've managed. How did you keep track of everything and what happened when something went wrong mid-process?
Look for: Uses systems (checklists, trackers) — not just memory. Proactive communication when issues arise. Does not wait to be asked for an update. Stays calm when plans change.
Support roles can be assessed in a single 30–40 minute interview with HR only. Use a practical task assessment where possible — e.g. ask an Admin candidate to demonstrate how they would set up a filing system or tracking sheet. Behavior in practical tasks is more predictive than answers alone.

Anti-Bias Reminders
Apply before scoring debrief
Halo Effect: Don't let one impressive answer inflate all scores. Score each competency independently.
Similarity Bias: Don't score higher because a candidate reminds you of yourself or a previous top performer.
Recency Bias: Don't let the last answer dominate your overall impression. Weight all answers equally.
Seniority Deference: In panel interviews, score independently BEFORE discussing with COO/CEO. Their seniority should not override your professional assessment.
Green flags · strong positive indicators
✓ Quantifies everything
Gives specific numbers — headcount managed, budget size, incident rates, timeframes. Doesn't need to be asked — volunteers data naturally.
✓ Owns failures openly
Can describe a genuine failure without deflecting blame. Articulates what they learned and changed. Self-awareness is a strong predictor of performance.
✓ Asks sharp questions
Questions reveal how they think. Strong candidates ask about operational scope, team structure, and success metrics — not just salary and holidays.
✓ Researched BaKuu Holding
Knows what the company does, the operating countries, and has specific observations or questions about the context. Shows genuine interest, not desperation.
✓ Proactive communication
Acknowledged receipt of interview invite. Was on time. Followed up after. Behavior during the recruitment process predicts behavior on the job.
✓ Pushes back respectfully
Disagrees with an interviewer's framing or corrects a factual error politely. Shows confidence and integrity — important for operational leadership roles.
Red flags · disqualifying and concerning signals
🚩 Vague about headcount
Claims large workforce experience but cannot name the structure, org chart, or specific challenges. "Around 500 people" with no detail is a credibility concern.
Action: Probe hard. Ask for names of direct reports, org chart, reporting chain.
🚩 Always says "we"
Uses "we" for every achievement but "I" when describing failures. Individual contribution is invisible. May be overstating their personal role.
Action: "What specifically did YOU do in that situation — not the team?"
🚩 Badmouths past employers
Describes previous employers or managers very negatively without any self-reflection. Predicts the same behavior when they leave BaKuu Holding.
Action: Note it. Ask "What did you take from that experience?" — see if any self-awareness emerges.
🚩 Evasive on terminations
Inconsistencies in why they left previous roles. "Mutual agreement" that sounds like termination. Stories that don't add up on dates or reasons.
Action: Probe calmly. "Walk me through exactly how that transition happened." Reference check is critical here.
🚩 No questions at end
A serious candidate for a senior role with no questions means either they're not genuinely interested or they haven't thought deeply about the role.
Action: Flag for HR. Ask directly: "What's your biggest unknown about this role right now?"
🚩 Inflexible on location
For site-based roles, candidate shows reluctance about remote conditions, rotation schedule, or SEA environment but hasn't disclosed this clearly.
Action: Address directly. "This is genuinely remote. Tell me about your last remote posting." Do not proceed if genuine hesitation remains.
🚩 Safety culture dismissal
For any ops role — candidate describes safety requirements as "box-ticking" or hints that productivity sometimes has to come before safety procedures.
Action: Automatic disqualifier for Site Manager and HSE roles. Flag for all ops roles.
🚩 Salary as first question
Opens the interview with salary expectations before understanding the role scope. Not disqualifying alone but signals motivation may be purely financial.
Action: Note it. Observe whether engagement increases once role scope is described. Probe intrinsic motivation later.
Red flags are indicators, not automatic disqualifiers (except safety culture dismissal for ops roles). Weigh them against the overall evidence. One red flag with strong STAR answers is different from three red flags with weak answers. Document all observations — they are legally important if a hire goes wrong.
