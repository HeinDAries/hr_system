Organisation Design & Development — Tool 1 / 9

OD
Foundations

Organisation Design is the deliberate process of shaping how an organisation is structured, how work flows through it, and how decisions are made — so that it can achieve its strategy. It is not an HR housekeeping task. It is one of the most powerful levers a business has.

"Every organisation is perfectly designed to get the results it gets. If you want different results, you need a different design."

Arthur W. Jones — Organisation Design Principle
What Organisation Design Is
Organisation design is the intentional configuration of four interconnected elements — structure, process, people, and rewards — to enable strategy execution. Change any one element without considering the others and the system compensates in unpredictable ways.
Structure
How the organisation is divided — by function, geography, product, or customer. Who reports to whom. How many layers. How wide the spans.
Process
How work flows through the organisation. How decisions are made. How information moves. Which processes are standardised and which are locally flexible.
People
The capabilities, numbers, and types of people the structure requires. Job design. Role accountabilities. Skills and competency requirements by layer.
Rewards
How the organisation incentivises the behaviours the structure requires. Misaligned rewards sabotage well-designed structures — every time.
What Organisation Design Is Not
The most common OD mistake is confusing organisation design with one of its components. Each of the following is a consequence of organisation design — not organisation design itself.
Not OD
Drawing an org chart. An org chart is a snapshot of reporting lines. It captures one dimension of structure and none of process, people, or rewards. A well-designed org can have a confusing org chart. A badly designed org can have a clean one.
Not OD
A headcount exercise. Deciding how many people to hire or cut is a consequence of the design — not the design itself. Headcount decisions made without a design rationale almost always produce the wrong result.
Not OD
A restructure announcement. Announcing who now reports to whom is not the same as designing how the work will actually get done differently. Most restructures fail because they stop at the announcement.
Not OD
A job evaluation exercise. Grading roles is an input to design — and an output of it — but it is not the design process itself.
Organisation Design vs Organisation Development
These two terms are often used interchangeably — and they are related but distinct. Understanding the difference shapes how the HRBP intervenes.
Organisation Design
— Concerned with structure, roles, and reporting
— Answers: "How should we be organised?"
— Typically triggered by strategy change or growth
— Has a defined endpoint — the new design
— Primarily analytical and structural work
— Examples: restructure, new country entry, role redesign
vs
Organisation Development
— Concerned with capability, culture, and effectiveness
— Answers: "How well are we functioning as an organisation?"
— Triggered by performance gaps or cultural dysfunction
— An ongoing discipline — no fixed endpoint
— Primarily behavioural and relational work
— Examples: leadership development, culture change, team effectiveness
At BaKuu Holding: Both are needed simultaneously. The structure needs to be designed for 5-country growth (Organisation Design). The capability to lead that structure needs to be developed (Organisation Development). The HRBP holds both.
Section 02 — When OD is Needed

When OD
is Needed

Organisation design is not a scheduled activity — it is triggered by specific conditions. The HRBP who can recognise these triggers early, and initiate an OD conversation before the symptoms become acute, earns a qualitatively different level of strategic influence.

The HRBP as Early Warning SystemMost organisations begin an OD process after the pain becomes undeniable — when productivity has fallen, when the restructure is already decided, when the new country is already contracted. The HRBP who reads the early signals and raises the OD conversation six months earlier is the one who gets to shape the design, not react to it.
Strategy Change
When the business changes direction — new markets, new products, acquisition, divestiture — the existing structure was designed for the old strategy and will resist the new one.
BaKuu Holding signal: Country 5 entry announced
Significant Growth
Organisations that grow beyond certain headcount thresholds need structural redesign. What works at 30 people breaks at 60. The informal coordination mechanisms of a small team cannot scale.
BaKuu Holding signal: 42 → 65 FTE in 18 months
Performance Decline
When delivery consistently underperforms and the root cause is not individual capability but structural — unclear accountabilities, duplicated work, slow decisions, coordination failures.
BaKuu Holding signal: Indonesia programme at 84% — structural or capability?
Leadership Transition
A new leader — especially at CEO, COO, or Site Manager level — often requires a structural review. The design built around the previous leader's strengths may not fit the new one.
BaKuu Holding signal: Myo H. succession — Myanmar SM transition
Structural Dysfunction
Escalating conflict between teams, decisions that take too long, work that falls between roles, duplication of effort — these are structural symptoms. Leadership coaching will not fix a structural problem.
BaKuu Holding signal: cross-country resource allocation disputes
External Disruption
Regulatory change, market contraction, competitor entry, technology shift — external forces that make the current structure uncompetitive or non-compliant.
BaKuu Holding signal: operating country regulatory changes (Myanmar, Indonesia)

The organisations that manage this transition well are the ones that design for it before they hit it, not after.

Galbraith's Star Model
The Star Model is the most practically useful OD framework for the HRBP. It maps five interdependent design elements — Strategy, Structure, Processes, Rewards, and People — and shows that they must all be aligned. A misalignment in any element undermines the whole system.

Star Model — BaKuu Holding Alignment Check
Strategy ↔ Structure — Partial misalignment
Strategy requires 5-country expansion. Structure currently has COO managing 4 SMs directly. Adding a 5th breaks the span. Structure needs redesign before Country 5 entry, not after.
Structure ↔ People — Significant misalignment
The structure requires a Site Manager-calibre leader for each country. No succession cover exists for any SM role. The People element cannot support what the Structure requires.
People ↔ Rewards — Partial misalignment
G4 drillers who take on trainee mentoring responsibilities are not compensated differently from G4s who do not. The Rewards element does not reinforce the People development behaviour the strategy requires.
Strategy ↔ Processes — Watch
Decision-making processes at BaKuu Holding are largely informal. As the organisation grows, informal processes will slow down. A formal governance model (Tool 9 of this department) is needed before the 5th country adds complexity.
McKinsey 7-S Framework
The 7-S framework maps seven elements of an organisation — three "hard" (Strategy, Structure, Systems) and four "soft" (Shared Values, Skills, Style, Staff). All seven must be aligned for the organisation to perform effectively. The soft elements are often ignored in OD work — and are usually where the real problems live.
Hard Elements — Easier to Define
Strategy
The plan for competitive advantage. At BaKuu Holding: grow to 5 countries, build internal driller pipeline, protect margins.
Structure
Current: COO → 4 SMs → Drillers. Flat, country-based. Works at current scale; will strain at 65 FTE.
Systems
HR processes, financial reporting, operational planning. Currently mostly informal — a known vulnerability as scale increases.
Soft Elements — Where Change Actually Sticks
Shared Values
What BaKuu Holding actually stands for — safety, technical excellence, delivery. Currently implicit. Thailand site lives them; Indonesia site questions them in HR context.
Skills
Technical drilling capability is strong. Leadership, people development, and cross-country management capability are the gaps identified in the HRBP toolkit.
Style + Staff
Leadership style varies significantly by country — a risk when trying to establish consistent group standards. Staff calibre at the driller level is strong; leadership bench is thin.
Nadler-Tushman Congruence Model
The Congruence Model treats the organisation as a transformation system — inputs (environment, resources, history) are transformed by four organisational components (Work, People, Formal Organisation, Informal Organisation) into outputs (strategy execution). Performance problems arise from incongruence between these components.
Work
The tasks that must be performed to deliver the strategy. At BaKuu Holding: drilling programme execution, trainee development, site safety management, cross-country coordination.
People
The individuals who perform the work — their skills, knowledge, experience, needs, and expectations. The G4 driller as both a technical specialist and an emerging people leader.
Formal Org
The official structure — reporting lines, processes, HR policies, job descriptions, and governance frameworks. What is written down and officially sanctioned.
Informal Org
The unofficial structure — who actually makes decisions, which relationships matter, the cultural norms. Often more powerful than the formal organisation. The Indonesia SM situation is an informal org problem.
The BaKuu Holding Congruence Gap: The formal org says the COO decides people policy. The informal org says each Site Manager decides independently. This incongruence produces the Indonesia resistance dynamic — and it cannot be resolved through HR process alone. It requires a governance redesign (Tool 9 of this department) and a relationship strategy (HRBP Tool 3).

Section 05 — HRBP vs OD Consultant

HRBP vs
OD Consultant

When should BaKuu Holding use an external OD consultant — and when should the HRBP lead? This is a boundary question that has significant implications for cost, quality, and the HRBP's own credibility and development.

The Default PositionThe HRBP should lead OD work unless there is a specific reason not to. Every OD engagement led by an external consultant is an opportunity the HRBP did not take — to develop their own capability, to build their internal reputation as a strategic advisor, and to demonstrate that the HR function can do more than transactional work.
When the HRBP Leads
Org diagnostic and design assessment. The HRBP has the deepest context on the organisation — relationships, history, informal dynamics. An external consultant takes weeks to acquire this. The HRBP already has it.
Role and job design. Redesigning jobs, grade structures, and accountabilities is HRBP work — it requires knowledge of the people, the pay structures, and the capability requirements that an external consultant does not have.
Change management and transition. The trust relationships the HRBP has built with site managers and the COO make the HRBP irreplaceable as the transition navigator. An external consultant is gone when the engagement ends.
Ongoing OD capability building. Developing the organisation's own capacity to think about design is an HRBP role. Dependency on external consultants for every OD question signals that the HR function has not grown.
When to Bring in a Consultant
Major enterprise-wide restructures. A group-level redesign involving 50+ roles, multiple functions, and Board-level governance requires specialised methodology and bandwidth the HRBP cannot provide solo. Use a consultant as a delivery partner — the HRBP retains strategy ownership.
When independent perspective is essential. If the HRBP is too close to the situation — or if leadership will not hear the same diagnosis from an internal voice — an external perspective can unlock the conversation. Use sparingly; overuse signals the HRBP is not trusted.
Specialist methodology the HRBP does not have. Process redesign (Lean, Six Sigma), large-scale culture transformation, or M&A integration require specialist skills. Bring the specialist in — and use the engagement to develop your own capability alongside them.
BaKuu Holding verdict: At current scale and complexity, the HRBP should lead all OD work. The 9-tool OD department in this toolkit gives the HRBP the methodology to do exactly that — without an external consultant.

Current Structure — What Works
Flat = fast. Three layers (COO → SM → Driller) means decisions are close to the operational reality. No bureaucracy between the field and the decision-maker. Works well at current scale.
Country-based structure matches the work. Mineral exploration is fundamentally site-specific. Each country has distinct regulatory, cultural, and operational requirements. Country-based SMs are the right model.
Grade structure is functional. G1–G8 gives enough granularity to manage career progression and pay equity without excessive complexity. The grade architecture in the C&B toolkit is a solid foundation.
Current Structure — The Tensions
COO span is approaching its limit. Currently: COO + 4 SMs + Group HR + Finance = 6 direct reports. Adding SM5 pushes this to 7. For a complex multi-country operation, this is the upper boundary of effective direct leadership.
No cross-country coordination role. There is no formal mechanism for the 4 SMs to share practice, align on standards, or coordinate shared resources. This gap grows as the organisation adds a 5th country.
HR model will not scale. One HR person for 65+ FTE across 5 countries and 5 sites is a structural gap. The business case for a second HR hire must be built and presented before the gap becomes acute.

Section 08 — OD Charter

BaKuu Holding
OD Charter

The OD Charter is the HRBP's mandate document for organisation design work at BaKuu Holding. It defines the purpose, scope, principles, and authority of the OD function — and it is the document the HRBP presents to the COO to secure engagement for the work ahead.

BaKuu Holding Group · OD Charter · 2026–2027
Organisation Design & Development Function
Authored by the Group HR Manager. Endorsed by the COO. Effective from Q2 2026.
01
Purpose of the OD Function
+
The OD function at BaKuu Holding exists to ensure that the organisation is always structured, staffed, and governed in a way that enables its strategy to be executed — now and in the future. It does this through proactive diagnosis, deliberate design, disciplined implementation, and continuous monitoring of organisational health. The OD function is led by the Group HR Manager and operates as a strategic advisory function to the COO and the BaKuu Holding leadership team.

03
Design Principles
+
Strategy-first. Structure follows strategy — always. We never design a structure and then fit strategy to it.
Minimum necessary complexity. The simplest structure that can execute the strategy is always preferred. Complexity has a cost — in coordination, communication, and leadership bandwidth.
People-grounded. No design is adopted without a clear view of whether it can be staffed with the people BaKuu Holding has or can realistically acquire. A structurally elegant design that cannot be staffed is not a design — it is a wish.
Designed for transition, not just destination. The best final-state design is worthless if the organisation cannot get there. Every design includes a transition plan.
Locally flexible, globally consistent. BaKuu Holding operates across four culturally distinct countries. Design standards must be consistent at the group level while allowing country-level adaptation where the operational or cultural context requires it.
