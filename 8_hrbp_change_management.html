HRBP Tool 8 — Change & Transition Management

Change &
Transition

Change is what happens to an organisation. Transition is what happens inside the people experiencing it. Most change programmes fail not because the plan was wrong — but because the transition was ignored. The HRBP manages both.

"Change is situational. Transition is the psychological process people go through to come to terms with the new situation."

William Bridges — Managing Transitions
The Critical Distinction
Most change initiatives focus entirely on the structural change — the new process, the org redesign, the new system. They treat people as the passive recipients of decisions made above them. This is why 70% of change programmes underdeliver.
Change
— External and situational
— Has a start and end date
— Managed by project teams
— Visible on a Gantt chart
— Examples: restructure, system launch, new policy, site expansion
Transition
— Internal and psychological
— Happens at each person's pace
— Managed by leaders and HRBP
— Invisible — felt, not tracked
— Examples: letting go of old ways, uncertainty, identity shift, rebuilding trust
The HRBP's role: manage the change (what, when, how) AND facilitate the transition (why it matters, what is lost, what comes next).
Why Change Fails at BaKuu Holding
Oil and gas field operations create specific change challenges that most textbook models do not address. Understanding these makes the HRBP's role clearer.
Site isolation
Field staff at remote sites have limited access to leadership. Rumour fills the communication vacuum faster than official channels can reach them. Changes announced at HQ take weeks to land meaningfully on site.
Multi-country, multi-culture dynamics
The same change message lands differently in Laos, Myanmar, Indonesia, and Thailand. What feels transparent and engaging in one cultural context can feel threatening or disrespectful in another.
Specialist identity
Experienced drillers carry strong occupational identity. Changes to how they work, who they report to, or what is expected of them touch something deeper than a process change — they touch professional pride.

Bridges' Three Zones of Transition
William Bridges identified that transition always begins with an ending, not a beginning. People must first let go of the old before they can embrace the new. The zone between is the most dangerous — and the one most organisations rush through.
Zone 1
Endings — Letting Go
People experience loss — of the familiar, the comfortable, identity, status, or relationships. This zone is marked by denial, anger, and grief. The HRBP's role is to acknowledge what is being lost, not minimise it. Rushing through this zone creates hidden resistance that surfaces later.
HRBP action: name the loss explicitly. Create space for it.
Zone 2
The Neutral Zone — In Between
The old is gone, the new is not yet established. This zone is characterised by confusion, anxiety, low productivity, and high attrition risk. It is also where the most creative problem-solving and innovation can happen — if people feel safe enough. The HRBP must hold steady here and communicate frequently.
HRBP action: over-communicate. Short feedback loops. Celebrate small wins.
Zone 3
New Beginnings — Embracing
People develop a new identity and relationship with the changed reality. Energy returns. Commitment builds. The HRBP's role is to reinforce and reward the new behaviours, embed them into systems (performance, reward, culture), and ensure backsliding is noticed and addressed quickly.
HRBP action: embed in systems. Recognise the early adopters publicly.
Applying Bridges at BaKuu Holding
The trainee programme attrition issue is a Bridges problem. When trainees arrive at site, they enter Zone 1 — the ending of their previous identity (civilian, student) — without being guided through it. They are expected to perform as drillers before the transition has happened.
What trainees experience at month 3
They are in Zone 2 — the neutral zone. Their previous identity has ended, but the driller identity has not yet formed. The discomfort is real and normal. Without a supervisor who names and holds this experience, they exit rather than persist through it.
The HRBP intervention
The 30/60/90-day check-in programme is a Bridges-informed intervention. It explicitly acknowledges the transition stages — "this stage is normal, here is what comes next, here is how you will know you have made it through." This is what the supervisors were not doing.
The broader principle
Any change at BaKuu Holding — new site opening, process change, leadership transition, restructure — should be analysed through the Bridges lens first. Ask: where are people in the transition curve? What does each zone require from leadership and HR?
Kotter's 8-Step Change Model — Applied to BaKuu Holding
Kotter's model is the most useful for large, planned change — restructures, new country entry, major policy overhaul. It emphasises that change requires urgency and coalition, not just a plan.
01 — Create urgency
The COO must understand why this change cannot wait. Use data: "Without this restructure, we project a 34% headcount shortfall by Q4." Urgency is not panic — it is a clear articulation of the cost of delay.
BaKuu Holding: vacancy gap data, attrition cost model, programme delivery risk
02 — Build the coalition
Identify who needs to lead this change with you. At BaKuu Holding: the COO is the sponsor, but the country managers are the delivery layer. A change the COO endorses but the site managers resist will fail at the implementation stage.
BaKuu Holding: engage Thailand champion first — use their model to convert Indonesia sceptic
03 — Form a clear vision
People can absorb almost any change if they can see where it leads and believe the destination is better. The HRBP crafts the vision narrative — what does success look like, and what does it feel like to work at BaKuu Holding when this change is complete?
BaKuu Holding: "A driller who joins BaKuu Holding has a clear 4-year path to G4 — and we never leave them alone in the neutral zone."
04–08 — Communicate, empower, win short-term, sustain, anchor
The remaining five steps are all about disciplined execution: communicating the vision relentlessly, removing blockers, celebrating early wins to build momentum, not declaring victory too early, and anchoring the change in culture and systems so it survives leadership turnover.
HRBP role: own steps 4 (communication), 6 (quick wins), 8 (culture anchoring)
ADKAR — The Individual Change Model
ADKAR is the most useful diagnostic for understanding why a specific individual or group is struggling with a change. It breaks the change adoption journey into five sequential building blocks — and shows where the blockage is.
A
wareness
Does the person understand why the change is necessary? If they do not see the reason, they will not move. The HRBP must ensure the case for change has been communicated clearly and in terms that connect to what the individual cares about.
Diagnostic: "Do you understand why we're making this change?" If no → communication gap.
D
esire
Does the person want to participate and support the change? Awareness without desire produces compliance at best, active resistance at worst. Desire is built through connection to personal benefit — not just organisational benefit.
Diagnostic: "What's in it for you?" If no answer → motivation gap. Address personal impact.
K
nowledge
Does the person know how to change? Even willing participants fail if they do not have the skills, information, or training to perform in the new way. This is the gap most organisations address last — and should address first.
Diagnostic: "Do you know what to do differently?" If no → training/process gap.
A
bility
Can the person actually perform in the new way in practice? Knowledge and ability are different. Practice, coaching, and permission to make mistakes during the transition period are all required to convert knowledge into consistent ability.
Diagnostic: "Can you do it?" If no → coaching, practice, psychological safety gap.
R
einforcement
Will the person sustain the change? Without reinforcement — recognition, accountability, and removal of barriers — people revert to old behaviours. The HRBP must embed the change into performance management, rewards, and leadership expectations.
Diagnostic: "Is the new way actually rewarded?" If no → systems and culture gap.
Section 03 — The HRBP's Role

The HRBP's
Role

The HRBP occupies a unique position in change — not the sponsor, not the project manager, but the architect of the human experience of change. This section defines what the HRBP is responsible for, and what they are not.

The HRBP as Change ArchitectThe HRBP does not design the org structure or own the project plan. The HRBP designs how the change will be experienced by people — the communication, the transition support, the leader capability, the resistance management, and the cultural reinforcement. These are the dimensions that determine whether the change sticks.

What the HRBP Should Never Do in Change
✗ Take ownership of the change — The HRBP advises and enables. The business leader must own the change and be seen to own it. A change that is perceived as "HR's thing" will never have the authority it needs.
✗ Position as "just communication" — Calling a change programme a communication exercise signals that the hard stuff — the loss, the resistance, the identity shift — will be glossed over.
✗ Minimise the loss — Telling people "it's not that different really" invalidates their experience and destroys the HRBP's credibility as a trusted advisor.
✗ Declare success too early — Real change adoption takes 12–18 months. Declaring success when the announcement has landed invites the change to unravel quietly while attention has moved on.
The HRBP as Honest Broker
In change, the HRBP occupies a unique position: trusted by leadership because of strategic alignment, but trusted by employees because of independence and confidentiality. This dual trust is the most valuable asset the HRBP holds — and the easiest to lose.

When leadership asks the HRBP to "manage the messaging" in a way that hides difficult truths, the HRBP must push back — not by refusing to help, but by reframing: "I can help you communicate this clearly and honestly. What I can't do is help you say something that employees will not believe — because the moment they don't believe it, they stop believing anything you say."

The HRBP who maintains this position — even when it is uncomfortable — earns a quality of trust from both sides that is irreplaceable in future changes.
Section 04 — Reading Resistance

Reading
Resistance

Resistance is not the enemy of change — it is information about what the change is costing people. The HRBP who can read and diagnose resistance accurately will navigate change faster and with less attrition than one who simply tries to overcome it.

"People don't resist change. They resist loss."

Ronald Heifetz — Leadership on the Line
The Four Types of Resistance
Information
Resistance
The person lacks information or has inaccurate information about the change. Rumour has filled the gap.
Signals
Questions start with "I heard that..." or "Is it true that..."
Response
Communicate clearly and directly. Repeat the truth more than you think is necessary.
Value
Resistance
The person understands the change but believes it is wrong — ethically, strategically, or professionally.
Signals
Principled objections. "This isn't how we do things here." Thoughtful, consistent challenge.
Response
Listen seriously. Some value resistance contains a legitimate concern. Incorporate where possible. Explain the trade-off where you cannot.
Loss
Resistance
The person understands and accepts the change but is grieving what it costs them — status, relationships, familiarity, identity.
Signals
Withdrawal, low energy, nostalgia for the old way. Not hostile — just sad.
Response
Acknowledge the loss explicitly. Do not rush people through this zone. Create rituals that mark the ending.
Political
Resistance
The person is using the change as leverage in a pre-existing power dynamic or personal agenda. Their objections are not about the change itself.
Signals
Resistance shifts as soon as personal situation changes. Objections are inconsistent or opportunistic.
Response
Address the underlying concern directly in private. Do not negotiate with the stated objection — it will shift again.

Section 05 — Communication Strategy

Communication
Strategy

The HRBP designs the change communication strategy — who says what, to whom, in what order, through which channel, and with what tone. This is the engineering of trust under conditions of uncertainty.

The Golden Rule of Change CommunicationCommunicate before people have to ask. The moment employees are asking questions you have not answered yet, rumour has taken hold — and rumour is always worse than the truth. Over-communicate when you have little to say. Under-communication is the single most common change failure.
The Communication Architecture
Change communication must be sequenced carefully. The wrong message to the wrong person at the wrong time destroys trust faster than silence.
Wave 1
Before announcement
Brief the leadership coalition
COO, country managers, direct sponsors. They need to understand before they are asked to communicate. If they learn at the same time as their teams, they lose authority.
Purpose: build the messenger coalition. Ensure consistent language. Prepare them for questions they will receive from their teams.
Private
1:1 or small group
Wave 2
Announcement day
Full announcement to all affected staff
Delivered by the most senior relevant leader — not HR. HR coaches the delivery; the leader delivers it. Include: what is changing, why, what it means for people, what happens next.
Purpose: eliminate the information vacuum. People who do not know will assume the worst. Give them the truth, even if incomplete.
All Staff
Town hall or site brief
Wave 3
Week 1–4
Q&A, listening sessions, follow-up
Create structured opportunities for people to ask questions and express concerns — safely, without judgement. This is where the HRBP listens for resistance type and feeds intelligence back to leadership.
Purpose: convert one-way announcement into two-way dialogue. People commit to changes they have been heard on — not ones imposed on them silently.
Dialogue
Small groups preferred
Wave 4
Ongoing
Progress updates and milestone celebrations
Regular updates on how the change is progressing — even when the answer is "still on track." Silence after the announcement is the second most common communication failure, after insufficient initial communication.
Purpose: sustain momentum. Recognise early wins. Keep the coalition engaged. Signal that leadership has not moved on just because the announcement was made.
Monthly
Scorecard + brief

Say ThisNot ThisWhy
"Here is what we know, and here is what we don't know yet.""Everything is fine. Nothing to worry about."Honesty about uncertainty builds more trust than false certainty. People know when they're being managed.
"This will be difficult. Here is the support that is in place.""Change is exciting! This is a great opportunity."Forced positivity invalidates real concerns and reads as leadership being out of touch with ground reality.
"What we are leaving behind mattered. It is okay to feel that loss.""The old way wasn't working. This is better."Dismissing the past tells people their experience was worthless — and breaks trust with those who built the old way.
"I don't have the answer to that yet — I will find out and come back to you by Friday.""We'll cross that bridge when we come to it."Committing to a follow-up and delivering on it is the single highest-leverage trust-building behaviour in change.
Section 06 — Change Phases Playbook

Change Phases
Playbook

The HRBP action playbook for each phase of a change — from first signal through to full embedding. What to do, what to watch for, and what success looks like at each stage.

01
Anticipation — Change is Coming
Weeks before announcement
+
HRBP Actions
Conduct change readiness assessment. Identify stakeholder resistance profile. Begin building the communication plan. Coach leadership on their role in the transition. Assess which HR systems need updating.
Watch For
Premature leaking of information — if people are hearing things informally before the official announcement, trust damage has already begun. Move the announcement forward if possible.
Success Signal
Leadership coalition is briefed, aligned, and ready to communicate. Communication plan is complete. Known resisters have been identified and a response approach is ready before they resist publicly.
02
Announcement — The Change Lands
Day 0 to Day 14
+
HRBP Actions
Be visible. Attend site briefings where possible. Collect the questions that were not asked in the room — people hold back in groups. Set up 1:1 check-ins with key individuals flagged as high-resistance. Begin listening for resistance type.
Watch For
Sudden increase in unplanned leave, sick days, or informal conversation spikes. Early exit conversations from key talent. Leaders going silent after the announcement — they must stay visible and accessible.
Success Signal
People are asking questions — this is healthy, not dangerous. Silence is the danger signal. A first Q&A session is scheduled within 5 days of announcement.
03
The Neutral Zone — The Hard Middle
Weeks 2–12
+
HRBP Actions
Maintain a high communication frequency — at minimum biweekly updates. Celebrate small wins visibly. Create short feedback loops: ask people what is working and what is not, then respond to what you hear. Monitor attrition and absenteeism weekly. Keep the coalition engaged — they tire faster than the HRBP expects.
Watch For
The "we're over it" signal from leadership — they have moved on psychologically because they knew about the change weeks before the team did. If leaders stop communicating, the neutral zone extends. Also watch: attrition spike 4–8 weeks after announcement as the reality sets in.
Success Signal
The nature of questions shifts from "why is this happening" to "how does this work in practice" — this signals the move from Zone 1 (endings) to Zone 3 (beginnings). First small wins are visible and being celebrated.
04
Embedding — Making it Stick
Months 3–12
+
HRBP Actions
Update performance frameworks to include the new behaviours. Revise rewards or recognition to reinforce the new way. Address any backsliding — publicly if needed. Assess whether the change has achieved what it set out to achieve and report on it. Recognise those who led the change.
Watch For
Quiet reversion to old practices, especially after a leadership change or a period of operational pressure. The change is not embedded until it survives its first major test. If the first crisis causes people to revert, the change was compliance, not adoption.
Success Signal
New starters onboard into the new way without being told it is "new." The change is no longer referred to as "the change" — it is just "how we do things." Leadership is using the new behaviours without being prompted.

Section 08 — BaKuu Holding Change Cases

BaKuu Holding
Change Cases

Three live change situations at BaKuu Holding — each analysed through the change management frameworks in this tool. Applied examples of how the HRBP diagnoses and responds to real transitions.

01
Trainee Programme Redesign — Myanmar
Culture/BehaviouralSite-levelActive
Bridges Analysis
Trainees are stuck in Zone 2 — the neutral zone — at month 3. Their pre-driller identity has ended; the driller identity has not formed. Supervisors did not know this was happening because they were never taught to name it. The redesign introduces structured Zone 2 support.
ADKAR Diagnosis
Supervisors have Awareness (they know training is expected) but lack Knowledge and Ability (they do not know how to hold a transition conversation). The intervention targets K and A — practical coaching on what to say at each trainee milestone.
HRBP Plan
Wave 1: Brief Myo H. — frame as professional development for his supervisors, not a criticism of their current practice. Wave 2: Deliver coaching programme (March). Wave 3: 30/60/90 check-in structure from next cohort. Monitor: conversion rate by July scorecard.
02
Myo H. — Retention & Succession Transition
Leadership TransitionMyanmarApproaching
The Change
Whether Myo H. renews his retention agreement (continuation scenario) or exits (transition scenario), a change is coming to Myanmar site leadership. The HRBP must plan for both — one is a retention intervention, one is a succession execution.
Bridges for the Team
If Myo exits, the Myanmar team enters a major Zone 1 event. Myo has been site manager for years — he is part of the team's identity. His successor will be judged against his memory, not their own merits. The transition plan must acknowledge this explicitly.
HRBP Plan
Q3 2026: retention conversation with Myo H. Simultaneously: identify internal successor candidate and begin development. If renewal: 2-year succession plan. If exit: structured handover plan including Myo's active involvement in successor integration. Protect Myanmar team from the uncertainty vacuum.
03
Country 5 Entry — Future State
StructuralGroup-widePlanning
Kotter Application
Country entry is a high-visibility change requiring Kotter's full 8-step approach. Urgency: competitive window for the contract. Coalition: COO + the Site Manager who will run Country 5 — must be identified and briefed before the contract is signed. Vision: what success looks like 12 months post-entry.
People Risk
The biggest people risk in country entry is deploying an internal leader into a context they are not ready for. The second is the cross-country deployment of experienced staff creating gaps in their home country. Both require the HRBP to model the supply-demand impact before the business decision is made.
HRBP Early Actions
Identify the internal SM candidate by Q3 2026. Begin development now — 15-month window to contract sign. Build country-specific rewards benchmark before the first offer is made. Prepare the existing 4-country team for what the 5th country entry means for their own resources and career paths.
