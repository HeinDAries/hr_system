OD Tool 2 / 9 — Org Structure Models

Org Structure
Models

Structure is the skeleton of the organisation — it determines how authority flows, how work is coordinated, and where decisions get made. Choosing the wrong structure for your strategy is one of the most expensive mistakes a growing business can make.

"Organisations are not machines. They are living systems. Structure shapes behaviour far more than any policy, incentive, or leadership programme ever will."

Henry Mintzberg — Structuring Organisations
Why Structure is a Strategic Decision
Most organisations treat structure as a HR administration task — updating org charts, changing job titles, shifting reporting lines. This is a category error. Structure determines:
Where decisions get made. A centralised structure pushes decisions up. A decentralised one pushes them down. Neither is right or wrong — the question is which serves the strategy.
How fast the organisation can move. Flat structures with wide spans make fast, context-specific decisions. Deep hierarchies make slow, consistent ones. Neither is universally superior.
What gets prioritised. A functional structure prioritises functional excellence. A geographic structure prioritises local responsiveness. You cannot optimise for everything simultaneously.
Who has power. Structure is one of the most visible signals of organisational power. Changing structure changes who matters — and who knows it. This is why restructures are politically charged even when they are operationally sensible.
The Structure Selection Question
Before choosing a structure, the HRBP must be able to answer five questions. If any of these cannot be answered clearly, the organisation is not ready to make a structural decision.
Q1 — Strategy
"What does the business strategy require us to be best at in the next 3 years?"
Q2 — Coordination
"Where is cross-unit coordination most critical — and most difficult right now?"
Q3 — Accountability
"Who in the current structure is clearly accountable for the most important outcomes?"
Q4 — Scale
"What happens to coordination and decision speed if we add 20 more people — and a new country?"
Q5 — People
"Can we staff the structure we are designing — with people we have or can realistically get?"
Section 02 — The Five Structure Models

The Five
Structure Models

Every organisation is some variant of five structural archetypes. Understanding all five — their logic, their strengths, and their failure modes — is the prerequisite for making a sound structural recommendation.

Model 01
Functional Structure
Organised by: Discipline +
What it looks like
Groups people by function — Operations, Finance, HR, Sales. Each function has a head who reports to the CEO/COO. Coordination happens at the top. Classic structure for organisations where functional excellence is the competitive advantage.
Strengths
— Deep functional expertise
— Clear career paths within functions
— Economies of scale within functions
— Simple, legible reporting lines
Failure Modes
— Silos: functions optimise for themselves
— Slow cross-functional coordination
— CEO/COO becomes a bottleneck
— Accountability for outcomes diffused
Best for: Single-product, single-market businesses where functional excellence is the competitive moat. Not well suited to multi-country, multi-site operations where local responsiveness matters.
BaKuu Holding verdict: Wrong model for BaKuu Holding. The work is fundamentally geographic — each country site has distinct regulatory, cultural, and operational requirements that cannot be effectively managed through a functional lens.
Model 02
Divisional / Geographic Structure
Organised by: Country / Region +
What it looks like
Groups people by geography, product, or customer segment — each division is largely self-contained with its own operations, P&L, and leadership. A small central function provides group oversight. This is the natural structure for multi-country operators.
Strengths
— Clear country/division P&L accountability
— Local responsiveness and context-sensitivity
— Manageable spans at group level
— Develops general managers with broad accountability
Failure Modes
— Duplication of shared functions
— Inconsistent standards across divisions
— Divisions become fiefdoms — resist group oversight
— Loss of economies of scale
Best for: Multi-country or multi-product businesses where local conditions vary significantly and local accountability is the primary performance driver. The natural home for mineral exploration operators.
BaKuu Holding verdict: The right structural model for BaKuu Holding — now and for the foreseeable future. The question is not whether to use this model, but how to evolve it as the group grows from 4 to 5+ countries.
Model 03
Matrix Structure
Organised by: Function × Geography +
What it looks like
People have two reporting lines — one to a functional head (e.g. Head of Drilling Operations) and one to a geographic head (e.g. Indonesia Site Manager). In theory, this captures both functional excellence and local responsiveness. In practice, it is extremely difficult to manage well.
Strengths
— Combines functional depth with geographic breadth
— Efficient use of scarce specialists
— Enables cross-geography sharing of practice
Failure Modes
— "Who is my real boss?" — chronic confusion
— Priority conflicts resolved politically, not logically
— Requires exceptional interpersonal skills at every level
— Very high coordination overhead
Best for: Large, mature organisations with strong management systems, high leadership capability, and deep cultural investment in collaborative working. Typically: global consultancies, large engineering firms, multinationals.
BaKuu Holding verdict: Wrong for BaKuu Holding at current scale. A matrix requires governance sophistication and leadership maturity that BaKuu Holding is still developing. The Indonesia resistance dynamic shows that even a clean single reporting line is contested — adding a second would compound that significantly.
Model 04
Flat / Network Structure
Organised by: Minimal hierarchy +
What it looks like
Removes as many layers as possible. Few or no middle managers. Teams self-organise around work. Relies heavily on cultural alignment, individual initiative, and strong communication systems. Works in small, high-trust teams with a shared mission.
Strengths
— Fast decisions, minimal overhead
— High engagement and ownership
— Low management cost
— Suits highly skilled, autonomous teams
Failure Modes
— Falls apart past 30–40 people
— Informal hierarchy replaces formal — less accountable
— Safety-critical environments need clear authority
— Career progression disappears — attrition rises
BaKuu Holding verdict: BaKuu Holding is already operating with a relatively flat structure (3 layers). The risk is that at 65 FTE, the remaining informal coordination mechanisms break down. Adding structure — not removing it — is the right direction of travel.
Model 05
Hybrid Structure
Organised by: Context-specific combination +
What it looks like
A combination of models — for example, a geographic divisional structure for operations with a functional centre of excellence for shared services. Most real-world organisations are hybrids — the discipline is in making the combination deliberate rather than accidental.
Strengths
— Tailored to the actual business model
— Can balance local and group priorities
— Allows shared functions without full matrix
— Evolves as the business evolves
Failure Modes
— Accidental hybrids lack intentional design
— "Who owns this?" gaps at the boundaries
— Harder to explain and communicate clearly
— Requires mature governance to prevent confusion
BaKuu Holding verdict: The recommended future-state structure for BaKuu Holding is a deliberate hybrid — geographic divisional for country operations, with a small group-level function for cross-country shared standards (HR, HSE, Finance). See Section 08 for the full recommendation.
Section 03 — Structure Trade-offs

Structure
Trade-offs

Every structural choice involves trade-offs. There is no structure that optimises everything simultaneously. The HRBP's job is not to find the perfect structure — it is to make the right trade-offs for the strategy, transparently and consciously.

The Fundamental TensionEvery structural design sits on a spectrum between two competing imperatives: integration (coordinating across units) and differentiation (allowing units to adapt to their context). More integration means more consistency but less local responsiveness. More differentiation means more local fit but less coherence. BaKuu Holding needs both — and the design must navigate this tension deliberately.

BaKuu Holding position: Technical and safety standards must be standardised — non-negotiable for client relationships and risk management. HR practices should have a group framework with country-level adaptation. This is the OD Charter design principle: locally flexible, globally consistent.

What the Current Structure Does Well
Clear country-level accountability. Each SM owns their country P&L, team, and programme delivery. No ambiguity about who is responsible for what. This is the most important design feature to preserve.
Low coordination overhead. Three layers means information travels fast. The COO is close enough to site reality to make informed decisions. No bureaucracy between field and headquarters.
Strong SM autonomy engages leaders. Thailand and Myanmar SMs are engaged precisely because they feel trusted and empowered. The structure has supported the retention of strong site leadership.
What the Current Structure Cannot Support
A 5th country without COO span redesign. COO currently has 6 meaningful direct relationships (4 SMs + HR + Finance). Adding SM5 approaches the outer limit of effective management at this complexity level.
Cross-country practice sharing. There is no structural mechanism for SMs to share what works — Thailand's high-conversion trainee model does not naturally flow to Myanmar because there is no group-level coordination function.
HR at 65+ FTE / 5 countries. The single Group HR Manager role is not structurally viable at the next scale. The model needs to evolve — either a second HR role, or a fractional support model for each country.

Section 06 — Design Principles Applied

Design Principles
Applied

The five OD Charter design principles — applied to the BaKuu Holding structural scenarios. Every structural decision at BaKuu Holding must pass through these five filters before it is adopted.

1
Strategy-First
Structure follows strategy — always. The BaKuu Holding strategy requires 5-country programme delivery, internal driller development, and margin protection as headcount scales. Every structural option is evaluated against these three requirements.
BaKuu Holding Application
Scenario B (Country 5 with Group Ops Lead) best serves the strategy — it enables 5-country delivery without COO overload, and the Group Ops Lead role directly supports the cross-country practice sharing that the driller development strategy requires.
2
Minimum Necessary Complexity
The simplest structure that can execute the strategy is always preferred. At 65 FTE, the temptation is to add layers and functions. The discipline is to add only what is structurally necessary — and nothing else.
BaKuu Holding Application
Do not create a Country Director layer or a regional structure. Do not add functional COEs unless they are critical for shared standards. The Group Ops Lead is a coordinator, not a manager — it adds a function without adding a layer.
3
People-Grounded
No design is adopted without a clear view of whether it can be staffed. A structurally elegant design that cannot be staffed is not a design — it is a wish.
BaKuu Holding Application
Country 5 cannot open until the SM candidate is identified and on a development plan (Q3 2026 at earliest). Group Ops Lead must be recruited or developed before Scenario B is activated — not after. The structure waits for the people; the people do not scramble for the structure.
4
Designed for Transition
The best final-state design is worthless if the organisation cannot get there. Every structural change includes a transition plan — including how it is communicated to SMs who may feel their autonomy is being constrained.
BaKuu Holding Application
The Indonesia SM will interpret any new Group Ops Lead role as a threat to his autonomy unless the role is positioned and introduced carefully. The transition plan must include a one-to-one conversation with each SM before the announcement — led by the COO, not just by HR.
5
Locally Flexible, Globally Consistent
Group standards for safety, financial reporting, and HR are non-negotiable. Everything else — how work is scheduled, how teams are managed day-to-day, how communication happens on site — is locally determined.
BaKuu Holding Application
The Group Ops Lead enforces three non-negotiables: safety standards, programme reporting format, and trainee development protocol. Everything else is the SM's domain. This boundary must be defined clearly and respected consistently — or the Indonesia SM's resistance will be justified.

Presenting this to the COO — Three Things to Say
1. "The current structure works — but it will not survive Country 5."
Frame it as proactive design, not criticism of the current state. The structure has served BaKuu Holding well at 42 FTE across 4 countries. The question is what it needs to become for 65 FTE across 5 — and whether we design that intentionally or inherit it by accident.
2. "I am not proposing more management layers. I am proposing better coordination."
The Group Ops Lead is not a new layer between the COO and the SMs — it is a coordination function. SMs retain full operational autonomy. The COO retains direct performance accountability. The Ops Lead provides the glue between countries that currently does not exist.
3. "I need two decisions from you today."
One: approval to begin the process of identifying the Group Ops Lead candidate and the Country 5 SM candidate. Two: commitment that Country 5 entry is contingent on both roles being filled — not on the contract timeline. These are the decisions that prevent the structure from being designed by crisis.
