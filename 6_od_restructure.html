OD Tool 6 / 9 — Restructure Playbook

Restructure
Playbook

Restructuring is the most disruptive intervention available to an HRBP. Used well, it unlocks organisational capability that no amount of coaching or process improvement can reach. Used poorly — or at the wrong time — it consumes enormous energy, damages trust, and leaves the organisation worse than before. The playbook makes the difference.

"Restructuring should be the last intervention, not the first. Most organisations restructure when they should be clarifying accountability. Most people problems are role problems. Most role problems are clarity problems."

OD Practice Principle
Valid Reasons to Restructure
A restructure is warranted when the current structure is genuinely incompatible with strategic need — and when no lighter-touch intervention (role design, accountability clarification, process change) can close the gap.
Scale change. The organisation is growing or contracting significantly enough that the current structure cannot handle the new headcount, span, or coordination demand. BaKuu Holding at 65 FTE with Country 5 requires a different structure than BaKuu Holding at 42 FTE today.
Strategy shift. The business is entering a new market, product, or delivery model that the current structure is not designed to serve. Adding a new country to a structure designed for four is a valid restructure trigger.
Coordination failure. The organisation consistently cannot coordinate across units — despite capability, effort, and role clarity — because the structure itself creates the barriers. No process change will fix a structural coordination problem.
Span breaking point. A critical leader's span of control has reached the point where the organisation is losing management quality — decisions slow, development stops, strategy does not reach the field. The COO-at-Country-5 scenario.
Invalid Reasons to Restructure
These are the most common reasons organisations restructure — and nearly all of them are wrong. The HRBP's role is to name the real problem before the restructure consumes the organisation's energy.
Performance problem. "The structure is broken" usually means "we have accountability gaps and poor role design." Fix the roles before restructuring. A restructure moves the accountability gaps to new boxes — it does not eliminate them.
People problem. Restructuring to manage out a difficult employee, to remove someone from a reporting line, or to solve a personal conflict is not restructuring — it is restructuring as cover for a people management failure.
New leader's instinct. A new COO or CEO who restructures in their first 90 days to "put their stamp on it" destroys more value than they create. The instinct to redesign before understanding the system is one of the most destructive leadership patterns in organisations.
Culture problem. If trust is low, accountability is diffuse, and coordination is poor, restructuring will not fix any of it. These are health problems (Tool 5). The same people, same culture, and same behaviours will reproduce the same patterns in the new structure.

Section 02 — Restructure Principles

Restructure
Principles

These are the non-negotiable principles the HRBP applies to every restructure — regardless of scale, urgency, or executive pressure. Violating any of them increases the probability of a failed restructure.

P1
Strategy first, structure follows
Structure is the answer to a strategic question. Before designing a new structure, the strategic question must be explicitly stated: "What does this structure need to enable that the current structure cannot?" If the question cannot be answered clearly, the restructure is not ready to proceed.
BaKuu Holding application: The Group Ops Lead restructure is answering a specific strategic question: "How does BaKuu Holding coordinate across 5 countries without increasing management layers or breaking the COO's span?" That question is answerable — the restructure is valid.
P2
Design for the future, not for the current people
The structure is designed for what the organisation needs to become — not to fit the capabilities or preferences of the people currently in it. People are then assessed against the structure. If a current role holder does not fit the new structure, that is a people decision — not a reason to compromise the design.
Common failure: The Group Ops Lead role is designed around the most available internal candidate rather than the strategic need. The role then becomes a consolation prize rather than a structural solution. Design the role first; find the person second.
P3
Minimum necessary change
The restructure changes only what needs to change to close the specific structural gap identified. It does not redesign the entire organisation to solve one problem. Every unnecessary change carries disruption cost — uncertainty, distraction, transition risk — that must be justified by a corresponding structural benefit.
BaKuu Holding application: The 2026 restructure adds two roles (Group Ops Lead, HR Coordinator). It does not redesign the country structure, change the SM reporting lines, or alter the G1–G4 driller pipeline architecture. Minimum necessary change.
P4
Sequence matters more than speed
The order in which restructure steps happen determines whether the change lands well or creates new problems. The most common sequencing failure: announcing a new structure before the roles within it have been defined. People are told they have a new reporting line but no one can explain what their new role is.
Critical sequence: Group Ops Lead role profile complete → COO approves → Role advertised → Person in post → Country 5 SM appointed. Not: Country 5 SM appointed → Group Ops Lead hired to manage the chaos that follows.
P5
Communication is a structural act, not a PR exercise
How people find out about a restructure, in what order, and through which channels determines whether they experience it as a managed change or a shock. Communication planning is not the last step in restructure design — it is embedded throughout. Every decision about structure has a corresponding communication implication.
Indonesia risk: If Wirawan S. learns about the Group Ops Lead role from a source other than the COO, with no prior context, his resistance will harden significantly. He must hear about this from the COO directly — with the authority boundary explicitly stated in the same conversation.
P6
The HRBP is designer, not just implementer
The HRBP's role is to design the restructure process — not simply execute what the COO or CEO has decided. This means challenging inadequate rationale, naming sequencing risks, identifying people impacts before they become surprises, and ensuring the structure being created is actually capable of doing what it is intended to do.
In practice: If the COO proposes a Group Ops Lead role with full SM management authority, the HRBP's job is to explain why that creates a fourth management layer, why the Indonesia SM will resist it, and what a better design looks like — before the role is created.
Section 03 — The 5-Phase Model

The 5-Phase
Model

Every restructure — from adding a single role to a full organisational redesign — follows the same five phases. The phases do not change; the depth and duration of each phase scales with the complexity of the change.

Phase 1 — Diagnose (Is restructuring the right intervention?)
The diagnosis phase establishes whether restructuring is genuinely needed — and if so, what specific structural problem it is solving. Most restructures that fail do so because Phase 1 was skipped or rushed.
What the HRBP does in Phase 1
Runs the health diagnostic (Tool 5) — is this a health problem or a structural problem?
Analyses spans and layers (Tool 4) — is the structure itself creating the problem?
Audits current role profiles (Tool 3) — are role design gaps the real issue?
States the structural gap explicitly: "The current structure cannot do X, which the strategy requires."
Presents diagnosis to COO with a recommendation: restructure OR lighter-touch intervention.
Phase 1 outputs
Written diagnosis — one page: current state, structural gap, strategic requirement
COO decision: proceed to design OR address through role/health intervention
Scope agreed: which parts of the organisation are in scope for redesign
BaKuu Holding timing: Phase 1 is complete. The diagnosis (Tools 3–5) has established that the COO span requires structural relief before Country 5 entry. Phase 2 — design — is the current live phase.
Phase 2 — Design (What should the new structure look like?)
Design is the creative and analytical core of restructuring. The HRBP produces design options — not a single answer — and presents them with the trade-offs clearly articulated. The COO decides; the HRBP designs.
Design activities
Model 2–3 structural options. Each option is defined by: reporting lines, role profiles, spans, layers, and authority boundaries — not just an org chart box.
Test each option against the structural gap: does this design solve the problem identified in Phase 1?
Model the people impact: which roles change, which are new, who is affected and how?
Cost each option: salary cost, transition cost, redundancy cost if applicable.
Draft role profiles for all new or significantly changed roles before presenting to COO.
Design quality tests
Does the design solve the specific structural gap — or a different problem?
Does every role in the new structure have a clear, non-duplicated accountability?
Are authority boundaries between new and existing roles explicitly defined?
Does the design comply with minimum necessary change (P3)?
Have the highest-risk stakeholders (Indonesia SM) been factored into the design?
Phase 3 — Decide (Which option, who is affected, and in what sequence?)
The decision phase is where the chosen design becomes a commitment. The HRBP is responsible for ensuring the decision is made with full information — including the people impact, the risks, and the sequencing requirements.
Decision requirements
COO formally selects design option. Decision is documented — not just communicated verbally.
Implementation sequence agreed: who hears what, in what order, on what timeline.
HRBP identifies any legal obligations: consultation requirements, notice periods, redundancy entitlements by country.
Confirm budget: new role costs approved before roles are advertised or announced.
Communication sequence — critical
Step 1 — COO + CEO — aligned before anyone else hears anything
Step 2 — Affected role holders — individually, before group announcement
Step 3 — Group announcement — after all individually affected have been spoken to
Step 4 — Wider team — site-level communication from SMs with consistent message
Phase 4 — Implement (Make the new structure operational)
Implementation is where restructures most commonly fail — not because the design was wrong, but because the transition from old to new is managed as an administrative process rather than a people change. The structure is new on paper before the people, processes, and authority boundaries are actually operating in the new mode.
Implementation tasks
All new role profiles published and communicated — before people start in new roles
New roles recruited or filled — no role vacant on day one of the new structure if avoidable
Authority boundaries communicated by COO directly to all affected leaders — not by email
PM and reward implications updated — if a role changes grade or accountability, pay and performance metrics update simultaneously
30-day and 90-day check-ins scheduled for all role holders in significantly changed roles
Common implementation failures
New structure announced before new roles are defined — people in limbo
Group Ops Lead hired but authority boundary not communicated to SMs — conflict follows immediately
Pay and grade not updated alongside role change — creates perceived unfairness and compensation anomalies
Transition support withdrawn after announcement — HRBP assumes implementation is complete when it has only just begun
Phase 5 — Embed (Did the restructure actually work?)
A restructure is not complete when the new org chart is published — it is complete when the new structure is operating as designed. Embedding is about ensuring the intended accountabilities, coordination mechanisms, and authority boundaries are actually functioning.
Embedding activities
90-day structural review: is the new structure solving the problem it was designed to solve?
Re-run the health diagnostic (Tool 5) for affected dimensions — has the health score improved?
Check for proxy resistance: are people operating as if the old structure still exists? Re-brief if needed.
Assess whether any unanticipated structural gaps have emerged — and address them before they become health issues.
BaKuu Holding embed measures — Group Ops Lead
COO effective span reduced from 6 to 4 — confirmed by calendar analysis
SM peer forum established and meeting quarterly — documented outputs
At least 2 cross-country practice transfers documented within 6 months of Ops Lead start date
Indonesia SM operating within agreed authority boundary — no escalation incidents to COO
D4 Coordination health score improves from 3/10 to ≥5/10 at next diagnostic (Tool 5)

Section 05 — Managing the People Impact

Managing the
People Impact

Every restructure affects people — even one that adds roles without removing any. The HRBP's people management responsibility does not start when redundancies are on the table. It starts the moment a structural change is being considered.

The people impact is not a separate workstream — it is embedded in every phase. Identifying who is affected, how, and what they need happens in Phase 1. Communication sequencing happens in Phase 3. Individual conversations happen before Phase 4 begins. Transition support continues through Phase 5. The HRBP who leaves people management until "after the design is done" is already behind.

Managing Reactions to Restructure
People react to restructure based on what they believe it means for them — not what it actually means. The HRBP's job is to reduce the gap between perception and reality through timely, specific, and honest communication.
Reaction: Resistance Indonesia SM profile
What it looks likeChallenges the rationale. Questions the need for the new role. Argues the current structure works fine. Escalates concerns to COO directly, bypassing the HRBP process.
Root causeUsually a perceived threat to authority, status, or control. The Indonesia SM's resistance to the Group Ops Lead is a sovereignty concern: "This role will reduce my autonomy." It is a rational response, not an irrational one.
HRBP responseAddress the concern directly, not generically. Name the authority boundary explicitly: "The Ops Lead coordinates standards. They do not manage you." Have the COO deliver this message — not the HRBP alone. Follow up in writing.
Reaction: Anxiety typical — unaffected roles
What it looks likeIncreased informal communication and speculation. Questions about job security. Reduced focus on work while waiting for "the full picture." Performance dip during transition.
Root causeInformation vacuum. People fill gaps in information with worst-case assumptions. Even people whose roles are completely unaffected will experience anxiety if they are not proactively told they are unaffected.
HRBP responseProactive and specific: "Your role is not changing. Here is what is changing and why. Here is what this means for you." Silence is not neutrality — it is a communication failure that creates anxiety.
Reaction: Opportunity capture Thailand SM profile
What it looks likeActive engagement with the change. Asks questions about the new role and how to contribute. May informally position themselves as a candidate. Can become an internal advocate for the change if managed well.
Root causeHigh performers who see structural change as a development opportunity. Thailand SM is a confirmed change champion — the restructure expands coordination expectations and the SM peer forum gives them a visible platform.
HRBP responseChannel the energy productively. Explicitly involve them in the SM peer forum setup. Use them as a reference case for Indonesia SM. Do not make promises about internal advancement that the restructure does not actually create.

Section 06 — BaKuu Holding Restructure 2026

BaKuu Holding
Restructure 2026

The full playbook applied to BaKuu Holding. Two structural changes — Group Ops Lead and HR Coordinator — sequenced correctly across the five phases, with the people impact mapped and the risk of each step identified.

BaKuu Holding Group — 2026 Structural Change Summary
Two additive roles. One sequencing requirement. Three risk points.
The 2026 BaKuu Holding restructure is minimal and additive — two new roles, no redundancies, no reporting line changes for existing roles. Its complexity lies not in scale but in sequencing (Ops Lead must precede Country 5 SM) and stakeholder management (Indonesia SM must be handled before the group announcement). Getting both right is the HRBP's primary execution challenge.

Three Risk Points — and How to Manage Each
Risk 1 — HighIndonesia SM resistance hardens before authority boundary is established
If Wirawan S. hears about the Group Ops Lead role through informal channels — or from a group announcement — before the COO has an individual conversation with him, the resistance will be significantly harder to manage. His interpretation will be: "A new layer is being inserted above me." That interpretation, once formed, is difficult to correct.

Mitigation: COO conducts individual conversation with Indonesia SM before any group announcement. Message: "This role coordinates group standards. It does not manage you. Your reporting line does not change. Here is what changes — and what does not." In writing within 24 hours of the conversation.
Risk 2 — MediumGroup Ops Lead authority boundary is ambiguous and creates conflict at start
If the Group Ops Lead role profile does not explicitly define where coordination authority ends, the role holder will naturally push boundaries — and SMs (especially Indonesia) will push back hard. Ambiguity at role level creates structural conflict at day one.

Mitigation: Role profile includes an explicit "Authority boundary" section: "The Group Ops Lead has authority to set group standards and facilitate the SM peer forum. The Group Ops Lead does not have authority to direct, performance manage, or override decisions of Site Managers." This language is non-negotiable in the role design.
Risk 3 — MediumOps Lead recruitment takes longer than expected — Country 5 timeline compresses
The Group Ops Lead is a specialist hire — G6 with cross-country minerals operations experience and coordination capability. If recruitment takes 4–5 months, the Q3 2026 target slips into Q4, compressing the Country 5 preparation window.

Mitigation: Recruitment commences June 2026 at latest. If no suitable internal candidate, external search begins immediately — not after internal process is exhausted. Interim: COO personally chairs SM peer forum until Ops Lead is in post. The forum does not wait for the role to be filled.

COO → Indonesia SM — Individual Pre-Briefing
This conversation must happen before any group announcement. It is a 1:1 conversation — not an email. The COO delivers it. The HRBP prepares the COO and may be present if appropriate. Duration: 20–30 minutes.
Conversation structure — COO talking points
Opening — context
"I want to talk to you before I brief the wider team, because what I'm about to share affects the group structure — and I want to make sure you hear it from me first and have a chance to ask questions."
The change — what is happening
"We're adding a Group Operations Lead role to the group structure. The purpose of this role is to coordinate standards across all four countries — and five once we enter the next market — and to run the Site Manager peer forum. It's a coordination role, not a management role."
The authority boundary — explicit and direct
"I want to be very clear about what this role does and does not mean for you. The Ops Lead does not manage you. Your reporting line to me does not change. The Ops Lead has no authority to direct your team or override your operational decisions. What they will do is work with you — and all the SMs — on group-wide standards and practices. You will still run Indonesia."
Why now — the rationale
"With Country 5 coming, my direct span would reach eight — which is not sustainable. This role protects the quality of my engagement with you and the other SMs. It's a growth investment, not a cost-cutting exercise. No roles are being removed."
Close — invitation for questions
"I'd like to hear your reaction. What questions do you have? Is there anything about this that concerns you that I haven't addressed?" [Pause — allow silence.] "I'll also send you a written summary of what we've discussed today so we're both clear on the scope."
HRBP prep note
Prepare COO for the likely pushback: "This is just a management layer in disguise." The COO must be ready to repeat the authority boundary clearly and without defensiveness. If the SM says "I'm not comfortable with this", the COO response is: "I hear that. I want to understand specifically what concerns you." Do not argue. Do not explain at length. Ask and listen.
COO → All SMs — Structured Briefing
After all individual conversations are complete — Indonesia SM first — the COO briefs all four SMs together. This can be a video call. It should not be an email. Duration: 45–60 minutes including Q&A.
Group SM briefing — agenda and talking points
1. Context (5 min)
"We are growing. By Q2 2027 we will be at 65 people across five countries. The structure that got us to where we are today is not the right structure for where we're going. We're making two targeted additions to the group structure to prepare for that growth. No roles are being removed. No reporting lines are changing."
2. The changes (15 min)
"We are adding two roles. First: a Group Operations Lead — G6, reports to me, focused on coordinating group standards and running the SM peer forum. This role does not manage any of you. Second: an HR Coordinator, reporting to Ba, supporting the group HR workload as we scale." [Distribute one-page role summary for each new role.]
3. What this means for you (10 min)
"Your roles as Site Managers are unchanged. You still report directly to me. The Group Ops Lead is a resource for you — they will help us share what's working across countries, set group standards more consistently, and run a quarterly forum where we can all learn from each other. [Thailand SM] has already shown us what's possible when best practice travels across the group."
4. Timeline and next steps (5 min)
"We are recruiting for the Ops Lead role from [month]. Ba will be in touch with each of you individually as the process moves forward. The HR Coordinator role follows in Q4. Any questions on timeline or process — come to Ba or to me directly."
5. Q&A (20 min)
Hold space for questions. Do not rush this. The quality of the Q&A determines whether SMs leave the briefing with clarity or with speculation. HRBP takes notes. Any questions that cannot be answered immediately are responded to in writing within 48 hours.
Group-Wide Announcement — Written Communication
After all SMs have been individually briefed, a written announcement is distributed to all group staff. Drafted by HRBP, reviewed and approved by COO, sent from COO. Clear, brief, and specific.
Draft announcement — from COO to all staff

Subject: BaKuu Holding Group — Structural Update

Team,

BaKuu Holding is growing. We are on track for Country 5 entry in 2027 and a team of 65 people across our operations. To prepare for that growth, we are making two targeted additions to the group structure.

Group Operations Lead (G6) — A new role reporting to me, responsible for coordinating group-wide operational standards and facilitating the Site Manager peer forum. This is a coordination role. Site Manager reporting lines are unchanged. We are recruiting for this role from [month].

HR Coordinator (G4) — A new role reporting to Ba, providing operational HR support as our team grows. We expect to recruit for this role in Q4 2026.

No existing roles are being removed or significantly changed. If you have questions about what this means for your role, please speak to your Site Manager or to Ba directly.

This is a strong period for BaKuu Holding. These additions are an investment in the infrastructure we need to operate well across a larger, more complex group.

[COO name]

HRBP note
Send in the local language where possible — or with a local language translation note. Ensure all SMs have been briefed before this is distributed. Confirm with COO the exact send date. Do not send on a Friday — allows no time for questions before the weekend.
HRBP Briefing Guide — Questions to Prepare For
The HRBP must be prepared to field questions from any role holder at any point in the process. These are the most common questions — with the recommended response framework.
QuestionWho asks itHRBP response framework
"Is my job safe?"Most commonly G3/G4 site staff who hear about structural change without full contextAnswer directly and immediately: "Yes. This change adds two new roles. No existing roles are being removed. Your role is not affected." Do not qualify this answer unnecessarily.
"Does the Ops Lead manage us now?"Any Site Manager — but especially Indonesia"No. The Group Ops Lead does not manage Site Managers. You continue to report to the COO. The Ops Lead coordinates group standards and runs the peer forum — they have no authority over your team or your operations."
"Why wasn't I consulted?"Higher-risk stakeholders who feel they should have had input into the design"The design decision sits with the COO. Your input on how the role works in practice is valuable — and the Ops Lead's first order of business will be to work with each of you on how the coordination function should operate. That is where your expertise is most relevant."
"When will this happen?"Any staff memberGive the honest timeline. "We are recruiting for the Ops Lead from [month]. We expect to have someone in role by [Q3 target]. The HR Coordinator follows in Q4."
"What does this mean for my career?"G4 drillers, G5 SMs — especially high performers assessing whether BaKuu Holding has room for their development"This restructure creates two new roles — one of which is G6. That expands the pathway within the group. Your development plan is not changing as a result of this. Let's talk about your specific situation."
"Is this about the Indonesia performance?"Indonesia SM or team members"No. This is a group-wide structural investment driven by our growth trajectory. The decision would be the same regardless of any individual country's current performance."
