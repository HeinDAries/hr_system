Spans &
Layers

Span of control and organisational layers are the two most visible structural variables in any organisation. They determine how decisions flow, how fast the organisation can move, and how much management overhead it carries. Getting them wrong is expensive — and usually silent until it breaks.

"Adding a management layer to solve a coordination problem is like adding lanes to solve traffic congestion. It relieves the symptom temporarily and makes the underlying problem worse."

OD Structural Design Principle
Span of Control — Defined
Span of control is the number of direct reports a manager has. It is one of the most consequential structural decisions in any organisation — and one of the least deliberately made.
Effective span is not the same as reported span. The COO at BaKuu Holding has 6 official direct reports — but the real coordination burden is higher because each Site Manager relationship involves multi-country complexity, regulatory variance, and a different client context.
Span is a proxy for coaching time. A manager with 8 direct reports has approximately 45 minutes per person per week if they work a standard week. A manager with 4 direct reports has 90 minutes. The choice is structural — not a matter of effort or commitment.
No universal right answer. The right span depends on: the complexity of each subordinate's work, the degree of interdependence between subordinates, how much the work is standardised, and the capability level of both the manager and their reports.
Management Layers — Defined
A management layer is a level in the hierarchy at which people have formal authority over the level below. Each layer adds coordination overhead, slows information flow, and creates a potential point of distortion between strategy and execution.
The layer cost. Every management layer adds approximately 15–20% overhead to the cost of the work it oversees — through salary, coordination time, and the inevitable re-work that comes from information being filtered through an additional person.
The layer signal. When a manager's primary role is to supervise and check the work of the level below — rather than to add distinct value — that layer is a candidate for removal. Each layer should add a meaningfully different type of judgement, not just more oversight.
Layers vs grades. Management layers and grades are not the same thing. BaKuu Holding has eight grades (G1–G8) but only three management layers (COO → SM → Driller team). G1–G4 are individual contributor grades with varying seniority — not separate management layers.
The Span–Layer Relationship
Span and layers are mathematically and structurally linked. Widening span reduces layers; narrowing span adds layers. For a given headcount, the choice of span determines the number of layers.

The benchmark tells you where to look — your diagnosis tells you what you see.

Section 03 — Dysfunction Signals

Dysfunction
Signals

Span and layer problems rarely announce themselves as structural issues. They surface as performance gaps, communication failures, and leadership complaints. The HRBP's diagnostic skill is recognising the structural root cause underneath the presenting symptom.

Wide Span Dysfunction — Too Many Direct Reports
The Bottleneck Manager
Decisions queue up waiting for the manager's attention. Response times lengthen. Reports start making decisions they shouldn't to avoid the queue — or defer decisions they should make because they can't get time.
BaKuu Holding signal: COO response time to SM operational questions
The Invisible Manager
Reports rarely see their manager. 1:1s are cancelled more than they happen. Development conversations disappear. The manager is present in name only — each report effectively manages themselves.
Risk: COO at Country 5 without structural relief
The Firefighting Manager
The manager can only respond to crises — never has time for proactive planning, coaching, or strategic work. The span has consumed all available capacity. Management quality drops to reactive mode.
BaKuu Holding signal: watch for COO calendar composition
Narrow Span Dysfunction — Too Few Direct Reports
Over-Management
With only 2–3 reports, a manager has too much capacity relative to their span. They fill the gap with micromanagement — reviewing decisions their reports should make, attending meetings they don't need to attend, adding process overhead.
Watch: new Group Ops Lead role — clear scope needed to prevent this
Management Cost Inflation
Narrow spans require more managers for the same headcount. Each manager carries a salary premium. A deeply hierarchical structure with 3-report spans at every layer can have 30–40% of headcount in management roles — an enormous cost burden.
At 43% people cost, BaKuu Holding cannot afford unnecessary management layers
Career Compression
Deep hierarchies create many grades between entry and senior roles — but each grade represents a small increment of additional authority or complexity. High performers feel stuck in a slow progression system. Attrition among the best rises.
G3→G4 already takes 18–24 months — any additional layer compression would worsen attrition
Layer Dysfunction Signals
Signal observedLikely structural causeDiagnostic question
Decisions always escalate to the topToo many layers, each passing the decision up rather than resolving it. Or authority boundaries undefined — so no layer is confident enough to decide."What percentage of decisions that reach the COO could have been made by the SM?" If the answer is more than 20%, the authority boundary is the problem.
Information loses fidelity as it travels upEach layer filters and interprets what it passes up. By the time the COO sees the picture, it has been through 2–3 interpretive lenses and no longer reflects field reality."When the COO visits a site, does what they see match what they've been told?" Significant gap = layer distortion.
Strategy doesn't reach the fieldToo many layers between strategy and execution. Each layer translates (and sometimes dilutes) the strategic direction before passing it on."Can a G3 driller in Myanmar explain what BaKuu Holding is trying to achieve in the next 12 months?" If not, the layer count is part of the problem.
Middle managers add process, not valueA layer exists that primarily coordinates and checks — but doesn't make distinct judgement calls that require their grade. This layer is a candidate for removal or redesign."If we removed this layer, what would be lost?" If the answer is primarily administrative coordination, the layer is overhead, not value.

SMs: 8–12 — within range
