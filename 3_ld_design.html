L&D Tool 3 / 7 — Learning Design Toolkit

Adult Learning
Principles

Learning design at BaKuu Holding starts with one constraint: the learner is a G1 trainee on a remote drill site, working 10–12 hour shifts, with limited literacy in some cases, and a G4 as their primary teacher. Every design decision must pass the field test — will this work in that context?

"Adults do not learn by being told. They learn by doing something, reflecting on what happened, understanding why it happened, and trying again differently. Every piece of learning BaKuu Holding designs must create this cycle — not replace it with a presentation."

BaKuu Holding L&D Design Principle · 2026
The Six Adult Learning Principles — Applied to BaKuu Holding
Self-direction
Principle 1
Adults need to understand why they are learning something and feel ownership over their development. They do not respond well to being told what to do without context.
BaKuu Holding applicationEvery G1 induction opens with the G4 explaining the full G1→G4 career pathway and the trainee's specific development plan. The trainee knows where they are heading and why each task connects to that path.
Experience base
Principle 2
Adults bring prior experience to learning. New learning is most effective when it connects to or builds on what the learner already knows and has done.
BaKuu Holding applicationG4 coaches begin every technical session with "tell me what you already know about this." Trainees from mining, construction, or agricultural backgrounds have relevant experience. The G4 builds on this rather than starting from zero.
Readiness
Principle 3
Adults learn best when the content is relevant to a challenge they are currently facing. Readiness is created by context — the learner must feel the need before the content lands.
BaKuu Holding applicationSequence G1 content to follow the work, not precede it. Teach equipment maintenance after the trainee has operated the equipment and has questions. Teach safety protocols in the context of a real situation they have seen — not in advance of any experience.
Problem-centred
Principle 4
Adults are motivated by solving real problems, not by acquiring abstract knowledge. Learning framed as "here is how to solve this specific problem you have" is retained far better than "here is some information."
BaKuu Holding applicationThe G4 coaching programme uses real Myanmar site scenarios, not hypothetical cases. "What would you do if your G1 trainee had not shown up for two days?" is the entry point to a development conversation, not a policy document about attendance.
Intrinsic motivation
Principle 5
The most durable learning motivation comes from internal drivers — competence, autonomy, belonging, purpose. External rewards support but do not sustain learning motivation on their own.
BaKuu Holding applicationThe month-3 check-in is designed to surface intrinsic motivation and identify when it is absent or eroding. A trainee who cannot articulate why they want to be a G4, or whose answer has changed since induction, is an attrition risk that the check-in is designed to catch.
Immediate application
Principle 6
Adults retain what they can immediately apply. Content that will be "useful later" is not retained. Every learning session must end with something the learner will do differently tomorrow.
BaKuu Holding applicationEvery G4 coaching session ends with one specific commitment from the trainee: "By end of this week I will…" The G4 asks about this commitment at the start of the next session. Without this loop, the session is conversation, not development.
Kolb's Learning Cycle — The Design Framework
Every structured learning activity at BaKuu Holding should complete at least one full cycle. The most common failure mode is designing only stage 3 — giving information — and skipping the experience, reflection, and experimentation stages that make information stick.
1
Concrete Experience
The learner does something. At BaKuu Holding: operating equipment for the first time, handling an unexpected failure, running a G1 development conversation for the first time.
→ DO IT
2
Reflective Observation
G4 asks: "What did you notice? What worked? What felt uncomfortable?" This is the stage G4s most commonly skip — moving straight from experience to telling the answer.
→ NOTICE IT
3
Abstract Conceptualisation
G4 guides: "So what does that tell you about how to handle this in future?" Then introduces the principle that explains what happened. Information is most useful here — after reflection, not before experience.
→ UNDERSTAND IT
4
Active Experimentation
G4 closes: "What will you do next time you face this? What specifically will you try?" This becomes the opening question next session. The cycle restarts with a new concrete experience.
→ TRY IT DIFFERENTLY
The G4 coaching failure mode: Starting at stage 3 without stages 1–2, and ending at stage 3 without stage 4. "Let me tell you how to handle this" — followed by nothing. The trainee receives information with no experiential anchor and makes no commitment to apply it. The G4 coaching programme must train G4s to hold the discomfort of silence during stage 2.
Section 02 — Designing for the Field

Designing for
the Field

The BaKuu Holding learning environment is a remote drill site. Every design decision must account for this reality — no LMS, shift patterns that limit learning time, a multilingual workforce, and a G4 who has never been formally trained to teach.

The BaKuu Holding Learning Environment
Design for the G1 on site, not for the L&D manager in the office
The learner is physically active, often in difficult conditions, with limited access to written materials, working in a second or third language, supervised by a G4 who has never been formally trained to teach, and aware that their performance is being assessed in real time on work that matters to the operation. This is a high-stakes, low-resource learning environment. Design for it deliberately.
Field Learning Constraints
No LMS or digital infrastructure. All learning design must be deliverable without technology — printed guides, verbal frameworks, structured conversations. If it requires a screen, it will not reach the G1 on site.
Shift patterns prevent long sessions. 10–12 hour shifts leave limited dedicated learning time. Any formal session must be 30–45 minutes maximum. The 70% happens during work, not alongside it.
Multilingual workforce. Myanmar, Lao, Bahasa Indonesia, Thai. All G1-level materials must be in the local language. G4 coaching frameworks must be language-flexible — built around questions, not scripted content.
Variable literacy levels. Visual guides, demonstration-based instruction, and verbal check-ins are more reliable than written assessments. Where documentation is required, keep it minimal — checkboxes and short phrases, not paragraphs.
G4 as untrained teacher. The G4 is an expert practitioner, not a trained instructor. All frameworks must be simple enough for a G4 to execute without preparation — they need a card, not a course.
Field Learning Design Rules
Rule 1 — One concept per session. A G4 coaching session covers one thing and does it completely. One skill, one situation, one mistake. Trying to cover three topics in 30 minutes results in none of them landing.
Rule 2 — Show before tell. Demonstration precedes explanation in all field learning. G4 does the task first, narrating what they are doing and why, then the trainee attempts it.
Rule 3 — End with a specific commitment. Every learning interaction ends with a specific action: "By Friday you will attempt X and we will debrief Monday." Without this, learning is conversation, not development.
Rule 4 — Simple documentation. Every milestone completed is documented in a single sign-off — G4 signature, date, brief observation. Documentation exists to make development visible to SM and Group HR, not to burden the G4.
Rule 5 — Design for mistakes. In field conditions, trainees will make mistakes in real operational conditions. Design learning to use mistakes as development material. A G4 who debriefs a mistake using the Kolb cycle turns an operational problem into a learning event without losing time.

Section 03 — On-the-Job Learning Design

On-the-Job
Learning Design

70% of development at BaKuu Holding happens through work — structured assignments, stretch tasks, progressive responsibility, and real-time feedback from G4 coaches. This section provides the design frameworks that make the 70% intentional rather than accidental.

The 70% is the pipeline. For G1 trainees, on-the-job learning is not a supplement to a training programme — it is the training programme. The quality of the G1's development experience is determined entirely by whether their G4 structures the work as a learning progression or simply assigns tasks to get the work done. The designs in this section give G4s the tools to do the former.
The G4 Structured Debrief — Four Questions
The structured debrief is the single most important learning design tool at BaKuu Holding. A G4 who uses these four questions consistently after significant work events will develop trainees faster than any formal programme could. The questions follow Kolb's cycle and take 10–15 minutes.
Q1
"What happened? Walk me through it."
The trainee describes the event in their own words. The G4 listens without interrupting or correcting. This stage serves two purposes: it builds the trainee's habit of observing what they do, and it gives the G4 accurate information about what the trainee actually experienced — not what the G4 thinks they experienced.
KOLB STAGE 1 — CONCRETE EXPERIENCE
Q2
"What did you notice? What surprised you?"
The trainee reflects on what stood out — what was different from what they expected, what felt difficult, what felt easy. The G4 holds silence here. This is the stage most commonly skipped — G4s feel uncomfortable with silence and fill it with answers. The silence is the development. Give the trainee 15–20 seconds to think before prompting.
KOLB STAGE 2 — REFLECTIVE OBSERVATION
Q3
"What does that tell you about how this works?"
The trainee forms a principle from their reflection. The G4 adds or corrects here — but only after the trainee has attempted their own interpretation. The G4 should not provide the answer before the trainee has tried. Even a partially correct answer from the trainee is a better foundation than a complete answer from the G4.
KOLB STAGE 3 — ABSTRACT CONCEPTUALISATION
Q4
"What will you do differently next time?"
The trainee commits to a specific action. "I'll try…" "Next time I'll…" "I want to practice…" This commitment is the bridge from reflection to changed behaviour. The G4 writes it down and opens the next debrief by asking what happened with the commitment. Without this, the cycle is incomplete and the learning does not transfer.
KOLB STAGE 4 — ACTIVE EXPERIMENTATION
G4 coaching failure modes — and how to fix themMost G4 coaching failures happen at Q2 (filling silence with answers) and Q4 (skipping the commitment entirely). The G4 coaching programme must specifically train for silence at Q2 and specificity at Q4. A commitment of "I'll be more careful" is not a commitment. "I'll check the drill angle before starting and call you over to verify" is a commitment.
Stretch Assignment Design — The G1 to G4 Progression
A stretch assignment is any task that requires the learner to operate at the edge of their current capability. Stretch assignments are the primary vehicle for G1→G4 development. They must be deliberately designed — not just "harder work" — with a specific capability target, a support structure, and a debrief attached.

Section 04 — Peer & Social Learning Design

Peer & Social
Learning Design

20% of development at BaKuu Holding happens through social learning — conversations with peers, SM peer forum practice transfer, cross-site exposure, and the informal learning that happens when people work alongside others who know more than they do. This section designs the structures that make peer learning intentional.

Why peer learning matters at BaKuu Holding
Thailand's practices are not in any document. They live in peer conversation.
The highest-value learning at BaKuu Holding is currently invisible — the practices that make Thailand the highest-performing country are held by one SM and transferred, if at all, through informal conversations at group meetings. Structured peer learning design makes this transfer explicit, replicable, and resilient to SM departure. The SM peer forum is the primary vehicle. Cross-site exposure is the secondary vehicle.
SM Peer Forum — Design Specification
FREQUENCY
Quarterly. 90 minutes per session. Ba facilitates. All four SMs attend — video call with fixed agenda. Not a meeting with an optional learning segment; a learning session with a brief operational update.
STRUCTURE — EVERY SESSION
10 min — Commitment review. Each SM reports on what they committed to try at the last session and what happened. No commitment review means no accountability loop.
40 min — Practice share. One SM presents one practice in depth: what the practice is, what problem it solves, how they implemented it, what the result was. The presenting SM rotates each session. Thailand SM presents first three times — then Myanmar, then Indonesia, then Laos.
30 min — Problem-solving. One SM brings a live challenge. Group discusses. Ba facilitates — does not solve.
10 min — Commitments. Each SM commits to one thing they will try before the next session.
BA'S ROLE
Facilitate, not teach. Ask questions that create insight rather than providing answers. Document commitments and share back within 24 hours. For Indonesia SM: the peer forum is the primary influence vehicle — use Thailand's data in the problem-solving segment to demonstrate the value of Group HR approaches.
Cross-Site Exposure — Design Specification
PURPOSE
Cross-site exposure accelerates G3→G4 development by exposing candidates to different operational conditions, a different SM's approach, and the experience of being unknown — no established reputation, no shortcuts. It is particularly valuable for Laos G3 candidates who need to develop G4-level independence before the retirement cliff hits.
STRUCTURE — 4–6 WEEKS AT HOST SITE
Week 1–2: Observation and assisted work. No independent operation. Candidate observes how the host site runs differently from their home site.
Week 3–4: Supervised independent work. Candidate runs a full shift with host G4 observing.
Week 5–6: Stretch assignment. Candidate takes responsibility for one specific challenge: training a G1, leading a non-routine operation, presenting to host SM.
End of placement: Candidate writes a one-page debrief on three practices from the host site and their application back home.
2026 PRIORITIES
Laos G3 candidate 1 → Thailand site, Q3 2026 (4 weeks). Laos G3 candidate 2 → Indonesia site post-recovery, Q4 2026 (4 weeks). Purpose: accelerate G4 readiness before first Laos G4 retirement in 2027.
Thailand Practice Capture — The First Peer Learning Priority
Before any peer transfer can happen at scale, the Thailand SM's practices must be captured. This is a Ba-led structured interview — not a survey, not a written submission. Ba conducts the interview; the output is a practice library of 3–5 Thailand practices that can be shared at the peer forum.

Section 05 — Formal Learning Design

Formal Learning
Design

The 10% — formal learning at BaKuu Holding is small in volume but high in impact. It is the only learning that Group HR designs and delivers directly. It must be designed to complement, not duplicate, the 70% and 20% happening on site. This section covers the design of the G4 coaching programme and the SM peer forum formal inputs.

The formal learning constraint at BaKuu HoldingBaKuu Holding has no training budget for external programmes in 2026 beyond what Ba can justify to the COO as direct pipeline investment. The G4 coaching programme is the highest-priority formal learning investment. All formal design must be deliverable by Ba — with Thailand SM as co-facilitator for G4 content — at near-zero cash cost.
The G4 Coaching Programme — Full Design
The G4 coaching programme is the most important formal learning intervention at BaKuu Holding. It directly addresses the capability gap causing Myanmar's 58% conversion rate and establishes the foundation for G4 coaching quality across all countries. The programme is 6 weeks, site-delivered in modular 45-minute sessions, in local language.

Section 06 — The Month-3 Check-in

The Month-3
Check-in

The month-3 check-in is the single highest-leverage intervention in the BaKuu Holding L&D system. It is the primary early warning mechanism for G1 trainee attrition risk — and it currently does not exist in any structured form at any BaKuu Holding site. This section provides the complete design.

"Month 3 is when the trainee knows enough to evaluate whether their real experience matches what they were promised. If the gap between promise and reality is too large — they begin to disengage. The check-in catches this moment before the decision is made."

BaKuu Holding L&D Framework · 2026
Why Month 3?
Month 1: The trainee is in survival mode — learning everything at once, adjusting to site life, still in the honeymoon period. They have not yet formed a clear view of whether this is the right path.
Month 2: The trainee begins to settle. The novelty wears off. They start comparing the job to the expectations they had when they joined. Dissatisfaction begins to form but is not yet conscious.
Month 3: The decision window. The trainee has enough information to evaluate their experience. If the development investment feels low, the career path feels unclear, or the relationship with the G4 feels unsupporting — they begin actively considering leaving. This is the intervention moment.
Month 4+: By month 4, the decision is often made even if the resignation has not happened yet. Intervention after month 4 is possible but the retention rate drops significantly. Month 3 is the deadline, not a preference.
The Seven At-Risk Signals
G4s are trained to watch for these signals from week 6 onwards. Two or more signals appearing simultaneously, or any single signal intensifying over 2+ weeks, triggers the check-in early — before month 3 if necessary.
S1
Reduced engagement or enthusiasm compared to first 4 weeks — quieter, less curious, stops asking questions about the work or their development.
S2
Increased lateness or unexplained absences — especially short absences for vague reasons.
S3
Mentions of other job opportunities, or questions about what other employers pay for similar work.
S4
Reduced quality or care in work output — not fatigue-related, but motivational. Work that used to be done carefully is now done quickly.
S5
Withdrawal from peer relationships — stops joining others during breaks, reduced communication with other G1s and G2s.
S6
Questions about pay, progression timeline, or benefits that were not asked before — indicates the trainee is re-evaluating the deal they made.
S7
Mentions of family pressure or personal circumstances — particularly common in Myanmar where family expectations about stable employment are a significant attrition driver.

The Five Check-in Questions — Reference Card
This is the printable reference card for G4s. One card per G4, laminated. Questions are sequenced — do not reorder. Each question has a listen-for note to help the G4 interpret what they are hearing.
1
"How has the work been for you these past few months?"
Open question. No framing. The trainee describes their experience in their own words. G4 listens without agreeing or disagreeing. This sets the tone: this is a conversation about the trainee, not a performance review.
LISTEN FOR
Energy and enthusiasm in the answer. Specific details (engaged trainees reference specific experiences). Vague or short answers ("it's fine, it's okay") are low-energy signals that warrant follow-up.
2
"What has been the most challenging part — and what helped you through it?"
Two-part question. The first part names difficulty — normalises that it exists. The second part identifies whether the trainee has support resources. "What helped you" reveals whether the G4, peers, or the job itself are providing adequate support.
LISTEN FOR
If the trainee cannot name what helped them — or cannot name anyone who helped them — this is a support gap. G4 coaching has not been present or effective. If challenge is disproportionate to the role, investigate workload or interpersonal issues.
3
"When you think about becoming a G4 driller — does that still feel like the right direction for you?"
The most important question in the check-in. Directly tests whether the intrinsic motivation that drove the trainee to join is still present at month 3. The phrase "still feel like" explicitly acknowledges that it might have changed — making it safe to say it has.
LISTEN FOR
Immediate "yes" with detail = engaged. Hesitation, "I think so", "it's hard to say" = at-risk signal requiring follow-up. "I'm not sure anymore" = immediate escalation conversation. The G4 must not fill this silence — wait for the full answer.
4
"What would make the biggest difference to your development between now and month 6?"
Forwards the conversation from evaluation to development. Asks the trainee to be specific about what they need — which often reveals what the G4 has or has not been providing. A trainee who says "more feedback" or "to understand what I need to do to be G2-ready" is telling the G4 exactly what to change.
LISTEN FOR
Specific development requests (good sign — trainee is invested). If trainee says "nothing" or "I don't know" — probe gently: "If you could change one thing about how you're being developed, what would it be?" Blank answers indicate either disengagement or a very comfortable trainee — context determines which.
5
"Is there anything else you want me to know — about the work, about how you're doing, or anything you've been thinking about?"
The open close. Creates space for the trainee to raise anything the structured questions did not surface. Family pressures, health concerns, interpersonal issues with peers or the G4 themselves — these often emerge here if the trainee trusts the G4 enough to share them.
LISTEN FOR
Anything that sounds like it is being said carefully or partially. "Actually there is one thing…" often leads to the most important content of the conversation. Give the trainee time. Do not rush to close. This question is worth the silence.
Section 08 — Learning Design Quality Standards

Learning Design
Quality Standards

Every piece of learning that Group HR designs or approves at BaKuu Holding is evaluated against these quality standards before it is delivered. The standards exist to prevent the two most common L&D quality failures: designing for the wrong learner and delivering without measuring.

The G4 Coaching Quality Rubric
Used by Ba to assess G4 coaching quality at certification observation (end of coaching programme) and at annual performance review. Each dimension rated 1–5. Score ≥3 on all dimensions = certification standard. Score below 3 on any dimension = targeted follow-up coaching on that dimension.
Dimension1 — Not yet3 — Developing5 — Strong
Question quality Uses predominantly closed or leading questions. Tells more than asks. "Was that because of X?" rather than "What do you think caused that?" Mix of open and closed questions. Uses the four debrief questions with prompting. Occasionally slips into instruction mode. Predominantly open questions, all four debrief stages completed, questions build on the trainee's previous answer rather than following a script.
Silence management Fills every pause immediately. Cannot hold silence for more than 3 seconds. Answers own questions. Can hold silence for up to 10 seconds. Occasionally fills before the trainee is ready. Does not answer own questions. Comfortable with extended silence. Waits for the trainee's full answer. Uses brief acknowledging responses ("uh-huh", "tell me more") rather than answers.
Reflection activation Skips directly from event description to principle. Does not use Q2 (reflective observation). Trainee is told what to learn from the experience. Uses Q2 but does not probe. Accepts first answer. Does not follow up "what did you notice?" with "what else?" Uses Q2 consistently. Probes the first answer. Creates genuine reflection before moving to Q3. The trainee forms the insight, not the G4.
Commitment quality Closes with vague commitment ("think about it," "try to do better") or no commitment at all. Does not ask Q4. Asks Q4 but accepts general commitments. "I'll try harder" or "I'll be more careful" — not specific enough to review. Asks Q4 and challenges vague commitments to specificity. Commitment includes: what the trainee will do, when, and in what situation. G4 writes it down to open next session with.
At-risk awareness No evidence of signal monitoring. Cannot name current at-risk status of any trainee. Has not run a month-3 check-in. Can name 2–3 at-risk signals. Has run at least one check-in. Would escalate if signals were extreme — but does not monitor regularly. Monitors all seven signals actively. Updates at-risk assessment after each significant interaction. Has run check-in for every trainee at month 3. Escalates within 48 hours when threshold is met.
Learning Design Quality Checklist
Every learning intervention designed by Group HR must pass this checklist before delivery. If any item is "No," the design is incomplete.
✓ Learner profile defined. We know exactly who the learner is, what they already know, and what context they are learning in (shift pattern, language, literacy).
✓ Gap type confirmed. The TNA confirmed this is a knowledge or ability gap — not a process, motivation, or structural gap that training will not fix.
✓ Learning outcomes specific. Outcomes are written as observable behaviours: "G4 can run the four-question debrief without a prompt," not "G4 understands coaching."
✓ Kolb's cycle completed. The design includes concrete experience, reflective observation, conceptualisation, and experimentation. Does not begin with information and end with information.
✓ Field-ready. Deliverable without digital infrastructure. Sessions ≤45 minutes. Language appropriate. G4 can deliver without preparation beyond a reference card.
✓ Success measure defined. We know how we will measure whether the learning changed behaviour, and when we will measure it. Not "did they enjoy it?" — "did conversion rate improve?"
✓ Commitment built in. Every session ends with a specific, time-bound commitment from the learner. The next session opens by reviewing the commitment.
The BaKuu Holding Design Anti-Patterns
These are the most common L&D design failures at BaKuu Holding's scale and context. Recognise them before they are built into a programme.
The PowerPoint programme. A slide deck is not a programme. If the primary delivery vehicle is a presentation, the learner will remember approximately 10% of it by the following week. Design for doing, not for watching.
The generic framework applied to BaKuu Holding. Buying a coaching course from an external provider and delivering it to Myanmar G4s. The content will not reflect their context, their language, their scenarios. BaKuu Holding-specific design beats generic external content at this scale every time.
The one-day workshop. A single full-day session is incompatible with site shift patterns and produces poor retention. Spaced repetition (weekly 45-minute modules over 6 weeks) dramatically outperforms one-day delivery for skill development.
Training without transfer support. Delivering a programme and expecting behaviour to change without any follow-up. Transfer support — commitment review at the next session, SM reinforcement, peer accountability — doubles learning transfer rates. The G4 coaching programme's commitment loop is non-negotiable for this reason.
Measuring satisfaction, not behaviour. "Did they enjoy it?" is not a success measure. The G4 coaching programme's success measure is Myanmar conversion rate — not whether the G4s rated the programme 4/5. Enjoyment and behaviour change are not correlated. Measure what matters to the pipeline.
