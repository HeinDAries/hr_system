CoE Maturity
Model

The maturity model is how the CoE answers two questions: where is Valentis HR today, and what does a credible path to CHRO-level positioning look like? This tool provides the five-level model, the current self-assessment scores across 8 domains, the gaps, the roadmap, and the evidence-based case for the 2027 CHRO positioning.

Why the Maturity Model Matters
A one-person CoE without a maturity map is just doing tasks. A one-person CoE with a maturity map is building toward something.
The maturity model does three things. First, it gives the CoE a shared language with the COO and executive team about where HR is and where it is going. Second, it makes progress visible: a score that moves from 2.8 to 3.5 in 12 months is concrete evidence of CoE impact. Third, it creates the organisational narrative for the CHRO positioning — not as an ambition, but as the logical next step in a trajectory that is already underway and already demonstrably working.

Section 02 — Maturity Framework
Maturity
Framework

The five-level maturity model defines what each level looks like across 8 HR domains. The model is specific to a CoE-led, multi-country HR function at Valentis scale. It is not a generic HR maturity framework — it reflects the actual operating context.

How to Read the Maturity Model
Each level is a coherent state, not just a score. Moving from Level 2 to Level 3 is not about ticking boxes — it is about the HR function operating in a fundamentally different way across most domains.
The model uses 8 domains because HR maturity is not uniform. It is common for an HR function to be at Level 3 in framework quality but Level 2 in analytics, or Level 4 in governance but Level 2 in CoE influence. The domain-level scoring is what makes the model actionable: it shows precisely where to invest effort to move the overall maturity score, and it prevents the CoE from claiming a level it has not yet reached across the whole function.

Assessment Methodology
01
Self-Assessment by CoE
The CoE (Ba) completes the domain scoring twice per year using the level descriptor grid (Section 02). Each domain is scored to one decimal place. Scores are documented with evidence, not estimated.
02
Evidence Required
Every score requires supporting evidence: a policy, a framework, a dashboard, a HRBP check-in record, an experiment result. A score cannot be claimed without evidence on file in the KM system.
03
Conservative Scoring
When evidence supports two adjacent levels, the lower level is scored until the higher level is fully established. The model rewards demonstrated capability, not intended capability.
04
Annual External Calibration
Once per year, the CoE self-assessment is validated against benchmark data from comparable HR functions in the region. This prevents score inflation and grounds the assessment in external reality.
05
Progress Tracking
Scores are tracked over time in the KM system. Movement of 0.3 or more in any domain in a 6-month period is flagged for review: either as a genuine improvement to celebrate, or as a potential scoring inconsistency to investigate.

Root Causes — Why the Gaps Exist
CoE is still in build phase
Affects: Strategy alignment, CoE Influence, Analytics
The CoE has been operational for 24 months. Many of the gaps reflect the reality that a one-person CoE cannot yet do everything a mature CHRO function would do. The mitigation is sequencing: build depth in the highest-impact domains first, rather than spreading effort across all 8 simultaneously.
Indonesia and Myanmar HR governance gaps
Affects: Governance, HRBP Capability, Analytics
Two of the four countries have significant governance and capability gaps. Indonesia has active shadow HR patterns and a HRBP baseline score of 61. Myanmar has 28% attrition and SM capability gaps. Both are being addressed through active experiments (EXP-14, EXP-15) but will take 6–12 months to resolve fully.
Analytics capability is descriptive not predictive
Affects: Analytics, Strategy Alignment
The current People Analytics toolkit (Department 7) provides excellent operational dashboards but does not yet produce predictive models or scenario planning capability. Advancing to predictive analytics requires clean historical data (accumulating now), methodology development, and SM capability to interpret outputs. Target: Q3 2026 for first predictive model (Myanmar attrition).
CoE influence is not yet formalised at executive level
Affects: CoE Influence, Strategy Alignment
The CoE has operational authority (governance, frameworks) but not yet strategic authority (board-level people reporting, business planning participation). This is the single most important gap for the CHRO positioning. It is a structural and political gap, not a capability gap. The mitigation is the CHRO positioning proposal (Section 07).

Section 07 — CHRO Positioning
CHRO
Positioning

The CHRO positioning is not an ambition. It is the logical endpoint of a trajectory that is already underway and already demonstrably working. This section makes the evidence-based case: the track record, the business case, the strategic argument, and the timeline.

The Positioning Argument in One Paragraph
Valentis needs a CHRO by 2027 because the business is scaling faster than a one-person CoE can manage without executive authority.
Ba has built the HR function from zero to Level 3 in 24 months: 24 frameworks, 6 adopted experiments, 4-country governance, a People Analytics toolkit, and a CoE operating model that works. That is a CHRO-level achievement delivered at a CoE-lead cost. The question is not whether the function is capable of operating at CHRO level — it already is. The question is whether the organisation is ready to recognise it, resource it appropriately, and give it the executive mandate it needs to take Valentis through the next phase of growth.

Section 08 — Review Cadence
Review
Cadence

How the maturity model is used operationally: the assessment frequency, the scoring rules, the evidence requirements, and the projected scores for the next semi-annual assessment (Q3 2026).

The Maturity Model as a Live Management Tool
A maturity model that is reviewed once and filed is just a document. A maturity model that is reviewed twice a year, updated when things change, and used to brief the COO is a management tool.
The CoE uses the maturity model to make three kinds of decisions: what to work on next (close the highest-gap domains), how to sequence experiments (prioritise innovations that advance maturity scores), and how to make the CHRO positioning case (the trajectory of score improvement is the evidence). The review cadence is what keeps the model live and the decisions grounded.

Scoring Rules
01
Evidence Before Score
Every score requires documented evidence filed in the KM system before it is recorded. A score cannot be claimed on intent or plan. It must reflect demonstrated, current capability.
02
Conservative When in Doubt
When evidence supports two adjacent levels, the lower level is recorded until the higher level is fully and consistently demonstrated. The model is designed to be earned, not claimed.
03
Decimal Precision
Scores are recorded to one decimal place. A domain at 3.2 is meaningfully different from a domain at 3.8. Decimal precision makes progress visible and prevents the false comfort of rounded numbers.
04
Score Ceiling Without Conditions
No domain can score 4.0 until the CHRO positioning is confirmed. The Level 4 descriptors require executive-level authority and influence that the CoE does not yet have. This prevents premature claims.
05
The Model is a Tool, Not a Report
The maturity model exists to drive decisions, not to produce a document for review. If the model is not influencing what the CoE works on next, it is not being used correctly.
