Section 02 — Strategic Forces & Drivers

Strategic Forces
& Drivers

Scenarios are not arbitrary alternatives — they are structural responses to real strategic forces. These are the forces acting on BaKuu Holding's organisation design between now and 2028. Each scenario reflects a different weight given to these forces.

Growth Forces — pushing structure to change
Country 5 expansion — Q2 2027
Adding a fifth country adds a fifth Site Manager reporting to the COO — taking effective span from 6 to 8 without structural relief. The Group Ops Lead role must precede Country 5 entry, not follow it.
42 → 65 FTE growth (35%)
Growth of 23 FTE in 6 quarters requires a functioning pipeline and onboarding capacity that the current structure does not have. HR Coordinator and G4 development accountability are not optional at 65 FTE.
Client and programme demand increasing
Revenue growth depends on programme delivery capacity — which depends on having enough trained G3/G4 drillers in place. The pipeline constraint is a growth constraint, not just a people risk.
Risk Forces — creating structural urgency
Dual SM retention risk — 2027 cliff
Myo H. (Myanmar) and Wirawan S. (Indonesia) both have retention bonuses expiring in Q1 2027. If either exits without a successor in development, BaKuu Holding faces a 6–12 month capability gap in that country — precisely when Country 5 activation demands COO attention elsewhere.
Laos G4 retirement wave — 2027
3 of 5 Laos G4 senior drillers at retirement risk within 3 years. Net -1 G4 by 2027 without pipeline intervention. The 24-month development cycle means activation window closes June 2026.
People cost at 43% revenue — scale sensitivity
Adding 23 FTE must be absorbed by proportionally greater revenue growth. If revenue does not scale with headcount, people cost as a percentage rises. Each scenario must be assessed against its people cost implications.

Section 03 — Three Scenarios

Three
Scenarios

Three structural futures for BaKuu Holding between now and 2028. Each represents a coherent set of design choices — not a best/worst/middle split. The question is not which scenario is "best in theory" but which is most appropriate given BaKuu Holding's strategic ambition, risk tolerance, and execution capacity.

A
Steady State
Optimise the current structure — no Country 5
BaKuu Holding holds at four countries and focuses on fixing structural gaps in the current model: G4 accountability, Laos pipeline, coordination infrastructure, and COO span relief via the Group Ops Lead. No Country 5 entry in the planning horizon. HR Coordinator hired at 55+ FTE. Growth is organic and contained within the four existing countries.
Lowest execution risk 42 → 52 FTE by Q2 2027 4 countries Misses growth ambition
B
Recommended
Managed growth — Country 5 entry with structural preparation
BaKuu Holding enters Country 5 in Q2 2027 — but only after completing the structural preparation sequence: Group Ops Lead in post (Q3 2026), SM peer forum running (Q2 2026), G5 succession identified (Q3 2026), HR Coordinator hired (Q4 2026). Country 5 entry is enabled by the new structure, not squeezed into the old one. Target: 65 FTE across 5 countries by end 2027.
HRBP recommended 42 → 65 FTE by Q4 2027 5 countries Sequenced and executable
C
Accelerated
Accelerated growth — Country 5 plus potential Country 6 preparation
BaKuu Holding moves faster and bigger: Country 5 entry Q4 2026 (compressed timeline), Country 6 preparation begins Q2 2027. Requires the Group Ops Lead to be recruited externally by Q2 2026 (not Q3), HR Coordinator by Q3 2026, and G5 succession to be running in parallel — not sequentially. High execution risk. Appropriate only if client demand or competitive pressure requires faster entry.

How to Use These Scenarios
Scenarios are decision tools — not predictions. They are used in three ways at BaKuu Holding. First, as a framework for the COO conversation: "here are three structurally coherent futures — which one matches our strategic intent?" Second, as a planning input: each scenario defines the sequence of structural changes needed, and therefore the HR workplan. Third, as a trigger document: if the business context changes (Country 5 confirmed earlier than expected, or a G5 exit forces the succession question), the scenario framework tells you which path you have moved to and what that requires next. The scenarios are reviewed quarterly — they are not set once and filed.
Section 04 — Scenario A

Scenario A
Steady State

BaKuu Holding holds at four countries and invests in fixing structural quality within the current footprint. No Country 5 entry in the 2026–2027 planning horizon. Growth is organic. The focus is on making the existing structure work excellently rather than expanding before it is ready.

When is Scenario A the right choice?If Country 5 is not commercially confirmed, if the COO has low risk appetite for execution complexity, or if the 2026 intervention backlog (Indonesia vacancies, Laos succession, Myanmar attrition) requires full attention before expansion is responsible. Scenario A is not a failure — it is a deliberate choice to build structural quality before structural scale.

Scenario A — Assessment
Strengths

Risks

Section 05 — Scenario B (Recommended)

Scenario B
Managed Growth

BaKuu Holding enters Country 5 in Q2 2027 — but only after completing the structural preparation sequence that makes the entry sustainable. The new structure enables Country 5; it does not struggle to accommodate it. This is the HRBP's recommended scenario.

Why Scenario B is recommendedScenario B matches BaKuu Holding's stated growth ambition (65 FTE, 5 countries by Q2 2027) while building the structural prerequisites that make that growth executable. The sequencing is the key: every structural change in the 2026 workplan creates a foundation that Country 5 entry depends on. Scenario B is achievable — but only if the 2026 structural changes are completed on schedule.

Section 06 — Scenario C

Scenario C
Accelerated Growth

BaKuu Holding compresses the Country 5 timeline to Q4 2026 and begins Country 6 preparation in parallel. All structural changes that are sequenced in Scenario B must now happen simultaneously — not sequentially. High execution risk. Appropriate only under specific commercial conditions.

When is Scenario C the right choice?Only if a specific commercial window requires Country 5 entry before Q2 2027, and the COO has explicitly accepted the execution risk of compressing the structural preparation timeline. Scenario C is not "more ambitious Scenario B" — it is a fundamentally different risk posture that requires different resourcing from day one.

Section 08 — Recommendation & Triggers

Recommendation
& Triggers

The HRBP recommendation, the rationale, and the trigger conditions that would cause a scenario switch. Scenarios are not permanent — they are reviewed quarterly and updated when context changes. This section is the live decision document.

HRBP Recommendation
Proceed with Scenario B — Managed Growth
Scenario B is the only scenario that simultaneously honours BaKuu Holding's growth ambition and respects the structural constraints that make growth sustainable. It enters Country 5 — which Scenario A does not — but it does so after completing the structural preparation that makes Country 5 entry manageable rather than chaotic.
Why not Scenario A
Scenario A solves the structural problems but does not capture the growth opportunity. If Country 5 has commercial viability, deferring it indefinitely while the structure is fixed does not serve the business case for the structural investment.
Why Scenario B
Achievable within the current resource envelope. The sequencing is executable. Every 2026 structural change builds the foundation the next change needs. Country 5 entry is enabled by the new structure — not added to the old one.
Why not Scenario C
The execution risk is disproportionate to the acceleration benefit unless a hard commercial window forces it. Compressing the structural preparation timeline for Country 5 increases the probability that the problems we are trying to fix (accountability gaps, retention risk, coordination failures) are simply imported into a fifth country.

The COO Briefing — How to Present This
The scenario framework is a strategic planning tool — but it must be presented to the COO in business language, not org design language. This is the recommended briefing structure.
Open with the business question
"I want to walk you through three ways the structure could evolve between now and 2028 — and get your view on which path matches our growth intent. The choice affects what we do in the next 6 months."
Present the scenarios as choices, not recommendations first
"Scenario A keeps us at four countries and focuses on structural quality — lowest risk, slower revenue growth. Scenario B enters Country 5 in mid-2027 with a sequenced structural build — this is what I recommend. Scenario C enters Country 5 faster but requires parallel execution of every structural change — appropriate only if there's a hard commercial window."
Make the ask explicit
"I'd like to confirm: are we planning for Country 5, and if so, is Q2 2027 the right target? That answer determines whether I build the 2026 HR workplan around Scenario B or Scenario A. And it determines when the Group Ops Lead hire needs to start."
Close with the trigger framework
"Whatever we decide today isn't permanent — I'll bring a quarterly update on whether we're on track for the chosen scenario or whether a trigger event means we should switch paths. The most important thing is that we're making this decision deliberately, not by default."
HRBP Note — What This Tool Earns You
Scenario planning is how the HRBP earns a seat at the business planning table.
Walking into a COO conversation with three structurally coherent futures — each with a timeline, a cost, a risk profile, and a trigger mechanism — is the difference between an HRBP who reacts to the organisation and one who shapes it. The scenario framework does not tell the COO what to decide. It tells the COO what each decision costs, what it requires, and what will force a review. That is the HRBP's contribution to business planning. This is the tool that closes the gap between "Group HR Manager" and "strategic HRBP."
