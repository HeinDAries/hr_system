HRBP Tool 4 — Organisational Diagnostic Tool

Organisational
Diagnostic

The ability to walk into a business unit, assess its organisational health, identify root causes of underperformance, and present a credible recommendation — is one of the most powerful things an HRBP can do. This tool makes that assessment systematic.

The Core PrincipleMost organisational problems are visible long before they become crises — if you know what to look for. The HRBP's diagnostic role is not to wait for the COO to flag a problem. It is to detect the signal before it becomes noise, and act on it while the intervention is still manageable.
What an Org Diagnostic Actually Is
An organisational diagnostic is a structured assessment of a business unit's people health — examining six dimensions that determine whether a team can perform at the level the business requires.

It is not a survey. It is not a performance review. It is not an audit. It is a diagnostic — the same way a doctor assesses a patient's health before prescribing treatment. You gather data from multiple sources, identify patterns, form a hypothesis about the root cause, and test it before recommending an intervention.

Done well, an organisational diagnostic is one of the most credibility-building things an HRBP can deliver. Done poorly — with conclusions formed before the data is gathered — it destroys trust and confirms every sceptic's view of HR.
When to Run a Diagnostic
A diagnostic is warranted when one or more of these conditions are present:
Performance signal: KPIs dropping — programme delivery, metres drilled, rig utilisation below target for two or more consecutive periods
Attrition signal: Turnover exceeds 15% annualised in a single business unit or country
Safety signal: LTIFR rising or near-miss frequency increasing in a specific site
Leadership signal: Manager departure, promotion, or change — assess team health within 30 days
Proactive: Quarterly health check on all business units — not just ones showing problems
Pre-change: Before any restructure, country expansion, or operating model change
The Diagnostic Mindset — Five Rules
01
Hypothesis First
Form a working hypothesis from early signals — but hold it loosely. Let the data confirm or disconfirm it.
02
Multiple Sources
Never rely on a single data source. Triangulate: quantitative data, manager perspective, team observation, exit data.
03
Root Cause Only
High attrition is a symptom, not a diagnosis. What is causing it? Keep asking why until you reach the root cause.
04
No Pre-Judging
If you walk into a diagnostic certain of the answer, you will find evidence to confirm it and miss the real issue.
05
Always Recommend
A diagnostic without a recommendation is analysis for its own sake. Every finding must connect to an action.

Section 03 — Early Warning Signals

Early Warning
Signals

The observable indicators that something is wrong in an organisation before the data confirms it. An experienced HRBP reads these signals in meetings, site visits, conversations, and data trends — and acts before the problem becomes a crisis.

The Signal PrincipleEvery organisational problem broadcasts early warning signals for weeks or months before it becomes visible in KPI data. The HRBP's advantage is seeing these signals earlier than anyone else — because you are watching for them, and you know what they mean.
Signal Type
Observable Behaviour or Data
Most Likely Diagnosis
High-performer exits cluster
Two or more strong performers resign from the same team within one quarter. Especially significant if they cite similar reasons or go to competitors.
D2 Leadership Quality — high performers leave managers, not companies. Almost always a direct supervisor issue before anything else.
Team meetings go silent
In meetings, team members stop offering opinions, challenging ideas, or raising concerns. Responses become short and non-committal.
D4 Culture / Climate — psychological safety has collapsed. The team has learned that speaking up has a cost.
Manager escalates everything
A site manager begins escalating decisions they would previously have made independently. Smaller and smaller decisions are being referred upward.
D1 Direction and/or D2 Leadership — either the manager lacks confidence, or the direction from above has become unclear or contradictory.
Leave spikes before programme end
Unplanned sick leave or compassionate leave requests concentrate in the final weeks of a drilling programme — especially from experienced drillers.
D5 Motivation / D4 Climate — programme fatigue, relationship breakdown with supervisor, or disengagement from the organisation. Cross-check with R&R data.
Near-miss frequency rises
Safety near-misses increase in frequency even without a recorded LTI. Teams begin normalising risk or skipping safety steps under time pressure.
D4 Culture and D3 Capability — production pressure overriding safety norms, combined with insufficient supervisor capability to hold the line.
Internal transfer requests rise
Employees begin requesting transfers to other countries or programmes without an obvious career logic behind the request.
D2 Leadership or D4 Culture — people are moving away from something, not toward something. Investigate the specific environment they are leaving.
Trainee dropout spikes at same point
Trainees consistently leave at the same phase of the programme — e.g., after 3 months, or after the first site rotation.
D3 Capability / D4 Culture — a specific experience in the programme is creating an attrition pinch point. Investigate that phase specifically, not the programme as a whole.
Information stops flowing upward
Senior leaders begin receiving surprises — problems they were not pre-warned about, decisions made without consultation, issues that were apparently common knowledge at the team level.
D1 Direction / D4 Culture — either the upward communication channels are broken, or people do not feel safe reporting bad news. A significant governance and culture risk.
Section 04 — Diagnostic Protocol

Diagnostic
Protocol

The step-by-step process for conducting a rigorous organisational diagnostic. From pre-brief to final recommendation — this is how a credible HRBP approaches every structured assessment.

01
Commission and Scope
Define clearly: which business unit, which time period, which trigger, and what question you are trying to answer. Get explicit agreement from the COO or site manager on the scope. An unsanctioned diagnostic will be perceived as HR snooping — and the findings will be dismissed regardless of their quality.
02
Gather Quantitative Data First
Before speaking to anyone, pull all available data: turnover rates, absenteeism, performance ratings, safety metrics, programme delivery data, compa-ratios, time-to-fill. Build your baseline picture from the numbers. This prevents the qualitative data (which comes from people) from being distorted by early impressions.
03
Form a Working Hypothesis
Based on the quantitative data, form a working hypothesis about the most likely root cause. Hold this loosely. It is a starting point for structured inquiry — not a conclusion. Write it down. It forces rigour in your thinking and helps you design the right qualitative questions.
04
Conduct Qualitative Conversations
Interview the site manager, two to three team members, and if possible one person who has recently left the team. Use open questions. Listen for what is not being said as much as what is. Look for: where does energy change? Where does language become careful or vague? What gets consistent agreement across conversations?
05
Observe if Possible
For site-based diagnostics, a site visit is worth more than any number of remote interviews. Observe a team briefing. Eat with the team. Watch how the manager interacts with people. The dynamic in a room tells you more in thirty minutes than three hours of structured interviews. Not always possible across four countries — but prioritise when the stakes are high.
06
Triangulate and Score
Use the 6-D Framework (Section 02) and the diagnostic tool (Section 05) to score each dimension based on the full data picture. Where multiple sources point to the same weakness, the signal is strong. Where only one source flags a problem, treat it as a hypothesis to investigate further — not a finding to report.
07
Identify Root Cause
Use the Five Whys technique on the weakest dimension. Keep asking why until you reach a root cause that is actionable. "Low engagement" is not a root cause. "Low engagement because the team supervisor provides no performance feedback and new drillers do not know whether they are on track" is a root cause.
08
Present Findings and Recommend
Present to the COO: three key findings, the root cause, and two to three specific interventions with estimated impact and timeline. Do not present the full diagnostic process — present the conclusions it produced. One page plus a conversation. Invite challenge. Adjust if the COO has context you were missing.

Section 08 — BaKuu Holding Live Cases

BaKuu Holding
Live Cases

Three real diagnostic scenarios drawn from BaKuu Holding Group's current organisational context. Each demonstrates how the 6-D framework applies to an actual business problem — and what the HRBP should do about it.

Case 01 — Myanmar Site
Trainee Attrition & Site Culture
Trigger: Trainee conversion rate dropped to 58% — below the 70% target. Three trainees resigned in Q1, all citing "environment" as primary reason.
Critical Risk
Quantitative Signals
Attrition at month 3 (end of first site rotation) — all three exits at the same programme phase. Exit interviews: "supervisors don't communicate expectations," "felt isolated." LTIFR stable but near-miss frequency up 18%.
6-D Assessment
D4 Culture/Climate: LOW — trainees feel unsafe raising concerns. D2 Leadership: MEDIUM-LOW — supervisors technically competent but weak on communication and mentoring. D3 Capability: MEDIUM — trainee skills adequate, support insufficient.
HRBP Recommendation
1. Supervisor communication coaching — targeted at the three supervisors managing trainee rotations. 2. Structured trainee check-in at 30/60/90 days with HRBP. 3. Revise trainee induction to set explicit expectations for the site environment. Timeline: 6 weeks. Success measure: zero exits at month 3 in next cohort.
Case 02 — Indonesia Site
Programme Delivery Slippage
Trigger: Indonesia programme delivery rate fell from 93% to 84% over two quarters. COO flagged it as an operational concern — HRBP requested permission to conduct a people diagnostic.
Moderate Risk
Quantitative Signals
Metres drilled per day down from 21 to 17.5. Three G3 driller vacancies open for 40+ days. Overtime hours up 22%. No new hires in Indonesia for six months. Site manager has not escalated vacancies through formal channels.
6-D Assessment
D6 Talent/Succession: LOW — three vacancies, no internal pipeline. D5 Motivation: MEDIUM-LOW — remaining team overloaded. D1 Direction: MEDIUM — site manager managing around the problem rather than escalating it through HR channels.
HRBP Recommendation
1. Activate emergency driller sourcing for Indonesia — TA COE, regional market. 2. Interim deployment: identify any cross-country G3 driller who can mobilise within 21 days. 3. Stay interview with remaining Indonesia team — assess flight risk before someone else leaves. 4. Address non-escalation with site manager — establish early warning process. Timeline: 30 days for first hire.
Case 03 — Laos Site
Succession Gap — Senior Driller Cohort
Trigger: Proactive quarterly health check revealed that four of five G4 senior drillers in Laos have 18+ years experience and no G3 employees are being developed toward G4 competency.
Strategic Risk
Quantitative Signals
Average age of G4 driller cohort in Laos: 51. Retirement risk within 3 years: 3 of 5. G3 driller count in Laos: 2. Average G3-to-G4 development time: 24 months. Gap: 3 retirements — 24-month development cycle — 2 G3 candidates. Exposure: net -1 senior driller by 2027.
6-D Assessment
D6 Talent/Succession: CRITICAL — no succession cover for a critical technical cohort. D3 Capability: MEDIUM — G3 pool exists but development not structured. D1 Direction: MEDIUM — Laos manager not yet flagging this as a 3-year planning risk.
HRBP Recommendation
1. Activate G3-to-G4 development plan for both current G3 Laos drillers immediately. 2. Hire one additional G3 driller in Laos within Q2 2026 to build pipeline redundancy. 3. Brief COO: Laos senior driller succession is a 2027 programme continuity risk — present the numbers. 4. Consider structured knowledge transfer programme while senior drillers are still in role. Timeline: G3 dev plans within 30 days.
