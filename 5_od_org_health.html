OD Tool 5 / 9 — Org Health Diagnostic

Org Health
Diagnostic

Structure defines how the organisation is designed to work. Health determines whether it actually does. An organisation can have the right structure and still be unhealthy — and a structurally imperfect organisation can be highly effective if its health is strong. The HRBP diagnoses both.

"Organisational health is the ability of an organisation to align, execute, and renew itself faster than the competition. It is the ultimate competitive advantage."

McKinsey Organizational Health Index
Structure vs Health — The Distinction
Structure and health are related but different. Understanding the difference is the first diagnostic skill.
Structure
— How work is organised and divided
— Reporting lines and authority
— Spans, layers, role definitions
— Visible on the org chart
— Changed through redesign
— Tools 2, 3, 4 in this OD toolkit
Health
— How well the organisation functions
— Quality of decisions, communication, trust
— Capability, alignment, accountability
— Invisible on the org chart
— Changed through culture and leadership
— This tool: Tool 5
Why Health Matters More Than Structure
McKinsey research across 1,500+ organisations found that companies in the top quartile of organisational health deliver three times the total shareholder return of bottom-quartile companies — and the effect is persistent over time.
Structure without health fails. A perfectly designed org chart with unclear accountability, low trust between the COO and SMs, and poor information flow will underperform a messier structure with strong alignment and capable execution.
Health issues masquerade as performance issues. When programme delivery drops, the first instinct is to look at capability or effort. Often the root cause is health — unclear direction, accountability gaps, or coordination failure between layers.
At BaKuu Holding: The Indonesia programme delivery drop, Myanmar trainee attrition, and Laos succession risk all have structural components — and health components. Fixing the structure without diagnosing the health misses half the problem.

Section 02 — The 7-Dimension Framework

7-Dimension
Framework

BaKuu Holding org health is assessed across seven dimensions — adapted from McKinsey OHI and Gallup Q12 research but calibrated for a small, multi-country, operations-led organisation. Each dimension has a definition, observable indicators, and a diagnostic question.

D1
Direction & Strategic Clarity
Does every layer of the organisation understand where BaKuu Holding is going and why their role matters in getting there? From CEO to G1 trainee — clarity of direction is the foundation of aligned effort.
Observable indicators
Can a G3 driller in Laos explain what BaKuu Holding is trying to achieve in the next 12 months? Do SMs make decisions that reflect group strategy, or only local priorities? Are growth and succession plans known at site level?
Diagnostic question: "If I asked five people at random across the org — including a G2 driller — to describe the company's direction, would they give consistent answers?"
D2
Accountability & Ownership
Do people own their outcomes — or do they own their effort? Accountability is healthy when individuals can clearly state what they are responsible for delivering, and when gaps are addressed, not explained away.
Observable indicators
When a programme section falls behind, does someone own the recovery — or does it diffuse across multiple roles? Are performance gaps addressed promptly or quietly tolerated? Do people raise risks proactively or wait to be asked?
"When something goes wrong at BaKuu Holding, is there a clear person who owns the recovery — or does it become a group conversation with no single driver?"
D3
Leadership Quality
Does every level of leadership — COO, Site Manager, Senior Driller — have the capability and the motivation to develop their people, set clear direction, and make good decisions consistently? Leadership quality cascades — and so does leadership failure.
Observable indicators
Do team members grow under their leaders — or stagnate? Are leaders making the right decisions for the organisation, or for their own country/team? Do leaders raise difficult conversations, or avoid them?
"If I observed each Site Manager for one week, would I see coaching conversations, or only task management?"
D4
Coordination & Integration
Do the different parts of the organisation work together effectively — or do they optimise individually at the expense of the group? Coordination is healthy when knowledge, people, and practices flow across boundaries without friction.
Observable indicators
Do the four SMs share practices across countries — or does each country reinvent its own approach? Is the Group HR framework applied consistently — or differently in each country based on SM preference? Do group functions feel like enablers or constraints to site teams?
"When Thailand solves a safety problem effectively, how long before Laos knows about it — and applies it?"
D5
Talent Pipeline & Capability
Does the organisation have the people it needs — now and in 2–3 years? Talent pipeline health is about both supply (are we developing enough people) and capability (are the right skills present at every level).
Observable indicators
Is there a ready G4 candidate for every G5 vacancy that might arise in the next 18 months? Is the G1→G4 development cycle producing enough completions to meet operational demand? Are capability gaps addressed through development — or only through hiring?
"If Myo H. resigned tomorrow, could BaKuu Holding continue to operate Myanmar at the same quality level within 90 days?"
D6
Culture & Psychological Safety
Do people feel safe enough to raise problems, admit mistakes, and challenge decisions? Psychological safety is the most consistent predictor of team effectiveness — and the most consistently underestimated health variable in site-based operations.
Observable indicators
Do trainees raise safety concerns to their G4 supervisor without fear? Are near-misses reported openly, or only when they become incidents? Do SMs tell the COO when things are going wrong — or do they filter the picture?
"When a G2 driller sees a safety risk on site, what is their first instinct — raise it, or stay quiet? And what does that tell us about culture?"
D7
Renewal & Adaptability
Can the organisation learn and change? Renewal health is about whether BaKuu Holding improves from experience — adopts new methods, responds to market shifts, incorporates what it learns from incidents and near-misses — rather than repeating the same approaches year after year.
Observable indicators
Has the drilling methodology changed in the last 3 years? Does BaKuu Holding learn from country-to-country variation — or treat each country as an isolated operation? When a project fails to deliver, is there a structured after-action review?
"Can I name three things BaKuu Holding has changed in the last 18 months as a direct result of learning from experience — not just from external pressure?"

D2 Accountability — Full Diagnostic 4/10 Red
Accountability at BaKuu Holding has three distinct failure patterns. Each has a different cause and a different fix. Treating them as a single "accountability problem" will produce a generic intervention that addresses none of them.
Failure patternRoot causeBaKuu Holding evidenceTargeted fix
Ghost accountability — no owner Role design gap. The accountability was never assigned to a specific role. Everyone assumes someone else owns it. Trainee development at month 3. Programme delivery recovery in Indonesia. Cross-country standards compliance. All involve a gap with no single owner. Role profile update (Tool 3). Explicitly assign the accountability to a named role with a measurable outcome. Do this before any performance conversation.
Diffuse accountability — multiple owners Authority boundary not defined. Two roles both believe they own the same outcome — and neither acts decisively because they assume the other will. HR compliance: Indonesia SM and Group HR each defer to the other. COO and SM on programme recovery: no single escalation decision. RACI clarification (Tool 9 — Decision Rights). One Accountable. One Responsible. Clear in both role profiles. Communicated by COO.
Consequence gap — accountability without teeth Performance management system does not distinguish between activity and outcome. People can "look accountable" without delivering results. G4 drillers who fail to develop trainees face no consequences under the current PM framework — because trainee development was never a defined accountability. PM framework update (PM Tools 1–2). Include trainee conversion rate as a G4 performance metric. Link to merit decisions.
D4 Coordination — Full Diagnostic 3/10 Red
Coordination is the lowest-scoring health dimension at BaKuu Holding. At 42 FTE across 4 countries with no formal cross-country mechanism, this is structurally expected — but it is becoming a competitive risk as the organisation scales.
What low coordination costs BaKuu Holding today
Thailand best practices not transferred. The Thailand SM is a confirmed high performer and change champion. Their safety practices, training approaches, and programme management techniques are not being systematically shared with Laos, Myanmar, or Indonesia. This is an opportunity cost — each site reinvents what Thailand has already solved.
Group HR framework applied inconsistently. Each SM applies group HR standards to the degree they choose. Indonesia's resistance means that performance management, development conversations, and compliance reporting vary significantly by country — creating legal risk and making group-level data unreliable.
No group visibility on talent. The COO does not have a reliable cross-country picture of who the G3→G4 pipeline looks like, which G4 drillers are succession-ready for SM, or where the critical skill gaps are by country. Each SM holds their own picture. Group HR holds approximations.
What improved coordination enables
SM peer forum (Q2 2026). A quarterly structured forum where all SMs share performance data, pipeline status, and one practice that worked. Chaired initially by COO; transferred to Group Ops Lead when created. Cost: 1 day per quarter per SM. Value: best practice transfer, group talent visibility, SM peer accountability.
Group Ops Lead coordination function. The primary purpose of the Group Ops Lead (Tool 3) is coordination — not management. A role with explicit accountability for cross-country standards transfer and the SM peer forum directly addresses the coordination health gap.
Group talent database. A single source of truth for pipeline status across all countries. Managed by Group HR. Updated quarterly. Enables the COO to see the group talent picture — not four separate country pictures.
D5 Talent Pipeline — Full Diagnostic 4/10 Red
The talent pipeline is the most quantifiable health risk at BaKuu Holding. The numbers are stark — and the lead time required to fix them means action must start now for outcomes to materialise by the time they are needed.
G5 succession gap — existential risk level
Zero internal succession candidates currently assessed as G5-ready. If Myo H. (Myanmar) or Wirawan S. (Indonesia) exit, BaKuu Holding must either recruit externally (6–12 month lead time, high cost, cultural integration risk) or promote an unready G4 (operational risk during transition).
Deadlines: Myo H. retention conversation by Sep 2026. Wirawan S. by Oct 2026. If either leaves without a succession candidate in development, the operational impact extends 12–18 months into 2027 — coinciding with Country 5 activation and the 65 FTE target.
Laos G4 retirement wave — quantified gap
5 senior drillers in Laos. Average age 51. 3 of 5 at retirement risk within 3 years. Only 2 G3 candidates currently in the pipeline. Net position by 2027: -1 senior driller in Laos without intervention. The G3→G4 development cycle takes 18–24 months — activation deadline June 2026.
If development plans activate June 2026 and take 24 months: G4-ready by June 2028. Retirement window opens 2027. Gap period: approximately 12 months with reduced G4 capacity in Laos. Mitigation: cross-country mobility (a healthy coordination score would enable this — another reason D4 and D5 are linked).

All three causes are addressable — with role design, rewards design, and PM framework updates already in progress across the toolkit.

Section 05 — Health Risks & Early Warnings

Health Risks
& Early Warnings

The HRBP's early warning system. These are the leading indicators that a health dimension is deteriorating — detectable before the lagging indicator (programme delivery drop, resignation, grievance) appears. Catching health problems early is the highest-leverage HR activity.

Leading vs Lagging IndicatorsA programme delivery drop is a lagging indicator — it tells you health failed weeks or months ago. An early warning is a leading indicator — it tells you health is weakening now, while there is still time to intervene. The HRBP monitors both, but acts on the leading indicators.
D2 Accountability — Early Warnings Red dimension
Leading indicator
Decisions escalate to COO that could have been made at SM level. Frequency of COO intervention in operational matters increases month-on-month.
Leading indicator
Blame language increases in reporting — "the other team didn't…", "we were waiting for…", "it wasn't our responsibility to…". Any escalation that includes finger-pointing at another role.
Lagging indicator
Programme delivery drops without a clear single owner of the recovery plan. Month-3 trainee exits continue despite awareness of the problem (no owner = no intervention).
D4 Coordination — Early Warnings Red dimension
Leading indicator
SMs solve the same problem independently that another site already solved. Duplicate effort — different solutions to identical problems appearing across countries.
Leading indicator
Group HR reports data quality differences by country that cannot be explained by legitimate country-specific factors. Variation in how PM, payroll, or compliance data is reported.
Lagging indicator
Group HR framework applied so differently by country that group-level reporting is unreliable. A new country (Country 5) receives no structured onboarding — reinvents its approach from scratch.
D5 Talent Pipeline — Early Warnings Red dimension
Leading indicator
G3→G4 promotion rate drops below 1 per country per 24-month period. The pipeline is running slower than the replacement demand. Visible in quarterly talent reviews if the process exists.
Leading indicator
G4 drillers begin exploring external options — LinkedIn profile updates, increased awareness of competitor pay. A single unsolicited approach from a competitor to a G4 driller is an early warning for the whole cohort.
Lagging indicator
A G5 SM exits without a ready successor — BaKuu Holding must recruit externally, taking 6–12 months. Programme delivery in that country drops during transition. Revenue risk is directly measurable.
D6 Culture / Psychological Safety — Early Warnings Amber dimension
Leading indicator
Near-miss reporting rate drops below historical baseline. In safety-critical environments, fewer near-miss reports often means people are less willing to report — not that fewer near-misses are occurring.
Leading indicator
Exit interview language shifts. If departing employees begin citing "not feeling heard" or "no point raising problems" as departure reasons — psychological safety is deteriorating, not just individual dissatisfaction.
Lagging indicator
A preventable safety incident occurs in which it emerges that team members were aware of the risk but did not raise it. The cultural cost of silence becomes visible only after the incident.

Presenting Health Data to the COO
The COO is an operational leader. Health data must be translated into operational and commercial language to be acted on — not presented as an HR scorecard.
Don't say: "D5 Talent Pipeline is scoring 4/10 — we have a health gap."
Do say: "We have zero succession cover for any SM role. If Myo H. exits, we face a 6–12 month external recruitment process during the period when we're trying to enter Country 5. The cost of that gap is estimated at [revenue impact]. Here's what we need to do before September."
Don't say: "D4 Coordination is our weakest dimension."
Do say: "Thailand solved the month-3 safety briefing problem 6 months ago. Laos had the same incident last week. We have no mechanism to transfer what works between sites. The Group Ops Lead role fixes this — and here's what it costs vs what it saves."
